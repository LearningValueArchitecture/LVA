About

What is the LVA?

The Learning Value Architecture (LVA) is a diagnostic framework for understanding where learning actually is — not where we hope it is. It was developed to address a persistent problem in education and training: most measurement stops at satisfaction and completion, missing the deeper questions of whether real capability was built, whether it’s durable, and whether the learner can leverage it.

The LVA organizes learning outcomes into four mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive (MECE) dimensions, each with progressive levels that describe a developmental trajectory from absence to mastery. A learner’s position is described as a four-dimensional profile (e.g., E4/C3/P2/O4) — capturing not just what they learned, but how they relate to it, whether the world can see it, and whether their environment supports it.

The framework is grounded in 20 learning science theories spanning motivation psychology, cognitive science, knowledge management, organizational learning, and behavior change. Each theory was mapped to the four dimensions through expert judgment, with alignment percentages reflecting relative emphasis rather than empirical correlation.

The Four Dimensions

Engagement

Does the learner care — and if so, how and why? Engagement measures a learner’s motivational relationship with the learning experience, from complete absence through externally driven compliance to deep identity-integrated alignment. Grounded primarily in Self-Determination Theory, Expectancy-Value Theory, and Interest Development Theory.

Capability

Was real ability actually built — and is it durable? Capability measures the durability and transferability of actual skill or knowledge acquired. It extends beyond Bloom’s cognitive operations and Dreyfus’s expertise stages by adding a consolidation axis (L1) and a knowledge creation axis (L7). Near transfer (d=0.5–0.8) is achievable; far transfer (d=0.1–0.3) requires deliberate design.

Portability

Does the learner know they have it, and can the world see it? Portability measures how visible, articulable, and transferable a learner’s capability is. Drawing on Polanyi’s tacit knowledge, Nonaka’s SECI model, and metacognition research, it captures the often-invisible gap between having capability and being able to leverage it.

Opportunity

Does the environment support or block learning application? Added in v2.0 based on COM-B and behavior change literature. Opportunity measures the degree to which a learner’s environment enables or prevents the application of learning. Supervisor support (r=+0.50) is the strongest single predictor of training transfer. Even capable, motivated learners fail when systems don’t support new behaviors.

MECE Architecture

The four dimensions are designed to be mutually exclusive (each captures a distinct aspect of learning value that the others do not) and collectively exhaustive (together they cover the full space of learning outcomes). Engagement measures motivation and investment. Capability measures actual skill acquisition and transfer. Portability measures the visibility and articulability of learning. Opportunity measures environmental support.

The strongest MECE challenge comes from metacognition, which straddles Capability (regulating learning processes) and Portability (knowing what you know). The LVA addresses this by recognizing that metacognitive knowledge belongs in Portability while metacognitive regulation belongs in Capability.

Methodology

Theory Selection: Comprehensive search across learning science, behavioral science, organizational learning, and knowledge management. 20 theories selected for breadth of coverage across four dimensions, strength of evidence base, and practical applicability to learning design.

Alignment Scoring: Expert judgment mapping each theory’s constructs to LVA dimensions. Scores reflect the relative emphasis a theory places on each dimension (0–100%), not empirical correlation. Multiple reviewers with background in educational psychology, instructional design, and organizational development.

Evidence Grading: STRONG = supported by meta-analyses or multiple randomized trials with consistent effect sizes. MODERATE = supported by multiple studies or strong theoretical grounding with limited empirical validation of specific claims. EMERGING = conceptual support with limited empirical evidence or primarily qualitative research.

Version History

v1.0 (2024): Three dimensions — Engagement, Capability, Portability. Based on initial learning science review.

v2.0 (2025): Four dimensions — added Opportunity dimension informed by COM-B and behavior change literature. Engagement refined to six named levels (Absent through Aligned) replacing generic labels. Capability expanded to 7 levels (added Unconsolidated and Generative). Portability restructured around visibility progression (Tacit through Integrated). All levels grounded in specific research with effect sizes where available. Added multi-level extension (individual, team, organizational, societal). Added regression states and topic-level granularity.

Limitations

The LVA is a diagnostic framework, not an empirical instrument. Alignment percentages between theories and dimensions represent expert judgment based on literature review, not statistical correlation. Evidence grades vary by level — some levels (e.g., Capability L1 Unconsolidated) rest on decades of meta-analytic evidence, while others (e.g., Portability L5 Translated) have primarily conceptual support. The MECE claim is grounded in careful design but not mathematical proof; the strongest challenge comes from metacognition, which straddles Capability and Portability. Some theories are more established than others — the Collective Edge (Fisher, 2025) is a practitioner framework while SDT has 40+ years of empirical validation. The framework is a diagnostic tool, not a predictive model; it describes where learning is, not where it will go.
