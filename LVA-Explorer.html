About

What is the LVA?

The Learning Value Architecture (LVA) is a diagnostic framework for understanding where learning actually is — not where we hope it is. It was developed to address a persistent problem in education and training: most measurement stops at satisfaction and completion, missing the deeper questions of whether real capability was built, whether it’s durable, and whether the learner can leverage it.

The LVA organizes learning outcomes into four mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive (MECE) dimensions, each with progressive levels that describe a developmental trajectory from absence to mastery.

The Four Dimensions

Engagement

Does the learner care — and if so, how and why?

Capability

Was real ability actually built — and is it durable?

Portability

Does the learner know they have it, and can the world see it?

Opportunity

Does the environment support learning progression?

MECE Architecture

The four dimensions are designed to be mutually exclusive (each captures a distinct aspect of learning value that the others do not) and collectively exhaustive (together they cover the full space of learning outcomes). Engagement measures motivation and investment. Capability measures actual skill acquisition and transfer. Portability measures the visibility and articulability of learning. Opportunity measures environmental support.

The strongest MECE challenge comes from metacognition, which straddles Capability (regulating learning processes) and Portability (knowing what you know). The LVA addresses this by recognizing that metacognitive knowledge belongs in Portability while metacognitive regulation belongs in Capability.

Version History

v1.0 (2024): Three dimensions — Engagement, Capability, Portability. Based on initial learning science review.

v2.0 (2025): Four dimensions — added Opportunity dimension informed by COM-B and behavior change literature. Sub-levels refined with evidence grades. Capability expanded to 7 levels (added Unconsolidated and Generative). All levels grounded in specific research with effect sizes where available.

Limitations

The LVA is an interpretive framework, not an empirical instrument. Alignment scores between theories and dimensions represent expert judgment based on literature review, not statistical correlation. Evidence grades reflect the current state of research and may change as new meta-analyses are published. The MECE claim is an aspiration grounded in careful design, not a mathematical proof — edge cases exist, particularly around metacognition and self-regulation.
