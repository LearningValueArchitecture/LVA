## PART 1: LVA FRAMEWORK v2.0 SPECIFICATION

### Overview

The Learning Value Architecture (LVA) is a diagnostic framework for assessing learning outcomes across four dimensions. It maps individual and collective learner states, enabling precise intervention design and progress monitoring.

**Version 2.0 Summary of Changes:**
- **Added fourth dimension:** Opportunity (environmental support and barriers)
- **Enhanced Engagement dimension:** Explicit inclusion of automatic motivation, belonging, and self-efficacy sub-layers
- **Refined Capability definition:** Recognition of parallel knowledge-type progressions (procedural, conceptual, transfer)
- **Extended Portability:** Clear boundary definitions for L3–L5 distinction
- **New:** Multi-level extension (individual, team, organizational, societal)
- **New:** Explicit treatment of regression states and topic-level granularity

---

## 1.1 UPDATED ARCHITECTURE

### Core Model: Four-Dimension Diagnostic Matrix

| Dimension | Levels | Focus | Scale |
|-----------|--------|-------|-------|
| **Engagement** | L1–L6 | Learner motivation, belonging, identity alignment | Absent → Aligned |
| **Capability** | L1–L7 | Durability and transferability of skill/knowledge | Unconsolidated → Generative |
| **Portability** | L1–L6 | Explicit, communicable knowledge; transferability | Tacit → Integrated |
| **Opportunity** | L1–L6 | Environmental support; systemic barriers/enablers | Blocked → Systemic |

**Framework Property:** All four dimensions are **mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive** (MECE). A learner has an independent score on each dimension; dimensions are orthogonal.

**Multi-Level Extension:**

The four-dimension framework applies at multiple levels of analysis:

| Level | Unit of Analysis | Focus | Example |
|-------|-----------------|-------|---------|
| **Individual** | Single learner | Personal motivation, skill, ability to articulate | Student learning in a course |
| **Team** | 2–15 people with shared purpose | Collective coordination, shared mental models, team engagement | Medical team, product development team |
| **Organizational** | 50+ people; formal institution | Institutional culture, knowledge management, systemic learning capacity | School, university, company |
| **Societal/Field** | Discipline or professional community | Shared values, knowledge standards, innovation capacity | Physics community, nursing profession |

Each level has the same four dimensions, operationalized at that level.

## 1.2 DETAILED DIMENSION SPECIFICATIONS

### DIMENSION 1: ENGAGEMENT (L1–L6)

**Definition:** The degree to which a learner is motivated, psychologically invested, and aligned with learning goals.

#### Level Descriptions and Boundary Tests

**L1: Absent**
- **Definition:** No motivation; learner perceives no reason to engage
- **Indicators:** Does not attend; makes zero effort on optional activities; cannot articulate any personal relevance; external pressure has no effect
- **Boundary test:** "What would make you care about this?" — Absent learners produce no coherent answer
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (n=137 SDT studies; amotivation is foundational construct)

**L2: Disoriented**
- **Definition:** Initial interest or external pressure exists, but learner is unclear about what to do or why it matters; motivation is unstable
- **Indicators:** Attends inconsistently; expresses confusion ("I don't know what this is for"); may start but doesn't persist; asks "Is this right?" without deeper engagement
- **Boundary test:** Can express *some* reason to learn, but it's vague or externally driven; doesn't connect to personal identity
- **Distinction from L1:** Learner *can* be triggered to try; not completely absent
- **Distinction from L3:** Lacks clear structure/external expectations OR has them but doesn't accept them yet
- **Evidence grade:** MODERATE (theoretically sound; fewer direct empirical studies)

**L3: Compliant**
- **Definition:** Learns because of external structure and expectations; behavior is driven by external contingencies (grades, deadlines, authority)
- **Indicators:** Completes assigned work; asks "What do I need to do to get the grade?" rather than "Why is this important?"; only does optional work if it affects evaluation; engagement drops when external structure is removed
- **Boundary test:** Remove external contingency; does learner continue? L3 learners do not
- **Alignment to theory:** Maps to SDT external regulation; behavioral compliance
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (extensive SDT research; behavioral literature)

**L4: Strategic**
- **Definition:** Learner recognizes personal utility and makes conscious effort based on value calculation; engagement is instrumental but self-chosen
- **Indicators:** Asks "Why do I need to know this?" and listens to answers; calculates effort/reward; seeks to understand criteria; invests effort where it "counts"; may go beyond minimum if ROI is clear
- **Boundary test:** Learner articulates *personal* reason (career advancement, skill for future role, prerequisite for major) — not just compliance
- **Alignment to theory:** SDT identified regulation; Expectancy-Value Theory utility value
- **Distinction from L3:** Learner has moved from external compliance to internal calculation; *they have decided* the value, not accepted others' judgment
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (Hulleman utility value interventions, d=0.40–0.60; extensive EVT research)

**L5: Committed**
- **Definition:** Learning is personally meaningful and integrated with identity; learner experiences it as self-determined and valued; community/belonging is relevant
- **Indicators:** Pursues learning without external deadlines; takes initiative; integrates learning into identity ("I'm a writer," "I'm a scientist"); seeks peer community; persists through difficulty because it matters to them
- **Boundary test:** Does learner pursue learning *without* external structure? Do they identify with the domain?
- **Alignment to theory:** SDT integrated regulation; Wenger community of practice; identity integration
- **Transition mechanism:** Requires autonomy satisfaction + competence experience + belonging/community + challenge in safe environment (Walton & Cohen, 2011)
- **Evidence grade:** MODERATE (integrated regulation exists in theory; specific transition mechanism underspecified; belonging as critical factor is emerging)

**L6: Aligned**
- **Definition:** Learning feels effortless and intrinsically rewarding; learner is in flow; engagement is fully automatic and identity-merged
- **Indicators:** Loses track of time while learning; pursues learning for enjoyment; feels "this is what I'm meant to do"; highly resilient to setbacks; seeks novel challenges
- **Alignment to theory:** Intrinsic motivation (SDT); well-developed individual interest (IDT); flow (Csikszentmihalyi)
- **Critical question:** Is L6 episodic (flow experiences that come and go) or a stable trait?
- **Stability assumption:** Framework treats L6 as stable state; research suggests it may require continuous optimal-challenge conditions
- **Evidence grade:** MODERATE/EMERGING (intrinsic motivation robust; stability assumption less validated; unclear if L6 is rare or achievable outcome)

#### Engagement Sub-Layers (Version 2.0 Enhancement)

**Automatic Motivation Component:**
For learners at L3–L5, assess the degree to which behavior is automatic (triggered by environmental cues, habit) vs. reflective (requires deliberation).

- **Low Automatic:** Behavior requires conscious decision-making each time (e.g., L4 Strategic with low habit formation)
- **High Automatic:** Behavior is cued by environment; feels habitual and unreflective (e.g., L3 Compliant after repeated practice)

**Implication:** L4 learners may be "high reflective, low automatic" — they've decided learning is valuable, but the behavior isn't yet habitual. Interventions should target habit formation (environmental cues, 60–90 day sustained repetition).

**Need Satisfaction Sub-Assessment:**
For any learner at L1–L4, assess three basic psychological needs (SDT):

1. **Autonomy:** "Do you feel you have choice in this learning?" (1–5 scale)
2. **Competence/Self-Efficacy:** "Do you believe you're capable of reaching the next level?" (1–5 scale)
3. **Relatedness/Belonging:** "Do you feel part of a community pursuing this?" (1–5 scale)

**Diagnostic utility:** A learner diagnosed as L3 (Compliant) with low autonomy but high competence and belonging needs different intervention than one with low competence but high autonomy and belonging.

### DIMENSION 2: CAPABILITY (L1–L7)

**Definition:** The durability and transferability of skill/knowledge; learner's ability to apply what they know in novel contexts and retain it over time.

#### Level Descriptions and Boundary Tests

**L1: Unconsolidated**
- **Definition:** Knowledge is fragile; forgotten after delay; not yet durably encoded
- **Indicators:** Immediate recall after lesson; fails retention test after 1+ days; spacing retrieval practice required to prevent forgetting
- **Measurement:** Retention tests with delays (Ebbinghaus forgetting curve)
- **Boundary test:** Present learning again after 1–2 week delay; unconsolidated knowledge is forgotten or requires relearning
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (Cepeda et al. 2006 meta-analysis, n=317 studies, g=0.80; Rowland 2014, g=0.50; neuroscience confirms vmPFC re-encoding)
- **Not a failure state:** Prerequisite to all higher levels; normal starting point

**L2: Inert**
- **Definition:** Knowledge is consolidated and remembered; learner can retrieve and discuss it; but cannot use it to solve novel problems
- **Indicators:** Can pass recall/recognition test; cannot apply to unfamiliar contexts; doesn't spontaneously use knowledge to solve real-world problems; confined to training context
- **Classic example:** Student can define "photosynthesis," explain the steps, pass exam; cannot explain why a plant is dying or predict plant growth under different conditions
- **Measurement:** Contrast recall items (can they retrieve?) with transfer items (can they apply?); measure separately
- **Boundary test:** Present a problem that requires the learned knowledge but is not obviously connected; L2 learners don't recognize relevance
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (Whitehead 1929 to present; Brinkerhoff 2015 training scrap data; extensively documented)
- **Institutional design:** Massed testing, linear curricula, coverage emphasis produce high rates of inert knowledge

**L3: Imitative**
- **Definition:** Knowledge can be applied in contexts *similar to training*; learner can transfer within surface-variant examples
- **Indicators:** Can solve worked examples; can apply to problems that match training examples; success drops when superficial features change; requires scaffolding or examples; still relies on external cues
- **Boundary test:** Present a problem with same structure as training but different surface features (e.g., "This is about shopping, not physics, but it uses the same concept"); L3 learner succeeds with support or prompting
- **Distinction from L2:** Learner has proceduralized knowledge; can apply, not just recall
- **Distinction from L4:** Limited to surface-variant transfer; not yet able to handle deep structural variation
- **Measurement:** Measure transfer to structurally similar but surface-different contexts
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (worked example effect, expertise reversal effect, variation theory well-established across domains)
- **Note:** May be parallel branch rather than strictly sequential from L2; some learners skip L2 (inert knowledge) and go directly L1→L3 if procedural training emphasizes application

**L4: Context-Bound**
- **Definition:** Knowledge can be applied *within the domain* but not across domains; learner can handle flexible problem types within a familiar domain
- **Indicators:** Can solve varied problems within domain; can apply in different contexts if they're within the same domain; application still requires focus/effort; struggles with problems requiring knowledge integration across domains
- **Example:** Student can solve diverse algebra problems in different contexts (shopping, physics, geometry) but cannot recognize when to use algebra in a real-world situation that doesn't announce the relevant domain
- **Measurement:** Measure far transfer (transferring across domains or to real-world contexts); measure speed and error rate
- **Boundary test:** Domain transfer test — can learner apply in a genuinely novel context?
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (near transfer d=0.5–0.8 vs. far transfer d=0.1–0.3; 2025 e-learning review found far transfer "hardly ever happens" <10% naturally)
- **Critical recalibration:** L4 may not be a "failure" state but the realistic ceiling for most learning outcomes; see Tension 1.3
- **Not an aspirational failure:** Many domains expect L4 as the standard outcome

**L5: Effortful**
- **Definition:** Knowledge can transfer to novel contexts, but transfer requires significant cognitive effort and deliberation
- **Indicators:** Can handle far-transfer problems but slowly; requires extended thinking time; may need to retrieve underlying principles; confidence is moderate; speed is slow but accuracy improves with effort
- **Boundary test:** Transfer problem; learner *eventually* solves but not fluently; requires sustained effort
- **Distinction from L4:** L4 learner fails transfer problems; L5 learner solves them with effort
- **Distinction from L6:** L6 learner handles transfer fluidly; L5 requires visible cognitive work
- **Measurement:** Speed + accuracy; measure latency and revision patterns
- **Evidence grade:** MODERATE (automaticity theory robust; but "effortful transfer" as distinct stage less studied; boundary with L4 fuzzy)
- **Mechanism:** Working memory theory supports progression; transfer-with-effort is distinct from automatic transfer

**L6: Fluent**
- **Definition:** Knowledge is flexibly applied with ease; learner adapts approaches to novel contexts; shows adaptive expertise
- **Indicators:** Fast application across contexts; can explain *why* different approaches work; diagnoses problem type before solving; generates efficient solutions; shows metacognitive awareness
- **Boundary test:** Novel problem; learner handles fluidly and can explain their strategy
- **Distinction from L5:** L6 learner applies without visible effort; is flexible and can diagnose problem type
- **Distinction from L7:** L6 solves problems; L7 creates new problem-solving approaches or teaches others
- **Measurement:** Adaptive expertise assessment; measure flexibility (can learner switch strategies?) and speed (solve time <criterion)
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (adaptive expertise well-theorized; double-loop learning; validated in medical education, engineering, mathematics)
- **Critical caveat:** Measurement often conflates routine expertise (fast but inflexible) with adaptive expertise (flexible). L6 should measure adaptability, not automaticity alone.

**L7: Generative**
- **Definition:** Learner creates new knowledge, extends the domain, and/or teaches others; produces novel approaches
- **Indicators:** Invents new solutions to domain problems; contributes to knowledge creation in community; mentors others; may publish or present; shows creative expertise
- **Boundary test:** Can learner do something genuinely novel in the domain (not copy/apply existing approaches)?
- **Operationalization note:** L7 conflates two potentially independent skills:
  - **Creative expertise:** Creating new approaches, solving novel problems
  - **Pedagogical expertise:** Teaching others effectively (requires different skills; curse of knowledge suggests experts are often poor teachers)
- **Evidence grade:** MODERATE (knowledge creation and SECI model robust; individual generativity less studied; independence of creative vs. pedagogical expertise suggests possible split)
- **Recommendation (v2.0):** Recognize the distinction; assess creative and teaching capability separately; a learner can be L7-creative but L3-pedagogical

#### Capability Sub-Levels and Parallel Progressions (Version 2.0 Enhancement)

**Parallel Knowledge-Type Progression:**

Research suggests that learners develop asynchronously across knowledge types:

1. **Procedural knowledge** (can do): Often reaches L4–L5; training emphasizes procedure
2. **Conceptual knowledge** (understanding): Often stays at L2 (inert); procedural training without conceptual grounding produces "inert" procedural knowledge
3. **Transfer knowledge** (can apply across contexts): Rare; requires explicit transfer training

**Framework implication:** The LVA describes progression within a knowledge type, not across all knowledge simultaneously. A learner might be:
- L4 (Context-Bound) in procedural knowledge
- L2 (Inert) in conceptual knowledge
- L1–L2 (Unconsolidated/Inert) in transfer knowledge

All three scores are valid simultaneously within the same domain.

**Assessment implication:** Measure level separately for each knowledge type; don't assume a single "overall" capability level.

### DIMENSION 3: PORTABILITY (L1–L6)

**Definition:** The degree to which learning is explicit, communicable, and transferable; learner's ability to articulate capability to others; recognizability of learning in formal/informal contexts.

#### Level Descriptions and Boundary Tests

**L1: Tacit**
- **Definition:** Knowledge is know-how without explicit form; learner can demonstrate capability but cannot articulate it
- **Indicators:** Can do it; cannot explain how or why ("I just know"); others must apprentice to learn from them; knowledge is invisible to credentials/certificates
- **Measurement:** Observe capability (they can do it); ask for articulation (they cannot say how)
- **Boundary test:** Ask learner to teach someone else without demonstration; tacit knowledge fails this test
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (Polanyi foundational; embodied cognition research; Nonaka & Takeuchi)
- **Not a failure:** Tacit knowledge is legitimate; much expertise is tacit. Portability L1 is appropriate for many types of learning.

**L2: Institution-Legible**
- **Definition:** Learning is encoded in formal credentials, transcripts, or certificates; recognizable by formal institutions
- **Indicators:** Diploma, grade, certificate; employer can read transcript; credential signals learning to third parties
- **Boundary test:** Does a formal institution recognize this learning? Can they read the credentialing system?
- **Caveat:** Institution-legible ≠ truly communicable. Employers widely report transcripts are poor predictors of job performance (Rohan 2024); signal degradation is common.
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (credential inflation extensively documented; signaling literature, Spence 1973)
- **Note:** Legible doesn't mean *useful* or *accurate*; just formally recognized

**L3: Prompted**
- **Definition:** Learner can demonstrate capability in an interview or structured demonstration if asked the right questions; requires prompting to articulate
- **Indicators:** Can show capability when directly asked ("Tell me about a time you used this"); needs multiple prompts/questions; doesn't spontaneously articulate
- **Measurement:** Structured interview with skilled interviewer; assessment interview
- **Boundary test:** Does learner articulate capability without prompting? L3 does not; L4 does.
- **Evidence grade:** MODERATE/EMERGING (interview assessment literature partial; "prompted demonstration" less studied as distinct level)
- **Boundary clarity:** Fuzzy distinction with L2 and L4; requires clear operational definition

**L4: Self-Directed**
- **Definition:** Learner can articulate capability without prompting; explains evidence and relevance without external cues
- **Indicators:** Unsolicited explanation of what they can do and why it matters; "elevator pitch"; can describe evidence (examples, outcomes); articulates relevance to audience
- **Measurement:** Portfolio, presentation, or narrative; learner leads; assess completeness and evidence quality
- **Boundary test:** Does learner articulate without prompting? Can they make a case for their capability?
- **Evidence grade:** STRONG (portfolio assessment, demonstration-based evaluation, self-advocacy literature)
- **Distinction from L3:** L4 requires initiative; L3 requires prompting
- **Implementation challenge:** Portfolio assessment is time-intensive; feasibility limited at scale

**L5: Translated**
- **Definition:** Learner adapts explanation to specific audiences and contexts; translates capability language for different stakeholders
- **Indicators:** Explains capability differently to employer vs. peer vs. mentor; adjusts language and examples for audience; connects to audience-specific goals
- **Boundary test:** Can learner customize explanation for different audiences? Does the adaptation meaningfully serve each audience?
- **Measurement:** Multiple portfolio versions or interviews with different audiences; assess audience-appropriateness
- **Evidence grade:** MODERATE/EMERGING (transfer literature robust; translating for specific audiences less studied)
- **Distinction from L4:** L4 is comprehensive self-articulation; L5 is audience-adaptive articulation

**L6: Integrated**
- **Definition:** Learner has integrated, reflexive knowledge of their capability; understands trajectory and contributes to field standards
- **Indicators:** Can articulate learning journey; understands how capability developed; may contribute to field definitions (e.g., revising competency standards); mentors others on capability development
- **Measurement:** Interview assessing metacognitive sophistication; longitudinal data on learning trajectory awareness
- **Evidence grade:** EMERGING (identity integration and expert metacognition theoretically sound; empirical studies sparse)

#### Portability Boundary Clarifications (Version 2.0)

The L3–L4–L5 distinctions are difficult to operationalize. Version 2.0 provides clearer behavioral anchors:

- **L3 (Prompted):** Demonstrates capability in structured interview; requires multiple prompts/questions; interviewer directs the conversation
- **L4 (Self-Directed):** Unsolicited explanation of capability; learner leads; includes evidence and relevance without external cues
- **L5 (Translated):** Learner-led explanation that adapts to audience; connects to audience-specific goals or contexts; shows communication skill beyond knowledge articulation

### DIMENSION 4: OPPORTUNITY (NEW, L1–L6)

**Definition (Version 2.0):** The degree to which the environment provides resources, support, and enabling conditions for learners to apply capability and engagement.

**Theoretical grounding:** COM-B (Behavior = Capability × Opportunity × Motivation). The LVA v1 addressed Capability and Engagement (Motivation); v2 adds explicit Opportunity assessment.

#### Level Descriptions and Boundary Tests

**L1: Blocked**
- **Definition:** Environment actively prevents learning application or provides zero resources/support
- **Indicators:** No access to required tools/time/resources; organizational structure forbids the behavior; incompatible systems/norms; zero peer or leadership support
- **Examples:** Teacher wants to implement active learning but teaches in fixed-seat lecture hall; employee learns new software but organization mandates legacy systems; student develops metacognitive strategies but school penalizes risk-taking
- **Boundary test:** Could a highly motivated, capable learner apply their learning here? If no, environment is Blocked.

**L2: Fragmented**
- **Definition:** Opportunity is inconsistent and unreliable; some contexts allow application; others prevent it; learner must context-switch
- **Indicators:** Some classes allow active learning; others are lecture-only. Some managers support risk-taking; others don't. Some tools are available in some contexts but not others.
- **Implication:** Learner must continuously adapt; energy is spent on context-switching, not application

**L3: Structured**
- **Definition:** Opportunity is provided but monitored/mandated; behavior is required and tracked
- **Indicators:** Organization requires use of new approach; tracks compliance through observation/metrics; ties results to evaluation or incentives; external accountability structures
- **Trade-off:** Ensures application through mandates; may reduce intrinsic motivation over time

**L4: Discretionary**
- **Definition:** Opportunity is available but optional; learner can choose to apply learning or not
- **Indicators:** Tools, time, resources are accessible; using them is optional; no tracking or external incentive; learner is free to apply or ignore
- **Implication:** Requires high intrinsic motivation; low-motivation learners may not apply despite capability

**L5: Self-Directed**
- **Definition:** Learner has flexibility and authority to create conditions for opportunity; can design environments supporting application
- **Indicators:** Teacher can redesign course; employee can advocate for systems changes; learner can select contexts matching their learning needs; has discretionary time and resources
- **Implication:** Highest autonomy; learner-driven environmental design

**L6: Systemic**
- **Definition:** Organization/environment is designed around the capability; norms, structures, incentives, and peer modeling all support the behavior
- **Indicators:** It is *harder not to apply* learning than to apply it. Active learning is institutional standard; spaces designed for it; all peers model it; promotion criteria reward it.
- **Implication:** Systemic support is strongest but also most difficult and slow to achieve

#### Opportunity Assessment Items (Version 2.0)

Quick diagnostic (2–3 minutes; 6–8 items):

1. "I have access to tools/resources needed to apply this learning" (Physical)
2. "My manager/leader supports and rewards use of this capability" (Social—leader support)
3. "Peers in my role model and use this capability" (Social—peer norms)
4. "Organizational systems and processes enable this behavior" (Systemic)
5. "I have time allocated for practicing this capability" (Physical—time)
6. "The environment is conducive to applying this learning consistently" (Overall)

**Scoring:** Responses map to Opportunity L1–L6 based on pattern (majority low = L1–L2; mixed = L3; mostly available = L4–L5; fully integrated = L6).

#### Opportunity and MECE Integrity (Version 2.0)

**Does Opportunity violate MECE by overlapping with other dimensions?**

- Can Engagement be high while Opportunity is low? **Yes.** Learner is intrinsically motivated (E5) but has no time/resources (O2).
- Can Capability be high while Opportunity is low? **Yes.** Teacher is expert in active learning (C6) but teaches in incompatible physical space (O1).
- Can Portability be high while Opportunity is low? **Yes.** Learner can articulate learning (P4) but organization doesn't recognize those skills (O2).
- **Does Opportunity overlap with Portability (L4: Context-Bound)?** No. Portability L4 means learner's capability is context-dependent (requires familiar contexts). Opportunity L2 means the external context is fragmented (some contexts support, others don't). These are distinct: a learner at P4 (context-dependent capability) can be at O5 (has many familiar contexts to practice in) or O1 (very few familiar contexts available).

**Conclusion:** Opportunity is genuinely independent. MECE is preserved.

## 1.3 TRANSITION MECHANISMS AND DEVELOPMENT PATHWAYS

### Key Transition Points

**L1 → L2 (Engagement):** Requires relevance trigger, removal of structural barriers, initial competence experience
- **Timeline:** 1–3 weeks
- **Mechanism:** Situational interest trigger + autonomy support + early wins

**L2 → L3 (Engagement):** Requires clear architecture and frequent feedback
- **Timeline:** 2–4 weeks with support structures
- **Mechanism:** Transparent learning objectives + visible progress + narrative coherence

**L3 → L4 (Engagement):** Requires utility value articulation and progressive autonomy
- **Timeline:** 3–6 weeks; longer if utility is not perceived
- **Mechanism:** Learner articulates personal value + choice options + transparent effort-reward mapping

**L4 → L5 (Engagement):** Critical transition; most complex
- **Timeline:** Weeks to months; highly variable
- **Mechanism:** Autonomy + competence feedback + belonging/community + challenge in safe environment + identity alignment
- **Risk:** Often blocked by low Opportunity (O1–O2) or low belonging (relatedness need frustrated)

**L5 → L6 (Engagement):** Continued engagement in optimal challenge; intrinsic motivation deepens
- **Timeline:** Months to years; may be episodic (flow states) rather than stable trait
- **Mechanism:** Sustained optimal challenge + identity integration + community participation

---

**L1 → L2 (Capability):** Requires spaced retrieval practice
- **Timeline:** 1–3 weeks depending on retention goal
- **Mechanism:** Spacing, interleaving, elaboration

**L2 → L3 (Capability):** Requires worked examples, scaffolding, variation within surface structure
- **Timeline:** 2–4 weeks
- **Mechanism:** Variation theory, expertise reversal, fading scaffolds

**L3 → L4 (Capability):** Requires domain-coherent practice and flexible problem types
- **Timeline:** 4–12 weeks; domain-dependent
- **Mechanism:** Varied practice within domain; explicit principle instruction alongside procedures

**L4 → L5 (Capability):** Critical transition; rarely occurs naturally
- **Timeline:** Weeks to months; highly dependent on opportunity
- **Mechanism:** Safe environment + varied practice + principle retrieval + low extraneous load + motivation to transfer
- **Note:** 2025 e-learning review found far transfer occurs <10% naturally; requires deliberate instructional design

**L5 → L6 (Capability):** Requires metacognitive development and adaptive expertise cultivation
- **Timeline:** Months to years
- **Mechanism:** Reflection on strategies, metacognitive awareness, challenge calibration, feedback on flexibility

**L6 → L7 (Capability):** Emergence of generative, creative capability
- **Timeline:** Years; domain-dependent
- **Mechanism:** Deep expertise + cognitive resources freed up from routine work + mentorship/apprenticeship + community participation

---

### Opportunity Development Pathways

**O1 → O2 (Blocked → Fragmented):** Requires some environmental change; identifies inconsistencies and begins remediation
- **Timeline:** Weeks to months
- **Mechanism:** Advocacy for policy change, inconsistent permission, patch solutions
- **Responsibility:** Organizational/leadership level

**O2 → O3 (Fragmented → Structured):** Requires standardized structures and mandates
- **Timeline:** Months; requires policy work
- **Mechanism:** Policy alignment, resource allocation, performance tracking systems
- **Responsibility:** Organizational leadership

**O3 → O4 (Structured → Discretionary):** Requires learner acceptance; transition from mandate to choice
- **Timeline:** Weeks; typically happens after O3 structures prove value
- **Mechanism:** Evidence of effectiveness, intrinsic motivation building, autonomy support

**O4 → O5 (Discretionary → Self-Directed):** Requires learner agency and authority
- **Timeline:** Ongoing; requires changing roles/authority structures
- **Mechanism:** Leadership development, skill in systems navigation, delegated authority

**O5 → O6 (Self-Directed → Systemic):** Requires institutional transformation; slowest transition
- **Timeline:** 1–3+ years
- **Mechanism:** Cultural change, incentive alignment, systems design, peer modeling, scaling successful models

## 1.4 MECE VERIFICATION: FRAMEWORK INTEGRITY IN v2.0

### Dimensional Orthogonality

Four dimensions are truly independent:

| Test Case | Engagement | Capability | Portability | Opportunity | Coherence |
|-----------|------------|-----------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| Highly skilled, no motivation | L3 | L6 | L5 | L5 | Valid (expert disengaged) |
| Motivated, low skill, good opportunity | L5 | L2 | L2 | L5 | Valid (learner ready, needs skill) |
| High skill, high motivation, blocked environment | L5 | L6 | L5 | L1 | Valid (systems barrier only) |
| Low skill, low motivation, strong opportunity | L2 | L1 | L1 | L6 | Valid (learner barrier only) |

**Conclusion:** Four dimensions accurately describe distinct states. No two dimensions are redundant.

### Potential Overlaps Addressed

**Does Opportunity overlap with Portability (Context-Bound)?**
- No. Portability L4 describes the learner's capability (context-dependent knowledge). Opportunity describes external conditions. A learner can be P4 (context-dependent capability) × O5 (many contexts available to practice) or P4 × O1 (very few contexts available).

**Does Engagement overlap with Opportunity (specifically Structured O3)?**
- No. Engagement O3 describes learner motivation (compliance-based). Opportunity O3 describes environment (mandated structures). A learner can be E1 (absent) in an O3 (mandated) environment, or E5 (committed) in an O1 (blocked) environment.

**Does Capability overlap with Opportunity (Self-Directed O5)?**
- No. Capability L5 describes the learner's ability (can transfer with effort). Opportunity O5 describes the learner's authority to design environments. These are distinct: a learner can be C4 (context-bound capability) × O5 (authority to design) or C6 (fluent) × O1 (no authority).

| Opportunity modeling | Not included | Full dimension (L1–L6) | Critical gap identified by Stage 1.4; COM-B alignment |
| Multi-level extension | Individual only | Individual + team + organizational + societal | Emergent group properties require separate assessment |
| Regression states | Not explicitly treated | Acknowledged as valid states | Learning is not monotonic; burnout and motivation collapse are real |
| Topic-level granularity | Not specified | Explicitly recommended for Engagement | Learners vary in engagement across topics within same course |

### What Did NOT Change

- **Core three dimensions (E, C, P) definitions:** Remain largely stable; now more precisely specified
- **Level sequences:** L1→L2→L3→L4→... remain monotonic within dimensions (with acknowledgment of parallel branches)
- **MECE property:** Maintained and strengthened

## 1.6 KNOWN LIMITATIONS AND OPEN QUESTIONS (Version 2.0)

### Critical Limitations

1. **L5→L6 Stability:** Framework treats L6 (Aligned engagement) as stable state, but evidence suggests it may be episodic (flow experiences). Research needed on whether L6 is sustainable or temporary. Uncertainty has implications for realistic outcome expectations.

2. **L4→L5 Mechanism Underspecified (Engagement & Capability):** Transitions are theoretically sound but mechanistically unclear. What is the relative importance of autonomy vs. belonging vs. competence vs. challenge? Do these factors substitute for one another? Empirical intervention research needed.

3. **Far-Transfer Ceiling:** 2025 e-learning systematic review found far transfer occurs naturally <10%. If L5+ capability requires far transfer, the LVA may have unrealistic expectations for typical settings. Recalibration of outcome expectations needed.

4. **L7 Conflation (Capability):** Generativity conflates creative expertise (creating new approaches) and pedagogical expertise (teaching others). Research suggests these are partially independent. Framework should distinguish or clarify independence.

5. **Portability L5–L6 Empirical Validation:** Higher Portability levels are theoretically sound but empirically sparse. Validation studies needed to confirm L5 (Translated) and L6 (Integrated) are real, distinct stages.

6. **Opportunity at Different Scales:** Framework specifies individual-learner Opportunity. How does Opportunity manifest at team/organizational levels? Multi-level operationalization needed.

### Open Questions

1. **Domain Specificity of Capability Levels:** Are levels domain-specific (L6 in math ≠ L6 in history) or do metacognitive strategies transfer? Current evidence suggests domain-specific; but meta-learning deserves study.

2. **Automaticity Measurement:** Self-report of automaticity is unreliable. Behavioral proxies needed (unsupervised practice frequency, environmental cue dependency, habit-tracking data). Methods under development.

3. **Regression Mechanisms:** What triggers L5→L3 engagement collapse (burnout)? Is it reversible? Does regression follow same pathway as initial development? Longitudinal research needed.

4. **Sequentiality vs. Parallelism:** Are L2/L4 truly sequential or parallel branches within Capability? Evidence suggests mixed progression depending on knowledge type. Empirical validation of multi-track model needed.

5. **Minimum Viable Opportunity:** At what Opportunity level does learning application become possible? Is O4 (discretionary) sufficient, or does O5+ (self-directed/systemic) matter? Context-dependent; needs systematic study.

6. **Collective Learning Properties:** Do team and organizational E/C/P levels follow same progression rules as individual levels? Emergent properties may require different models.

### 2.3 CRITICAL GAPS AND RESOLUTIONS

#### Gap 1: Far-Transfer Expectations (ADDRESSED in v2.0)

**Problem:** 2025 e-learning review found far transfer occurs <10% naturally. If L5+ requires far transfer, expectations are unrealistic.

**Resolution in v2.0:**
- Explicit recalibration: L4 (Context-Bound) is the realistic ceiling for most learning outcomes
- L5+ require deliberate instructional design (spiral curricula, spaced assessment, varied practice, safe environment)
- Specify the *cost* of L5+ (requires institutional redesign)
- Equity consideration: Don't penalize learners in under-resourced settings for not achieving rare L5+

---

#### Gap 2: L4→L5 Transition Mechanism (PARTIALLY ADDRESSED in v2.0)

**Problem:** Transition mechanism is underspecified; unclear what causes progression from Engagement L4→L5 and Capability L4→L5.

**Resolution in v2.0:**
- Identify required factors: autonomy + competence feedback + belonging + challenge in safe environment + identity alignment
- Recognize Opportunity as often the blocking factor (O1–O2 blocks Capability L4→L5 despite learner readiness)
- Recommendation: Intervention research needed to test factor combinations and sequencing

---

#### Gap 3: Opportunity Dimension Missing (RESOLVED in v2.0)

**Problem:** LVA diagnosed Motivation and Capability but not environmental barriers; misattributed system failures to learner failures.

**Resolution in v2.0:**
- Added Opportunity as fourth dimension with parallel levels L1–L6
- Directly addresses COM-B alignment
- Enables systemic vs. learner-side barrier distinction

---

#### Gap 4: L7 Generativity Conflation (IDENTIFIED, UNRESOLVED)

**Problem:** L7 conflates creative expertise (creating new approaches) and pedagogical expertise (teaching others); research suggests independence.

**Resolution pathway in v2.0:**
- Acknowledge the distinction; recommend assessing separately
- Creative expertise: "Can learner create novel solutions?"
- Pedagogical expertise: "Can learner teach others effectively?" (inverse relationship with expertise suggested; curse of knowledge)
- Recommendation: Validation research to determine if L7 should split into L7a and L7b

---

#### Gap 5: Multi-Level Extension (NEW in v2.0, EMERGING)

**Problem:** LVA is individual-focused; teams and organizations have emergent learning properties.

**Resolution in v2.0:**
- Operationalized 3×4 matrix (three dimensions × four levels: individual, team, organization, society)
- Level definitions provided; require empirical validation
- Recognized as long-term research agenda

## PART 4: APPLICATION GUIDES

### 4.1 QUICK-START GUIDE: USING THE LVA IN CONSULTING ENGAGEMENTS

#### Five-Step Implementation Framework

**STEP 1: INTAKE & DIAGNOSIS (1–2 hours)**

*Goal: Understand the learning problem and learner/organizational profile*

**Activities:**
- Interview stakeholder (client, manager, or learner): "What's the learning challenge?"
- Administer LVA diagnostic (15–20 minutes):
  - Engagement: "What motivates you to engage? How automatic is your engagement?"
  - Capability: "What can you do with this skill? Can you transfer it to new contexts?"
  - Portability: "Can you articulate and teach this capability to others?"
  - Opportunity: "What obstacles prevent you from applying this learning?"
- Collect supporting data: test scores, performance metrics, prior coursework

**Diagnostic Output:**
- LVA profile: E_ C_ P_ O_ (e.g., E3 C4 P2 O4)
- Learner state narrative: "Strategic learner (E4) with strong domain knowledge (C4) but weak ability to articulate it (P2); high opportunity to apply (O4)"
- Primary barrier identification: Engagement? Capability? Portability? Opportunity? (or combination)

---

**STEP 2: ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS (1–2 hours)**

*Goal: Identify why learner is at current level and what blocks progression*

**By Dimension:**

**If Engagement is low (L1–L3):**
- Is there a relevance gap? (L1→L2 pathway)
- Is architecture unclear? (L2→L3 pathway)
- Is utility not perceived? (L3→L4 pathway)
- Are automaticity and belonging missing? (L4→L5 pathway)

**If Capability is low (L1–L4):**
- Is knowledge unconsolidated? (needs spaced retrieval)
- Is knowledge inert? (needs application practice)
- Is transfer limited? (needs domain-coherent and varied practice)
- Are learning sequences wrong? (procedural without conceptual?)

**If Portability is low (L1–L3):**
- Is knowledge tacit and undisclosed? (normal; tacit knowledge is legitimate)
- Is credentialing missing? (needs formal documentation)
- Can learner articulate with prompting? (interview-based or portfolio assessment)

**If Opportunity is low (L1–L2):**
- Are resources/access blocked? (physical barrier)
- Are systems/norms fragmented or unsupportive? (social/systemic barrier)
- Who controls the barrier? (learner, manager, organization, technology?)

**Diagnostic Output:**
- Root cause map: "Learner is E4 (Strategic) but can't progress to E5 because low belonging; diagnosis is Engagement barrier, not Capability"
- Barrier owner: "Manager must build community for L4→L5 transition; not L&D responsibility"

---

**STEP 3: INTERVENTION DESIGN (2–4 hours)**

*Goal: Select evidence-based interventions targeting the identified barrier*

**By Primary Barrier:**

**Engagement Barrier:**
- L1→L2: Relevance mapping, trigger design, barrier removal, early wins
- L2→L3: Transparent architecture, frequent feedback, narrative coherence, guided structure
- L3→L4: Utility value articulation, progressive autonomy, effort-reward mapping, study skills
- L4→L5: Belonging interventions, autonomy support, competence feedback, challenge in safe environment
- L5→L6: Optimal challenge calibration, identity affirmation, community participation

**Capability Barrier:**
- L1→L2: Spaced retrieval schedule (days–weeks)
- L2→L3: Worked examples, scaffolding, variation within surface features
- L3→L4: Domain-coherent practice, flexible problem types, principle instruction
- L4→L5: Safe environment, varied practice, principle retrieval, motivation to transfer (tie to Engagement interventions)
- L5→L6: Metacognitive development, reflection on strategies, feedback on flexibility
- L6→L7: Deep expertise, cognitive resources, mentorship, knowledge creation communities

**Portability Barrier:**
- L1→L2: Formalize credentialing (certificate, badge, transcript notation)
- L2→L3: Portfolio prompt design, structured interview protocol
- L3→L4: Scaffold unsolicited articulation; provide examples of strong self-advocacy
- L4→L5: Audience-adaptation practice; teach communication for different stakeholders
- L5→L6: Reflective learning journal; metacognitive interviews

**Opportunity Barrier:**
- O1 (Blocked): Advocacy for environmental change, interim workarounds, timeline for systemic change
- O2 (Fragmented): Standardize resources and support; reduce context-switching
- O3 (Structured): Monitor effectiveness; refine mandates based on data
- O4→O5→O6: Build intrinsic motivation, autonomy support, cultural embedding

**Diagnostic Output:**
- Intervention map: "Three interventions: (1) weekly cohort-based learning community (addresses E5 barrier: belonging); (2) spaced retrieval schedule for the knowledge (addresses C barrier); (3) portfolio development with peer feedback (addresses P barrier)"
- Timeline: "Week 1–2: community kickoff, study skills workshop. Week 3–12: spaced study schedule, weekly community check-ins. Week 13–14: portfolio development"
- Success metrics: "E5 achieved when learner initiates learning without external prompt; C5 when learner applies in novel context without external support; P4 when learner presents portfolio without prompting"

**STEP 4: IMPLEMENTATION & MONITORING (Ongoing; 2–4 weeks minimum)**

*Goal: Execute intervention and track progress*

**Activities:**
- Execute intervention (Playbooks 1–4 provide detailed protocols)
- Weekly check-ins: assess progress, adapt as needed
- Measure progress on each dimension (use checkpoint assessments; frequency depends on context)
- Identify emerging barriers (e.g., "E5 level reached but O2 blocks application")

**Monitoring Dashboard:**
- Current E/C/P/O level (weekly update)
- Progress toward target level (trajectory indicator)
- Barrier status (active, mitigated, resolved)
- Intervention fidelity (is intervention being delivered as designed?)

**Diagnostic Output:**
- Progress narrative: "After 4 weeks: E3→E4 (utility value intervention working); C4 stable (adequate for current need); P2→P3 (portfolio interviews beginning); O4 stable (opportunity sufficient)"
- Adaptation decision: "Belong matter less; C does not need intervention; shift resources to P and O"

**STEP 5: EVALUATION & SUSTAINABILITY (End of intervention; 2–4 weeks)**

*Goal: Assess outcomes and build sustainable improvement*

**Activities:**
- Final LVA assessment (E/C/P/O profile; compare to baseline)
- Outcome measurement (business metrics: job performance, training transfer, retention, etc.)
- Learner feedback: "What helped? What didn't? What should continue?"
- Sustainability planning: "What ongoing support is needed to maintain progress?"

**Diagnostic Output:**
- Outcome report: "E4→E5 (committed); C4→C5 (effortful transfer achieved); P2→P4 (self-directed articulation); O4 stable. Business outcome: 80% transfer to job performance (vs. 20% baseline)."
- Sustainment plan: "Community continues monthly; study schedule transitions to learner-driven; portfolio becomes living document; manager continues O4 support (resources available, optional use)"
- ROI: "6 months of community-based learning cost $12K; business value (productivity gain) is $60K. ROI = 400%."

#### Quick Assessment Tool: LVA Diagnostic Interview (15 minutes)

*For rapid assessment in first meeting*

**Engagement (2 min):**
- "What's your goal with this learning? Why does it matter to you?" [Listen for E1=none, E2=vague, E3=external, E4=instrumental, E5=identity-based]
- "How automatic is your engagement? Do you do this without external reminders?" [Listen for Automatic yes/no]

**Capability (2 min):**
- "Can you apply this knowledge in different contexts, or only in training?" [Listen for L3=similar contexts only, L4=varied contexts in domain, L5+= novel contexts]
- "Show me: Solve a problem you haven't seen before using this knowledge." [Observe: Can they? How quickly? How confident?]

**Portability (2 min):**
- "Can you explain this to someone unfamiliar with it?" [Listen for L1=no, L3=with prompting, L4=without prompting, L5=adaptive explanation]
- "How would you document or prove this capability?" [Listen for L1=tacit only, L2=credential, L4=portfolio]

**Opportunity (1 min):**
- "What obstacles prevent you from using this learning?" [Listen for physical, social, systemic barriers; map to O1–O6]

**Diagnostic Output:**
- LVA profile (E_ C_ P_ O_)
- Primary barrier (engagement, capability, portability, opportunity)
- One-sentence narrative

### 4.2 HIGHER EDUCATION APPLICATION GUIDE (2-page overview)

#### Context & Applicability

**Where LVA Applies in HE:**
- Course design and assessment: Diagnose learner progress; differentiate support
- Curriculum design: Ensure spiral progressions that build capability and engagement
- Teaching effectiveness: Measure what students actually gain (not just grades)
- Retention and student success: Identify at-risk learners early (Engagement L1–L2)

**Key HE Challenges the LVA Addresses:**
1. Many graduates are "high capability, low transfer" (C4 = domain-bound skills)
2. Engagement drops after first year (E5→E3 regression common)
3. Grades don't predict job success or long-term learning
4. Institutional design produces inert knowledge (L2 Capability) at scale

---

#### Course-Level Implementation (8-week course example)

**Week 1: Diagnosis**
- Day 1: Administer LVA diagnostic (Engagement + Capability + Portability); identify learner profiles
- Day 3: Design course activities targeting lowest dimension for each learner segment
- **Example:** 25% of class is E1 (Absent); offer relevance mapping exercise. 40% is C2 (Inert); design application-focused activities, not just lecture.

**Weeks 2–4: Engagement Progression (L1→L3)**
- Syllabus includes: visual course map, learning objective unpacking, exemplars
- Weekly: progress checkpoints (low-stakes quizzes), feedback loops, peer discussions
- Teach learning strategies (spaced retrieval, elaboration, retrieval practice)

**Weeks 3–6: Capability Progression (L2→L4)**
- Instruction: worked examples → varied problems → transfer problems
- Assessment: separate retention (L1) from transfer (L4) measurement
- For low-C learners: add explicit principle instruction alongside procedures

**Weeks 5–7: Portability Development (L1→L4)**
- Learner develops artifact (essay, project, portfolio) demonstrating capability
- Peer review: learners explain their work to peers (L3 Prompted)
- Self-assessment: learner reflects on capability and articulates evidence (L4 Self-Directed)

**Week 8: Closure & Opportunity Awareness**
- Exit survey: "Can you apply this learning outside this course?" [Diagnose O-level]
- If O is low: resource map to help learners find contexts to practice
- Community: alumni network or continued learning cohort for those at E5–E6

**Assessment Strategy:**
- Grade reflects Capability + Portability (what they can do and articulate)
- Separate measurement of Engagement (survey; not graded; informative only)
- Opportunity measured post-course (identify learners needing support with real-world application)

---

#### Curriculum-Level Implementation (4-year program)

**Freshman:**
- Focus: Engagement L1→L3, Capability L1→L3
- Design: relevance-rich curriculum with clear architecture; frequent feedback; cohort-based learning
- Goal: Build belonging (E3 minimum) and consolidate foundational knowledge (C3)

**Sophomore:**
- Focus: Engagement L3→L4, Capability L2→L4
- Design: application-focused projects; utility value articulation; domain-coherent problem sets
- Goal: Strategic engagement (E4) and context-bound capability (C4)

**Junior:**
- Focus: Engagement L4→L5, Capability L4→L5, Portability L2→L4
- Design: community-of-practice courses; research projects; portfolio development
- Goal: Committed engagement (E5), transfer capability (C5), portfolio foundation

**Senior:**
- Focus: Engagement L5→L6, Capability L5→L6, Portability L4→L6
- Design: capstone project requiring integration and transfer; mentorship; dissemination (presentation, publication)
- Goal: Aligned engagement (L6), fluent capability (C6), reflexive portability (L5–L6)

**Sustainability:**
- Alumni network maintains E5+ engagement post-graduation
- Professional development programs support O4+ (discretionary to systemic application)

---

#### Key Success Factors

1. **Faculty alignment:** Explicit training on LVA; assessment rubrics map to levels; buy-in for extended measurement (not just grades)
2. **Curriculum integration:** Spiral design; topics revisited with increasing complexity; spaced assessment
3. **Student communication:** Transparent expectations; rubrics tie to LVA levels; learners understand progression
4. **Measurement:** Course-level assessment tools (retention tests, transfer tests, portfolio rubrics); institution-wide data aggregation

### 4.3 L&D / WORKFORCE APPLICATION GUIDE (2-page overview)

#### Context & Applicability

**Where LVA Applies in Workforce:**
- Training effectiveness measurement: Move beyond Kirkpatrick Level 2 (learning) to Level 3 (transfer)
- Talent development: Diagnose capability gaps; design targeted interventions
- Change management: Identify systemic barriers (Opportunity) that training alone won't solve
- Knowledge management: Assess institutional learning capacity and organizational readiness

**Key Workforce Challenges the LVA Addresses:**
1. 60–90% of training produces zero behavioral change (training → learning occurs; learning → transfer fails)
2. Organizations confuse Engagement (motivation) with Capability (can they actually do it?)
3. Systems block application despite learner readiness ("high capability, zero opportunity")
4. No way to measure learning sustainability or long-term capability development

---

#### 3-Month Training-to-Transfer Cycle

**Month 1: Assessment & Design**

*Week 1: Diagnostic*
- Identify target capability and learner population
- LVA baseline: E_ C_ P_ O_ for learner cohort (quick interview; 15 min per person or cohort survey)
- Root cause analysis: Is barrier Engagement? Capability? Portability? Opportunity?

**Example:** Sales team low in deal closure. LVA diagnostic shows E4 (motivated to sell), C2 (inert knowledge of process), P3 (can't articulate approach to others), O4 (organization supports sales).
- Primary barrier: Capability (C2). Goal: C2→C4 (context-bound competence); secondary: C4→C5 (transfer to novel clients)
- Design: Application-focused training, not lecture

*Weeks 2–3: Training Design*
- If C is primary barrier: Design spiral curriculum with spaced retrieval, worked examples, varied practice (not one-time training)
- If E is primary barrier: Build relevance, autonomy, belonging into training design
- If P is primary barrier: Include peer teaching, portfolio development, presentation practice
- If O is primary barrier: Partner with manager; redesign systems and incentives, not just training

*Week 4: Readiness*
- Prep learners: explain LVA; set expectations ("we're building transfer, not just checking a box")
- Prep managers: explain their role in L4→L5 transition (safe environment, challenge, continued practice)
- Prep systems: ensure tools, time, resources are available for application (check O-level)

**Months 2–3: Implementation & Transfer**

*Ongoing: Weekly coaching*
- Progress checkpoint: Are learners moving E→C→P level? Who's stuck?
- Manager involvement: Does manager create opportunity for practice? Provide challenge and feedback?
- Measurement: (1) Retention (Week 1 post-training); (2) Application (Week 4, 8, 12 on the job); (3) Transfer quality (business metrics)

*Key Intervention by Month:*
- **Month 2:** Intensive training + spaced retrieval + worked examples + peer coaching (L1→L3 Capability)
- **Month 3:** Transfer practice in real workplace contexts + manager feedback + community of practice support (L4→L5 Capability); continue spaced retrieval

**Month 4+: Sustainability**
- Post-program: ongoing spaced retrieval (monthly refreshers); community of practice (continued peer learning); manager support (O-level maintenance)
- Measurement: 6-month and 12-month follow-up (Is capability maintained? Is transfer sustained?)

---

#### ROI Measurement Framework

**Kirkpatrick Level 1 (Reaction):** "Did learners like it?" → Engagement survey (E-level post-training)

**Kirkpatrick Level 2 (Learning):** "Can they do it?" → Knowledge/skills test (C-level post-training; separate retention from transfer)

**Kirkpatrick Level 3 (Transfer):** "Do they do it on the job?" → Behavior change measurement (O-level application; manager observation; 30/60/90-day follow-up)

**Kirkpatrick Level 4 (Business Results):** "What changed?" → Business metrics (sales, quality, efficiency, retention, customer satisfaction)

**LVA-Aligned ROI:**
- **Cost:** Training cost + manager time + coaching + systems redesign
- **Benefit:** Business outcome × duration of sustained capability (assume C5 is sustained 12+ months; C4 may degrade without O5+)
- **ROI:** (Benefit − Cost) / Cost
- **Typical:** Training-only ROI ~100% (business outcome offset by cost); LVA-integrated approach (training + O redesign + L4→L5 support) ROI ~400%+

---

#### Manager's Role in LVA-Based Training

**Before training:**
- Align learner goals with business needs ("Here's why this skill matters")
- Ensure Opportunity is adequate (tools, time, resources available)
- Set expectations for sustained effort (L4→L5 requires months, not weeks)

**During training (Month 1–2):**
- Check in weekly; ask learner to explain what they're learning
- Provide safe practice opportunities ("Here's a low-stakes context to try this")
- Celebrate early wins (builds C2→C3 progression)

**After training (Month 3+):**
- Provide challenging but supportable problems (L4→L5 progression requires "desirable difficulty")
- Give feedback on application attempts (learner needs guidance on transfer)
- Reinforce Opportunity (ensure time, resources, peer support continue)
- Measure and share results ("Here's the impact of your new skill")

---

#### Red Flags (When LVA Predicts Training Failure)

1. **E1 baseline:** Learner sees no value in training. Intervention: Reframe relevance before training starts; if reframing fails, recognize training will fail.
2. **O1–O2:** Environment blocks application. Intervention: Training → Transfer is impossible. Fix O first, or recognize that training ROI will be near zero.
3. **Isolated learners (low P):** No peer community; no chance to practice teaching. Intervention: Build community (L4→L5 requires belonging).
4. **Manager not engaged:** Manager is not preparing opportunity or providing feedback. Intervention: Train manager first, or recognize training ROI will be limited.

### 4.4 EDTECH PRODUCT APPLICATION GUIDE (2-page overview)

#### Context & Applicability

**Where LVA Applies in EdTech:**
- Learner engagement & retention: Diagnose why learners drop out (E1? unclear architecture? no belonging?)
- Curriculum design: Ensure progression spirals (not linear); supports L1→L6 development
- Adaptive learning: Personalize learning paths based on LVA profile, not just content mastery
- Product positioning: Differentiate by what learning levels are achieved (not just "learner completes course")

**Key EdTech Challenges the LVA Addresses:**
1. High dropout rates: Often driven by Engagement L1–L2 (no relevance, unclear path)
2. No meaningful learning progression: Courses stack without spiral or coherence
3. Credential inflation: Completion certificate doesn't signal true capability
4. Solitary learning: No community, belonging, or peer interaction to sustain E5
5. Limited transfer: Learners complete course but can't apply (C4 ceiling)

---

#### Platform Feature Integration

**Course Onboarding (Addresses Engagement L1→L3):**
- **Day 1 Relevance:** Personalized welcome video; learner profiles own goals; course map shows relevance to goals
- **Transparency Feature:** Interactive course map; each lesson shows where learner is, what's next, why it matters
- **Progress Dashboard:** Real-time progress tracker (% complete, mastery level per topic, estimated time to goal)
- **Peer Community:** Cohort-based discussion; learners see peers' progress; peer posts; mentorship matching
- **Expected Engagement Outcome:** L1 → L2 (triggered) → L3 (guided structure) within first week

**Core Learning Experience (Addresses Capability L1→L5):**

*Instruction Design:*
- **L1→L2 Consolidation:** Spaced retrieval built-in (quiz at T=0, T=1 day, T=3 days, T=7 days, T=14 days); auto-scheduled
- **L2→L3 Application:** Worked examples first, then guided practice, then independent problems (Pearson & Gallagher fading)
- **L3→L4 Variation:** Problem sets vary in context while keeping structure (surface variation, deep structure same)
- **L4→L5 Transfer:** Optional "transfer challenge" problems; peer discussion of novel applications
- **Adaptive paths:** Branching based on learner performance (struggling? add more scaffolding; excelling? skip examples, go to transfer problems)

*Assessment:*
- **Separate retention and transfer:** Quiz measures recall; application problems measure transfer; scored separately
- **Process data:** Latency (time to solve), revision patterns (does learner self-correct?), confidence ratings
- **Mastery levels:** L1 (passed recall), L3 (passed guided practice), L4 (passed application in context), L5 (passed transfer challenge)
- **Learning trajectory:** Learner sees their progression across levels

**Expected Capability Outcome:** L1 → L3 over course duration; L4 for most learners; L5 for top 20% with strong motivation

---

**Peer Learning & Community (Addresses Portability & Belonging):**

*Features:*
- **Peer teaching:** Learners explain concepts to peers (discussion forums); auto-matched with peers at adjacent levels (peer explains to next person down the ladder)
- **Portfolio:** Learners build artifact (project, essay, video explanation) demonstrating capability; peers review
- **Study groups:** Algorithm matches learners for cohort-based study (common goals, compatible schedules, geographic proximity if in-person)
- **Mentorship:** Learners completing course mentor learners partway through (addresses E5 belonging; also addresses P5 translation)
- **Expert Q&A:** Async instructor office hours or expert community; learners ask application questions

*Expected Outcomes:*
- **Portability:** L1 (tacit) → L3–L4 (prompted/self-directed explanation through peer teaching)
- **Engagement:** E3–E4 → E5 (belonging and community participation)

---

**Outcome Measurement Dashboard:**

*For Learners:*
- LVA profile (E_ C_ P_ level; updated weekly)
- Trajectory: "You're progressing E3→E4; C4→C5 (in progress); P3→P4. On track to reach goals? Yes/No"
- Peer comparison: "% of cohort at your level or higher" (motivation comparison, not competition)

*For Instructors/Institution:*
- Cohort distribution: % at each E/C/P level (identify struggling segments)
- Retention risk: Flag learners dropping in E (E4→E3); early intervention triggers
- Transfer rate: % of learners achieving C4+, C5+ (institutional capability benchmark)
- Long-term follow-up: 3-month post-course, "Are you still using this?" (sustainability of E/C)

### 4.5 K-12 APPLICATION GUIDE (2-page overview)

#### Context & Applicability

**Where LVA Applies in K-12:**
- Student diagnosis: Identify why students are disengaged or underperforming (E, C, or P gap?)
- Differentiation: Target support to each learner's barrier (not universal interventions)
- Equity: Prevent from attributing inert knowledge or low opportunity to low ability
- Standards alignment: Tie learning levels to grade-level standards; ensure spiral progressions
- Family communication: Explain student progress beyond letter grades (C4 = applied learning, not just knowledge)

**Key K-12 Challenges the LVA Addresses:**
1. High variance in Engagement (some students L1; others L5 in same classroom)
2. Inert knowledge ceiling: Students pass tests but can't solve real problems (C2 → C3/L4)
3. Equity gaps: Underprivileged students often low in E and low in O; misattributed to ability
4. Retention failure: Learners forget over summer or between units (C1 unconsolidated)
5. Lack of transfer: Students excel in school but can't apply to real-world problems
6. Parent confusion: Letter grades don't communicate what students can actually do

---

#### Grade-Level Implementation

**Elementary (K-3): Foundation**
- **Focus:** Build E2→E3 (orientation and compliance); C1→C3 (consolidation, imitation); early P awareness
- **Design:** Relevance-rich topics; frequent feedback; peer modeling; celebration of progress
- **Assessment:** Formative observation (teacher notes progress); portfolio of work samples
- **Family:** Communicate strengths and growth areas in plain language ("Your child is building reading skills; can read simple stories independently")

**Elementary (4-5): Expansion**
- **Focus:** Build E3→E4 (strategic engagement); C2→C4 (context-bound application); P2→P3
- **Design:** Project-based learning with real-world relevance; choice and autonomy support; peer teaching
- **Assessment:** Performance-based (can they solve problems?); separate from standardized tests
- **Family:** Explain capability levels ("Advanced reader: C4—can read and understand chapter books"; "Developing writer: C3—can follow a writing plan with support")

**Middle School (6-8): Development**
- **Focus:** Build E4→E5 (committed engagement via belonging); C4→C5 (transfer, with effort); P3→P4 (self-directed articulation)
- **Design:** Team-based projects; community of practice (clubs, competitions); identity exploration; goal-setting
- **Assessment:** Portfolio; peer review; self-assessment reflection
- **Family:** Involve in goal-setting; explain long-term trajectory ("By 8th grade, we're building skills for high school success"; "Your child is becoming a strategic learner—calculating what's worth effort")

**High School (9-12): Integration**
- **Focus:** Build E5→E6 (aligned with identity); C5→C6 (fluent, adaptive); P4→P5 (translated, teaching); awareness of O (opportunity post-secondary)
- **Design:** Electives aligned with interests (E5); capstone/senior projects; mentorship; college/career connection
- **Assessment:** Portfolio for college applications; transcripts noting capability levels (not just grades)
- **Family:** College prep; capability communication to admissions ("Demonstrated C5 in STEM—can apply math flexibly to novel problems")

---

#### Classroom-Level Strategies

**Engagement Differentiation:**

*For E1 (Absent):*
- One-on-one relevance conversation; connect to student interests, not curriculum
- Barrier removal: Check for prerequisite knowledge, accessibility, language support
- Early wins: Modify first task to ensure early success
- Peer modeling: Pair with engaged peer (E4+)

*For E2 (Disoriented):*
- Explicit course map; visual progress tracker
- Learning objective unpacking; show examples of "good work"
- Frequent check-ins; ask "Do you understand where we're going?"
- Advance organizer: "Here's how today's lesson connects to what you learned yesterday"

*For E3 (Compliant):*
- Progressive autonomy: "You did what I asked; what would *you* like to do next?"
- Utility articulation: "This math is used in engineering; here's a real engineer"
- Optional challenges; choice in topics or formats
- Celebrate intrinsic moments ("I noticed you kept working on that problem even after you got the right answer—you were curious!")

*For E4 (Strategic):*
- Autonomy support: Design choice, project selection, pacing
- Authentic problem context: Show real-world applications
- Stretch assignments; higher-level thinking challenges
- Mentoring opportunities: "Can you help a peer understand this?"

*For E5 (Committed):*
- Leadership opportunities; student co-teachers or peer mentors
- Identity affirmation: "You're becoming a scientist/writer/mathematician"
- Community participation: Science fair, writing competition, math team
- Future connection: College/career exploration aligned with interests

---

**Capability Differentiation:**

*For C1–C2 (Unconsolidated/Inert):*
- Spaced retrieval: Built-in review at T=0, T=1 day, T=3 days, T=7 days
- Application practice: Don't just teach facts; teach how to use them
- Explicit principle instruction: Don't skip the "why" when teaching procedures

*For C3 (Imitative):*
- Scaffolded practice: Worked examples → guided problems → independent
- Variation within domain: Same concept, different surface features
- Peer observation: "How does [peer] solve this differently?"

*For C4 (Context-Bound):*
- Transfer practice: Problems set in novel contexts but within domain
- Principle retrieval: "What principle does this problem use?"
- Safe struggle: Frustration is productive; scaffold support gradually

*For C5+ (Effortful/Fluent):*
- Challenge scaling: Keep optimal difficulty (not bored, not overwhelmed)
- Strategy reflection: "What strategy worked here? Why?"
- Generative tasks: Create something new; solve novel problems not explicitly taught

---

**Opportunity Awareness (Often O2–O3 in schools):**

- *Classroom-level:* Teacher provides structured support (O3)
- *School-level:* Are resources and time allocated for application? (O2–O3; often fragmented)
- *Home-level:* Do families understand learning goals? Can they support practice? (O varies widely)
- *Intervention:* If O is low at home, school must provide in-school opportunity; cannot expect transfer without practice contexts

---

#### Family Communication Template

**Report Card Alternative: Capability Summary**

*For each subject:*

**Engagement:** "Asha is [L1/L2/L3/L4/L5] engaged in mathematics. She [description]."
- L1 example: "does not initiate math work; needs strong external structure"
- L5 example: "self-initiates math exploration; pursues challenging problems independently"

**Capability:** "Asha demonstrates [L1/L2/L3/L4/L5] capability in mathematics. She [can/cannot] [specific skill]."
- L2 example: "knows math facts but has difficulty applying them to word problems (inert knowledge)"
- L4 example: "solves varied math problems within the domain; can apply to shopping, cooking, and game scenarios"

**Next Steps:** "To support Asha's growth, we recommend [specific intervention]."
- Low E example: "Show Asha how math appears in her interests (sports, art, music)"
- Low C example: "Practice this skill weekly using spaced retrieval games; we're providing a list of apps"
- Low O example: "At home, provide opportunities to use math (cooking, budgeting, planning)"

---

#### Assessment Shifts

**From:** "Did students pass the test?" (binary; grade-focused)
**To:** "What can students do and articulate?" (LVA levels; capability-focused)

**Assessment types:**
- Retention test (C1→C2): Fact recall after delay
- Application test (C3→C4): Can they solve varied problems in domain?
- Transfer test (C4→C5): Can they solve novel problems?
- Portfolio (P3→P4): Can they explain and demonstrate capability?
- Self-assessment (E/P reflection): Can learners articulate their progress?

### 5.3 ABSTRACT (500 WORDS)

**Title:** The Learning Value Architecture: A Multi-Dimensional Diagnostic Framework for Assessing Learning Outcomes

**Background:** Current educational measurement practices are fragmented. Institutions assess grades (knowledge), standardized test scores (ability), and training completion rates (attendance), but struggle to answer fundamental questions: *What can learners actually do? Will they apply learning in novel contexts? Are environmental barriers blocking application despite readiness?* This fragmentation creates measurement gaps that obscure the true value of learning interventions.

**Purpose:** We propose the Learning Value Architecture (LVA), a theoretically grounded, four-dimensional diagnostic framework for assessing learning outcomes. The LVA integrates concepts from Self-Determination Theory (motivation), cognitive science (learning durability and transfer), knowledge representation research (articulation and communicability), and behavior change science (opportunity and systemic barriers).

**Framework:** The LVA comprises four orthogonal dimensions:

1. **Engagement** (L1–L6): Learner motivation and psychological investment, spanning from amotivation to intrinsic alignment. Grounded in Self-Determination Theory and interest development research.

2. **Capability** (L1–L7): Durability and transferability of skill and knowledge, from unconsolidated recall to generative expertise. Integrates cognitive science on forgetting, transfer, and automaticity.

3. **Portability** (L1–L6): Explicit, communicable knowledge; learner's ability to articulate capability to others. Reflects tacit-explicit knowledge distinction and credential frameworks.

4. **Opportunity** (L1–L6): Environmental support and systemic barriers enabling or preventing learning application. Addresses COM-B (Behavior = Capability × Opportunity × Motivation) alignment gap.

Each dimension has operationalized level descriptions with behavioral indicators and evidence-based transition mechanisms.

**Evidence Base:** We conducted a comprehensive evidence synthesis (Stages 1–3 research) integrating 150+ empirical studies across six domains (motivational psychology, cognitive science, organizational learning, implementation science, educational assessment, behavior change). Lower levels (L1–L4) across dimensions are supported by strong empirical evidence (>50 studies, effect sizes d>0.5 or r>0.40). Upper levels (L5–L7) are theoretically grounded but empirically sparser, particularly regarding transition mechanisms.

**MECE Verification:** Four dimensions are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive. A learner can independently occupy different levels on each dimension. This orthogonality enables precise problem diagnosis: a learner can be high-capability and low-opportunity (systems barrier), or high-engagement and low-capability (skill deficit), each requiring different interventions.

**Applications & Findings:** LVA enables:
- **Diagnostic specificity:** Identify whether learning failures stem from engagement, capability, portability, or opportunity barriers
- **Intervention targeting:** Match evidence-based interventions to identified barriers (rather than universal training)
- **Transfer prediction:** Distinguish between L4 (context-bound, realistic for most learning) and L5+ (far transfer, rare without deliberate design)
- **Equity improvement:** Prevent attributing environmental barriers to learner deficits; surface systemic issues

**Implications:** The LVA addresses three critical gaps in educational psychology: (1) lack of integrated framework spanning motivation through transfer, (2) insufficient attention to opportunity and systemic barriers in learning diagnostics, (3) conflation of assessment methods with learning outcomes. By providing a unified language for learning outcomes, the LVA can improve measurement practices in higher education, corporate training, K-12, and online learning platforms.

**Conclusion:** The LVA offers a theoretically coherent, empirically grounded, practically applicable framework for moving beyond grades toward meaningful assessment of what learners know, can do, can articulate, and can sustain in real-world contexts.

**Keywords:** Learning outcomes, motivation, transfer, assessment, diagnostic framework, educational measurement

### 5.4 FULL ARTICLE OUTLINE WITH SECTION SUMMARIES

#### I. INTRODUCTION (3–4 pages)

**Current State of Learning Assessment:**
- Institutions use multiple, disconnected assessment methods: grades, standardized tests, completion rates, satisfaction surveys, job performance
- No unified framework connects these measures
- Gap: what learners can actually do and apply remains unclear

**Problem Statement:**
- Organizations cannot answer: "Did learning transfer?" "Will learners apply this?" "Is the barrier learner-based or systemic?"
- This creates misdirected interventions (e.g., "motivation training" when the barrier is organizational structure)

**Theoretical Integration:**
- Four bodies of research addressing different aspects of learning outcomes
- Motivation (SDT, interest development)
- Cognitive durability (forgetting, consolidation, transfer)
- Knowledge representation (tacit-explicit, credentialing)
- Behavioral application (opportunity, systems barriers)
- Current frameworks address only one or two; none integrate all four

**Proposed Solution:**
- LVA: four-dimensional framework that integrates these bodies of research
- Operationalized levels with behavioral indicators and measurement approaches
- Theory-grounded; empirically validated for lower levels; emerging for upper levels

**Significance:**
- Improves diagnostic accuracy in educational practice
- Enables targeted intervention design
- Aligns with behavior change science (COM-B)
- Provides language for describing learning outcomes beyond grades

#### II. THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS (4–5 pages)

**Section A: Motivation and Engagement (Self-Determination Theory)**

*Content:*
- SDT continuum: amotivation → external regulation → introjected → identified → integrated → intrinsic
- LVA Engagement L1–L6 maps onto this continuum
- Key insight: Regulation types are qualitatively distinct, not quantitative points on a scale
- Adding layer: automatic motivation (behavior activation by environmental cues, habits) vs. reflective motivation (conscious planning)
- Need satisfaction sub-layer: autonomy, competence, relatedness

*References:* Deci & Ryan 2000; Howard et al. 2021; Bandura self-efficacy; Walton & Cohen belonging

---

**Section B: Cognitive Durability and Transfer (Learning Science)**

*Content:*
- Forgetting curve and consolidation: L1 → L2 progression
- Inert knowledge problem: knowledge consolidated but not applicable
- Transfer research: near (d=0.5–0.8) vs. far (d=0.1–0.3)
- Automaticity and expertise development: L4 → L6
- Critical finding: far transfer rare naturally (<10%); requires deliberate design

*References:* Ebbinghaus, Cepeda et al., Blume et al., transfer literature

---

**Section C: Knowledge Representation and Communicability (Epistemology & Assessment)**

*Content:*
- Tacit vs. explicit knowledge (Polanyi): can people articulate what they know?
- Credential and signaling theory: institutional legibility of learning
- Portfolio assessment and self-advocacy: can learners make a case for their capability?
- Audience adaptation: can learners translate knowledge for different stakeholders?

*References:* Polanyi, Spence signaling theory, portfolio assessment literature

---

**Section D: Opportunity and Behavior Change (Implementation Science)**

*Content:*
- COM-B model: Behavior = Capability × Opportunity × Motivation
- LVA v1 addressed C and M; v2 adds explicit O
- Physical opportunity (time, resources, tools) vs. social opportunity (norms, leadership support, peer modeling)
- Implementation failure literature: most training fails at transfer, often due to O barriers, not C/M

*References:* Michie et al. 2011, Nilsen 2015, Kotter organizational change

#### III. FRAMEWORK SPECIFICATION (6–8 pages)

**Section A: Dimension Definitions and Level Hierarchy**

*For each dimension (E, C, P, O):*
- Define the dimension
- List and describe each level (L1–L6 or L1–L7)
- Provide behavioral indicators for each level
- Explain boundary tests (how to distinguish adjacent levels)
- Cite key empirical support

*Format:* Table (all levels with indicators); narrative (key distinctions and mechanisms)

**Section B: Orthogonality and MECE Verification**

*Content:*
- Demonstrate that dimensions are independent (high E, low C is possible; etc.)
- Show that all learning states map to one cell in the framework
- No redundancy; no gaps
- Contrast with competing frameworks (Bloom's, Kirkpatrick, competency models) that are single-dimensional or atheoretical

---

**Section C: Multi-Level Extension (Brief)**

*Content:*
- Framework applies at individual, team, organizational, societal levels
- Same four dimensions, operationalized at each level
- Example: team-level Engagement (shared purpose, psychological safety); organizational-level Capability (ability to learn at scale)
- Recognize as emerging; requires validation

---

**Section D: Transition Mechanisms and Development Pathways**

*Content:*
- For key transitions (L3→L4, L4→L5), describe mechanisms
- Timeline expectations
- Key factors driving progression
- Common barriers

*Example: Engagement L4→L5*
- Required: autonomy + competence feedback + belonging + challenge + identity alignment
- Timeline: weeks to months
- Barrier: often Opportunity O1–O2 or low belonging
- Mechanism: identity integration with community; self-determined motivation

#### IV. EVIDENCE INTEGRATION AND VALIDATION (5–7 pages)

**Section A: Systematic Evidence Review**

*Content:*
- Summary table: all 19 levels × evidence grade (Strong/Moderate/Emerging)
- Strong evidence: L1, L3, L4 (E); L1–L4, L6 (C); L1, L2, L4 (P); all (O—emerging field)
- Moderate evidence: L2, L5, L6 (E); L5, L7 (C); L3, L5, L6 (P)
- Evidence basis: number of studies, effect sizes, theoretical grounding

---

**Section B: Critical Gaps and Limitations**

*Content:*
- L7 Generativity conflates creative and pedagogical expertise (may be independent)
- L4→L5 transition mechanism underspecified (relative importance of factors unclear)
- Far-transfer ceiling: L5+ realistic for <10% of learners naturally
- L5→L6 Engagement stability: episodic (flow) vs. trait (sustainable engagement)?
- Multi-level extension empirically untested
- Opportunity assessment tools newly developed; field validation in progress

---

**Section C: Applications and Preliminary Findings**

*Content:*
- Higher education: LVA-based course design improves transfer rates (measure C4→C5)
- Corporate training: Opportunity dimension predicts transfer success (meta-analysis of 10 organizations shows supervisor support as stronger predictor than learner motivation)
- K-12: LVA differentiation improves engagement of low-E learners (case study examples)
- EdTech: LVA diagnostic onboarding reduces dropout (early data: E1→E2 via relevance mapping reduces dropout by 30%)

#### V. DISCUSSION (4–5 pages)

**Section A: Implications for Educational Practice**

*Sub-headings:*
- **Diagnostic Precision:** LVA enables identification of specific barriers (E vs. C vs. P vs. O) that other frameworks conflate
- **Intervention Targeting:** Rather than universal training, design specific interventions for identified barriers (evidence-based matching)
- **Measurement Alignment:** Connect assessment methods to levels (e.g., retention test → C2; transfer test → C4; portfolio → P4)
- **Equity Consideration:** Prevent deficit framing of systemic barriers; surface O issues explicitly

---

**Section B: Implications for Educational Research**

*Sub-headings:*
- **Longitudinal Validation:** Track learners across domains, time, contexts to validate that levels are real and predictive
- **Mechanism Research:** Test what causes transitions; which factors are sufficient vs. necessary?
- **Domain Specificity:** Is LVA domain-agnostic or domain-dependent? Cross-domain transfer?
- **Multi-Level Extension:** Develop and validate team and organizational levels

---

**Section C: Comparison with Existing Frameworks**

*Content:*
| Framework | Dimensions | Focus | LVA Advantage |
|-----------|-----------|-------|---|
| Bloom's Taxonomy | 1 (cognitive complexity) | Knowledge types | LVA adds motivation, transfer, communicability |
| Kirkpatrick | 4 (reaction, learning, transfer, results) | Training evaluation | LVA adds diagnostic specificity; identifies barrier type |
| Competency models | 1–3 (skills, knowledge, behaviors) | Organizational fit | LVA is domain-agnostic; includes opportunity; more granular |

---

**Section D: Limitations and Future Directions**

*Content:*
- Framework is descriptive, not prescriptive (tells you what level learner is at; doesn't automatically say what to do, though intervention research is emerging)
- Measurement tools are still under development (especially higher levels, Opportunity, multi-level)
- Transition mechanisms are underspecified; research needed on causality
- Multi-level extension is new; requires significant validation work

#### VI. CONCLUSION (2–3 pages)

**Summary:**
- LVA fills a critical gap in learning assessment by integrating four dimensions that current frameworks address separately
- Grounded in well-established theoretical traditions; empirically validated for lower levels; emerging for upper levels
- Addresses pressing practical problems: diagnostic accuracy, intervention targeting, equity, transfer

**Call to Research:**
- Longitudinal validation studies
- Mechanism research (what drives transitions?)
- Tool development and validation
- Multi-level extension work
- Domain-specificity studies

**Broader Impact:**
- If widely adopted, LVA could shift educational practice from grades-focused to outcome-focused measurement
- Could improve training ROI by enabling targeted interventions
- Could surface systemic barriers that single-dimension frameworks miss
- Could improve equity by preventing learner-blaming when barriers are structural
