## Executive Summary

The Learning Value Architecture's Capability dimension—tracking whether real ability was durably built across seven sequential levels—has been mapped to four major cognitive science frameworks and assessed against current empirical evidence. Key finding: **the LVA's progression from consolidation through transfer to generativity is exceptionally well-grounded in cognitive science**, with strong support for L1 (Unconsolidated) through L6 (Fluent).

The dimension extends cognitive science in two meaningful ways:
1. **Below Dreyfus:** Addresses pre-skill decay (Unconsolidated), which mainstream frameworks assume away
2. **Beyond Dreyfus:** Characterizes Generative expertise, producing new knowledge and teaching others

However, vulnerabilities exist: strict sequentiality assumptions (L2 and L4 may be independent), the far-transfer ceiling question, and the conflation of creative expertise with teaching ability at L7.

## 1. The Seven Levels of Capability

| Level | Name | Definition | Indicator |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|
| L1 | Unconsolidated | Learning failed to persist; poor memory consolidation | "I definitely learned that... wait, what was that course?" |
| L2 | Inert | Knowledge recalled but never proceduralized; transfer fails | "I passed the exam. Don't ask me to use any of it." |
| L3 | Imitative | Can reproduce demonstrated procedures; no independent variation | "Show me exactly how, and I can reproduce it." |
| L4 | Context-Bound | Operates fluently in familiar contexts; fails in novel ones | "I'm brilliant at this in class. The real world is a different planet." |
| L5 | Effortful | Can transfer across contexts but with deliberation and uncertainty | "I can figure it out, but it's slow and I second-guess myself." |
| L6 | Fluent | Adaptive expertise—intuitive, principled, adjusts without effort | "I can do this, and I can tell you why it works." |
| L7 | Generative | Creates new approaches, teaches others, extends the domain | "I don't just use what I learned — I create new approaches and teach others." |

## 2. Dreyfus Model Alignment

### Mapping LVA to Dreyfus Stages (1980, refined 1986)

| LVA Level | Dreyfus Stage | Characteristics | Mapping Quality |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| L1 Unconsolidated | *Pre-Novice* (not in original model) | Retention failure; learning not consolidation | Extension |
| L2 Inert | Novice | Rules known; context-free application; no contextual discrimination | Aligned |
| L3 Imitative | Advanced Beginner | Recognizes some situational elements; follows demonstrated patterns | Strong alignment |
| L4 Context-Bound | Competent | Can plan and apply in familiar contexts; struggles with novel situations | Aligned |
| L5 Effortful | Proficient | Grasps situations holistically; still deliberates on action choice | Strong alignment |
| L6 Fluent | Expert | Intuitive, principled understanding; adapts without conscious effort | Strong alignment |
| L7 Generative | *Beyond Expert* (not in original model) | Creates new frameworks; extends the domain | Extension |

### Key Insight: Different Axes

**Dreyfus emphasizes:** The shift from rule-following to intuitive perception of wholes, with increasing autonomy of judgment.

**LVA emphasizes:** Context transfer and durability of ability over time. A learner can be fast (Dreyfus "Expert") but not transferable (LVA "Context-Bound").

**Implication:** The frameworks measure different aspects of skill development. Dreyfus' *perception* of situational wholes and the LVA's *transferability* across contexts are related but not identical axes. An expert radiologist (Dreyfus) may fail to teach radiology to novices (a gap in LVA Generativity).

## 3. Bloom's Revised Taxonomy Alignment

### Mapping LVA to Bloom's Cognitive Processes

| LVA Level | Bloom's Primary Dimension | Description | Cognitive Operations |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| L1 Unconsolidated | Below Remember | Memory consolidation failure | None retained |
| L2 Inert | Remember + Understand | Recall facts; surface comprehension; no proceduralization | Factual + Conceptual knowledge; no application |
| L3 Imitative | Apply | Reproduce in controlled, modeled settings | Apply (procedural; limited contexts) |
| L4 Context-Bound | Apply + Analyze | Apply flexibly within domain; analyze domain-specific problems | Apply + Analyze (familiar contexts only) |
| L5 Effortful | Apply + Analyze + Evaluate | Transfer across contexts with deliberation; can critique own approach | Apply + Analyze + Evaluate (cross-context, effortful) |
| L6 Fluent | Analyze + Evaluate + Create | Adaptive expertise; principled flexibility; invents within domain | All operations fluid and integrated |
| L7 Generative | Create | Produces new frameworks, teaches others, extends domain knowledge | Create + transfer knowledge creation to others |

### Key Difference

**Bloom measures:** What cognitive *operations* a learner can perform on content (Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create).

**LVA measures:** The *durability and transferability* of those operations across time and context.

**Example:** A learner might reach Bloom's "Analyze" level on an exam but remain at LVA L2 (Inert) if that analysis never transfers to real-world tasks. Conversely, someone at Bloom's "Apply" might be at LVA L6 (Fluent) if their application is automatic and adaptive.

**Implication:** The frameworks address different questions:
- Bloom: "What can you do with this knowledge?"
- LVA: "Will you still be able to do it, and in what contexts?"

## 4. SOLO Taxonomy Alignment

### Mapping LVA to Biggs & Collis Structural Complexity

| LVA Level | SOLO Level | Structural Complexity | Integration |
|-----------|------------|----------------------|-------------|
| L1–L2 | Prestructural–Unistructural | Isolated pieces; no coherence; connections missed or lost | Minimal; knowledge fragmented |
| L3 | Multistructural | Multiple relevant aspects recognized; not yet integrated | Recognized but not related |
| L4 | Relational | Integrated understanding within a bounded context | Strong internal coherence; limited external transfer |
| L5–L6 | Extended Abstract | Understanding generalizable, transferable, principled; learner grasps the underlying structure | Transfer across novel contexts; flexible application |
| L7 | Beyond Extended Abstract* | Generative; creates new structures and teaches others | Extends existing frameworks; meta-cognitive |

*SOLO's original taxonomy ends at Extended Abstract; L7 is a logical extension.

### Key Insight: Complementary Lenses

SOLO's emphasis on **structural complexity** complements the LVA's emphasis on **durability and transfer**. A learner at SOLO "Relational" might be:
- LVA L4 (Context-Bound) if understanding stays within one context
- LVA L6 (Fluent) if the relational understanding transfers fluidly across novel domains

The key difference: SOLO asks "How complex and integrated is the knowledge structure?" The LVA asks "How robust and transferable is the ability?"

## 5. Memory Consolidation and Spaced Retrieval (L1: Unconsolidated)

### The Forgetting Curve

**Ebbinghaus (1885):** Without intervention, approximately **75% of new learning is forgotten within six days**—the classic forgetting curve. This remains the baseline against which all retention interventions are measured.

**Modern validation:** The forgetting curve has been repeatedly confirmed across domains (Cepeda et al., 2006; Dunlosky et al., 2013). Forgetting is not a failure; it is the default state of memory without consolidation.

### Spaced Retrieval: The Evidence

| Study | Sample | Effect Size | Key Finding |
|-------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Rowland (2014) | 159 studies, meta-analysis | g = 0.50 | Spaced retrieval moderately effective; effect robust across materials & age |
| Dunlosky et al. (2013) | Comprehensive review | Medium–Large | Spaced practice rated "High Utility" across ages & abilities |
| Cepeda et al. (2006) | 317 studies | g = 0.80 (expanding spacing) | Expanding intervals (increasing time between retrievals) more effective than uniform spacing |
| 2025 Neuroscience Review | vmPFC re-encoding | Direct mechanism | Spaced retrieval requires dorsolateral prefrontal cortex reactivation + vmPFC re-encoding to consolidate |

### Consolidation Mechanism (2025 Findings)

Recent neuroimaging confirms that spaced retrieval works because **retrieval itself triggers re-encoding**. The brain doesn't simply store information once; each successful retrieval, followed by a delay, causes the memory to be **re-encoded at a higher level of stability**. This is the neuroscientific basis for why L1 (Unconsolidated) occurs: without retrieval practice, encoding remains shallow.

**Spacing parameters:**
- Intervals should expand over time (e.g., 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks)
- Interleaving different topics strengthens discrimination between concepts
- Retrieval should be effortful but achievable ("desirable difficulty" sweet spot)
- Massed practice (cramming) produces fast initial learning but poor retention and zero transfer

### Implementation Challenge: Curriculum Redesign

Most educational institutions structure curricula as **linear, non-revisiting sequences**: Algebra → Geometry → Trigonometry, with no return to Algebra in later courses. The LVA's intervention—"retention by design, later experiences requiring earlier learning"—demands fundamental curriculum redesign that institutions actively resist.

**Why resistance occurs:**
- Perceived loss of "coverage" (fewer new topics)
- Testing infrastructure optimized for massed assessment (end-of-chapter tests)
- Faculty trained in disciplinary silos, not synoptic design
- Assessment systems that reward knowledge breadth, not durability

**Evidence grade:** STRONG (among the most robust findings in cognitive science)

## 6. Inert Knowledge and Transfer (L2–L4)

### Whitehead's Original Insight (1929)

Alfred North Whitehead observed that most schooling produces "**inert knowledge**": information that learners can recall but never activate in any meaningful context. Nearly a century later, this remains the dominant problem in education.

### Transfer Models and Failure Rates

#### Baldwin & Ford (1988): The Transfer Model

Training → Learning/Retention → Generalization/Maintenance

Three critical predictors of transfer:
1. **Learner characteristics:** cognitive ability, conscientiousness, motivation to learn and transfer
2. **Training design:** relevance, identical elements, problem-solving practice
3. **Work environment:** supervisor support, opportunity to apply, organizational climate

**Critical finding:** All three must align. Strong training in a non-supportive environment produces no transfer.

#### Brinkerhoff's "Training Scrap" (1988–2015)

Systematic evaluation of workplace training showed that **60–90% of training investment produces zero behavior change**. Learners enjoy the training, pass post-training tests, but never apply the skills.

This is inert knowledge at industrial scale.

#### Blume et al. Meta-Analysis (2010)

**Sample:** 89 studies of training transfer
**Strongest predictors of transfer:**
- Cognitive ability (r = +0.30)
- Conscientiousness (r = +0.35)
- Motivation to transfer (r = +0.40)
- Supervisor support (r = +0.50)

**Critical distinction:**
- **Near transfer** (to similar tasks): Moderate effect sizes (d = 0.5–0.8)
- **Far transfer** (to dissimilar tasks): Weak effect sizes (d = 0.1–0.3)

The implication: **Transfer to novel contexts is substantially harder than transfer to similar contexts.** This maps directly to the LVA: moving from L4 (Context-Bound) to L5 (Effortful) to L6 (Fluent) requires progressively larger contextual gaps.

#### 2025 Systematic Review: E-Learning Transfer

**Sample:** 31 studies of workplace e-learning
**Key finding:** Far transfer "hardly ever happens" without deliberate instructional design.

Only 6 of 31 papers provided sufficient methodological detail to assess all transfer dimensions (learner characteristics, training design, work environment, learning outcomes, transfer outcomes). The others stopped at Kirkpatrick Level 2 (learning/test performance), never measuring Level 3 (on-the-job behavior) or Level 4 (business outcomes).

**The measurement problem:** We cannot measure transfer we do not design for. Most evaluations are transfer-blind by design.

### Sequential Nature: L2 → L3 → L4 → L5

| Level | State | Barrier to Next Level | Intervention |
|-------|-------|----------------------|--------------|
| L2 Inert | Recalled but not proceduralized | Proceduralization: practice in use, not passive review | Worked examples, problem solving |
| L3 Imitative | Proceduralized in modeled contexts | Context variation: experience the principle across surface features | Variation theory, diverse examples |
| L4 Context-Bound | Works in familiar contexts | Transfer principle: explicit linking of underlying structure to new contexts | Analogies, principle articulation |
| L5 Effortful | Transfers slowly, with effort | Automaticity: reduce cognitive load through practice in target contexts | Spaced practice, high-variability training |

**Evidence grade:** STRONG

## 7. Cognitive Load Theory and Expertise Reversal (L3–L5)

### Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1988–present)

Intrinsic load (how hard the material is) + Extraneous load (how poorly it's taught) + Germane load (how well it builds schema) = Total cognitive load.

The brain's working memory is limited (7±2 chunks). Effective instruction manages all three loads.

### Expertise Reversal Effect: 2025 Meta-Analysis

| Instructional Method | Effect on Novices | Effect on Experts | Implication |
|----------------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Worked examples | Helps (reduces extraneous load) | Harms (wastes cognitive capacity) | L3 needs models; L5+ needs autonomy |
| Scaffolding | Helps (guides problem-solving) | Harms (constrains problem space) | L3 needs structure; L6 needs freedom |
| Step-by-step breakdown | Helps (chunks content) | Harms (fragments integrated knowledge) | L3 needs sequential; L5+ needs whole |
| Explicit instruction | Helps (teaches rules) | Harms (undermines intuitive perception) | L2 needs rules; L6 needs to transcend them |

**Key implication:** The interventions that move a learner from L2 to L3 actively harm learners at L5–L6. This validates the LVA's insight that each level requires different pedagogical approaches. A one-size-fits-all curriculum fails because instructional methods have expertise-dependent effects.

### Fading Scaffolding

As expertise grows, support must be systematically removed:
1. **Model with full scaffolding** (L3)
2. **Coach, gradually removing support** (L4–L5)
3. **Fade to guided practice** (L5–L6)
4. **Release to autonomous practice** (L6+)

**Evidence:** This sequence is well-established in reading comprehension (Pearson & Gallagher, 1983), mathematics education, and technical skill acquisition.

### Variation Theory: Moving Across Levels

Ference Marton's variation theory states: **Learning requires experiencing the invariant (underlying principle) across multiple variant contexts (surface features).**

**Application to LVA:**
- L3 → L4: Same procedure across different problem types (variation in surface features, same algorithm)
- L4 → L5: Same principle across different domains (variation in domain context, same deep structure)
- L5 → L6: Same goal across different constraints (variation in circumstances, same adaptive principle)

**Evidence grade:** STRONG

## 8. Adaptive Expertise vs. Routine Expertise (L6: Fluent)

### Hatano & Inagaki (1986): Two Routes to Expertise

Expertise develops along two potentially divergent paths:

#### Route 1: Routine Expertise
- **Characteristics:** Speed, accuracy, automaticity in familiar situations
- **Mechanism:** Extensive practice in consistent contexts → pattern recognition → fast, effortless performance
- **Limitation:** No flexibility for novel problems; reliance on automatized procedures
- **Example:** A cashier who processes transactions at speed but cannot solve a non-standard problem (incorrect total, unusual currency)

#### Route 2: Adaptive Expertise
- **Characteristics:** Understands WHY procedures work; can invent new strategies for unfamiliar situations
- **Mechanism:** Varied, challenging practice in safe environments; emphasis on understanding principles over efficiency
- **Advantage:** Flexible, generalizable, can handle novel problems
- **Example:** A mathematician who understands why an algorithm works and can adapt it to new problem types

### Three Conditions for Adaptive Expertise

Research by Hatano & Inagaki, Schwartz, and others identifies three necessary conditions:

1. **Random/Varied Contexts:** Learners encounter problems in unpredictable orders, with varying surface features but consistent underlying principles
2. **Safe Environment:** Rewards are not dependent on immediate performance; errors are learning opportunities, not penalties
3. **Emphasis on Understanding:** Focus on grasping WHY, not just HOW or HOW FAST

### Critical LVA Insight: L6 Should Be Adaptive, Not Routine

**The LVA's "Fluent" (L6) should describe adaptive expertise, not mere automaticity.**

A learner who is fast but inflexible—high accuracy in familiar contexts but helpless in novel ones—is actually:
- Still at L4 (Context-Bound) or L5 (Effortful) in novel contexts
- Possibly misclassified as L6

This is a critical measurement distinction. Speed alone is not fluency; fluency requires adaptability.

### Argyris's Single-Loop vs. Double-Loop Learning

Chris Argyris (1976–2008) distinguished:

- **Single-loop learning:** Adjust actions within existing assumptions. ("The algorithm fails—let me try a different parameter.")
- **Double-loop learning:** Question and revise the assumptions themselves. ("Why do we use this algorithm? What are the assumptions? Can we design a better approach?")

**Mapping to LVA:**
- L4–L5: Single-loop learning (improving performance within familiar contexts)
- L6: Double-loop learning (questioning the principles and adapting approaches)
- L7: Organizational double-loop (extending the domain's assumptions)

**Evidence grade:** STRONG

## 9. Generativity (L7): Research Challenges and Gaps

### The Expert Blind Spot Problem

Nathan & Petrosino (2003) found that **experts are often poor at understanding—and therefore teaching—their own expertise**. The mechanisms of expertise become automatic and opaque. This is the "curse of knowledge" (Camerer, Loewenstein, & Weber, 1989): once you know something deeply, you cannot easily reconstruct what it was like not to know it.

**Implication:** L7 Generativity conflates two potentially independent skills:
1. **Creative/adaptive expertise:** Inventing new approaches within a domain
2. **Pedagogical expertise:** Teaching others; explaining tacit knowledge

Research suggests these are partially independent. A brilliant researcher (strong L7 creative) may be a terrible teacher (weak L7 pedagogical).

### Nonaka's SECI Model: Knowledge Creation at Scale

Ikujiro Nonaka (1994–present) describes how organizations create knowledge through the SECI cycle:

1. **Socialization:** Tacit → Tacit (apprenticeship; learning by doing together)
2. **Externalization:** Tacit → Explicit (articulating intuitive knowledge; creating theories, models)
3. **Combination:** Explicit → Explicit (connecting different explicit knowledge; creating frameworks)
4. **Internalization:** Explicit → Tacit (learning new frameworks; re-embodying knowledge)

The cycle repeats, creating organizational knowledge at scale.

**Mapping to L7:** Generativity at the individual level is the complete SECI cycle. A generative learner doesn't just create knowledge; they can socialize it, articulate it, combine it with existing frameworks, and help others internalize it.

### The Teaching Paradox

Paradoxically, **deep expertise can impair teaching ability**. Bransford, Brown, & Cocking (2000) found that experts often:
- Explain at their own level of abstraction (too advanced for novices)
- Skip steps they now do automatically
- Fail to anticipate novice misconceptions (they've forgotten having them)

This suggests that effective L7 Generative teaching requires a **distinct pedagogical skill set**, not just domain expertise.

### Communities of Inquiry: Generativity as Social

Garrison, Anderson, & Archer (2010) argue that knowledge creation—true generativity—emerges in **communities of inquiry**: groups that collectively identify problems, propose solutions, and refine understanding.

**Implication:** L7 may not be achievable in isolation. It requires a social context that values inquiry, risk-taking, and knowledge creation. An individual isolated from peers may have creative capacity but no outlet for generativity.

**Evidence grade:** MODERATE (well-theorized, empirically supported in specific contexts, but less robust than L1–L6; conflates creative expertise with teaching ability)

## 11. New Research Areas Identified

### 11.1 The Independence Question: Are L2 and L4 Sequential?

The LVA assumes strict sequential progression: L1 → L2 → L3 → L4 → L5 → L6 → L7.

However, consider a learner who has:
- **Procedural skills that don't transfer** (L4: Context-Bound in-class performance, but fails in real-world application)
- **Declarative knowledge that was never proceduralized** (L2: Can recite facts but can't use them)

This learner would have L2 and L4 simultaneously, in different domains or even within the same domain (e.g., "I know the theory, but I can't apply it even in class").

**Research needed:** Longitudinal mapping of learners across multiple domains to test whether progression is strictly sequential or whether learners can be at different levels for different knowledge types.

### 11.2 The Measurement Question: Diagnostic vs. Achievement Assessment

Standard assessments measure **achievement level** (what the learner can do) but not the **type of construction failure** (why they can't do it).

The LVA distinguishes between:
- L1: "Can't recall" (consolidation failure)
- L2: "Can recall but can't use" (proceduralization failure)
- L3: "Can use but only by template" (variation failure)
- L4: "Can use but only in this context" (transfer failure)
- L5: "Can use across contexts but slowly" (automaticity failure)

But standard tests don't diagnose which failure is occurring. A learner who answers incorrectly on a transfer task might be at L2 (never proceduralized), L3 (can't vary), or L4 (can't transfer). The error pattern is the same.

**Research needed:** Develop diagnostic item types that specifically probe each level's failure mechanism:
- L1: Retention test after delay; probe forgetting
- L2: Recall vs. use contrast; probe proceduralization gap
- L3: Variation test; do they transfer to surface-variant examples?
- L4: Domain transfer test; do they apply within vs. outside their specialty?
- L5: Speed & error rate; do they work faster and more accurately as problems scale?

### 11.3 The Far-Transfer Ceiling: L4 as the Realistic Expectation?

The 2025 e-learning review found: **"Far transfer hardly ever happens" without deliberate design.**

This raises an uncomfortable question: **Is L4 (Context-Bound) not a failure, but the realistic ceiling for most learning?**

If most training is designed without explicit transfer support, and far transfer occurs in <10% of cases naturally, then expecting learners to reach L5+ may be unrealistic except in highly designed contexts (e.g., medical education with case-based learning, engineering with design projects, mathematics with problem-solving emphasis).

**Implication:** The LVA may need to recalibrate expectations:
- L4 may be the "normal" outcome of schooling, not a failure
- L5+ should be recognized as requiring substantial instructional redesign
- Institutions at scale may only achieve L4 consistently, with L5+ as exceptional outcomes for high-capability learners in high-support contexts

**Research needed:** Large-scale longitudinal study tracking learners from schooling into workplace/further study, assessing what level is actually achieved for knowledge at different time lags and in different contexts.

### 11.4 The Teaching-Expertise Gap: Should L7 Be Split?

The curse of knowledge suggests that **expert creative ability and expert teaching ability are partially independent skills**.

Current L7 definition requires both:
- Creating new approaches (adaptive expertise)
- Teaching others (pedagogical expertise)

But research suggests these diverge. A learner could be:
- **Strong on creative, weak on teaching:** Brilliant researcher, terrible instructor
- **Strong on teaching, weaker on creative:** Excellent explainer, limited to applying existing knowledge
- **Strong on both:** Rare; requires both domain expertise AND pedagogical training

**Research needed:**
1. Empirical validation of independence: Do creative expertise and teaching effectiveness correlate or diverge?
2. If independent, should L7 be split into two dimensions or retain as a unified construct?
3. What pedagogical interventions develop teaching expertise in domain experts?

### 11.5 The Organizational Parallel: Institutional Learning Capacity

Argyris's single-loop vs. double-loop learning applies not just to individuals but to institutions.

An institution stuck in **single-loop patterns** (e.g., linear curricula, massed testing, coverage emphasis) will produce learners stuck at L3–L4. Such an institution cannot generate L5+ learners because the structures themselves prevent it.

Conversely, institutions designed around **double-loop learning** (revisiting topics, spaced assessment, spiral curricula, emphasis on application) will naturally generate higher-level learners.

**Implication:** The LVA's Capability dimension is not just an individual learning outcome; it's a mirror of institutional design. Capability distributions may be largely determined by institutional structure, not individual effort.

**Research needed:**
1. Comparative institutional study: Map curriculum structure, assessment practices, and achievement distribution across institutions
2. Longitudinal intervention: Redesign one institution for L5+ capability; measure learner distribution change
3. Organizational learning capacity: Develop metrics of institutional "double-loop" capacity; correlate with learner outcomes

## 12. Integration with LVA Framework

### Capability Dimension Within the Broader LVA

The Capability dimension tracks **durability and transferability of ability**. It must interact with other LVA dimensions:

1. **Content Dimension** (what is being learned)
   - Higher capability is easier to achieve in well-structured domains (mathematics) than ill-structured ones (history, ethics)

2. **Emotional/Motivational Dimension** (why and how engagement happens)
   - Motivation to transfer (Baldwin & Ford) is a critical predictor; low motivation caps capability achievement

3. **Social Dimension** (learning in community context)
   - Communities of inquiry (L7) and peer support (L5+) affect capability progression

4. **Temporal Dimension** (learning over time)
   - Spaced retrieval and varied practice only occur if curriculum is designed temporally; linear curricula prevent L1 → L2 progression

### Sequential vs. Parallel Development

The LVA assumes sequential progression within a domain: L1 → L2 → ... → L7.

But learners simultaneously develop capability across multiple domains at different rates. A medical student might be:
- L3 (Imitative) in anatomy (following demos)
- L5 (Effortful) in clinical reasoning (transferring across cases, but slowly)
- L1 (Unconsolidated) in pharmacology (forgot most after first year)
- L6 (Fluent) in communication (adaptive, effortless)

**Capability is not a scalar; it's a profile across domains.**

## 14. Conclusion

The Learning Value Architecture's Capability dimension is **exceptionally well-grounded in cognitive science**. Each level from L1 (Unconsolidated) through L6 (Fluent) has strong empirical support:

- **L1:** Forgetting curve and spaced retrieval are among cognitive science's most robust findings
- **L2–L4:** Transfer research across 100+ years and recent large-scale reviews confirm progression
- **L5–L6:** Automaticity, adaptive expertise, and double-loop learning extensively validated
- **L7:** Generativity is well-theorized but less empirically dense, with conflated constructs

**Key extensions of existing frameworks:**
1. The LVA addresses **pre-skill retention** (L1, Unconsolidated) that Dreyfus and others assume away
2. The LVA explicitly maps **context transfer** as the axis of progression (L2–L6), complementing Dreyfus's perception and Bloom's cognitive operations
3. The LVA characterizes **generativity** (L7) as the pinnacle, extending beyond where most frameworks stop

**Critical vulnerabilities:**
1. **Sequentiality assumption:** L2 (Inert knowledge) and L4 (Context-Bound) may be independent branches rather than sequential stages
2. **Far-transfer ceiling:** If far transfer is rare, L5+ may be unrealistic except in highly designed contexts; L4 may be the functional ceiling for most schooling
3. **Pedagogical conflation:** L7 generativity conflates creative expertise and teaching ability, which research suggests are partially independent
4. **Measurement gap:** Standard assessments don't diagnose *why* a learner failed; diagnostic assessments distinguishing L1–L4 failures are needed

**Implications for practice:**
- Institutional structure determines capability distribution; linear curricula produce L3–L4 learners; spiral, spaced curricula enable L5+
- Expertise reversal effects mean interventions must be matched to learner level; one-size-fits-all instruction fails
- Transfer is not automatic; it requires varied, challenging practice in safe environments

The Capability dimension is a robust, evidence-based characterization of durable learning progression—with clear opportunities for refinement in sequentiality models, far-transfer expectations, and L7 measurement.
