# Science of Learning Research Plan — v2
## Grounding, Validating, and Extending the Learning Value Architecture (LVA)

**Date:** March 14, 2026
**Purpose:** Strategic consulting — a comprehensive research agenda to (1) validate and challenge the LVA framework against established science of learning, (2) identify evidence-based interventions to bridge the Engagement, Capability, and Portability gaps, and (3) extend the framework to account for behavior change, collective/team dynamics, organizational learning, and societal-level transformation.

**Domains:** Higher Education, L&D / Workforce Learning, EdTech, K-12 (where findings transfer)

**Unit of Analysis Expansion (new in v2):** The LVA currently diagnoses *individual learner states*. This research plan extends the inquiry to four nested levels — **Individual → Team/Group → Organization/Institution → Society/System** — because learning problems and their solutions operate at all four levels, and interventions at one level cascade to others.

---

## 1. Executive Framing

The Learning Value Architecture (LVA) proposes that every learning problem maps to exactly one of three dimensions: **Engagement** (is the learner's system turned on?), **Capability** (was real ability durably built?), and **Portability** (does the learner own it as visible professional capital?). Each dimension uses a multi-level diagnostic progression, and together they form a MECE (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive) diagnostic instrument.

This research plan treats the LVA not as settled doctrine but as a working hypothesis — a diagnostic architecture that needs to be pressure-tested, grounded in empirical research, and refined where the evidence demands it.

### Four Research Tracks

**Track A — Theoretical Validation:** Does the LVA's structure align with what cognitive science, motivational psychology, and learning science actually tell us about how learning works?

**Track B — Intervention Evidence:** For each gap the LVA identifies, what does the research literature say actually works? This now includes behavior change models, motivation assessment and fostering strategies, and organizational change frameworks.

**Track C — Framework Extension:** Are there learning problems the LVA doesn't capture? This track now explicitly examines collective/team learning, organizational learning, and societal-level transformation as potential expansion points.

**Track D — Multi-Level Analysis (new):** How do the LVA's three dimensions manifest differently at individual, team, organizational, and societal levels? What does it mean for a *team* to have an Engagement gap, or for an *organization* to have a Portability problem?

## 2. Research Questions

### 2.1 Foundational Questions (Track A — Validation)

**RQ1. Engagement-Motivation Alignment.** Does the LVA's Engagement dimension — progressing from Absent through Disoriented, Compliant, Strategic, Committed, to Aligned — map coherently onto established motivational frameworks (Self-Determination Theory, expectancy-value theory, interest development theory)?

**RQ2. Capability-Construction Sequence.** Does the Capability dimension's neurological construction sequence — Unconsolidated → Inert → Imitative → Context-Bound → Effortful → Fluent → Generative — align with established models of skill acquisition (Dreyfus, ACT-R, expertise research) and memory science (consolidation, retrieval, transfer)?

**RQ3. Portability as Independent Construct.** Is the Portability dimension — Tacit → Institution-Legible → Prompted → Self-Directed → Translated → Integrated — empirically distinguishable from Capability? What evidence exists that "consciousness of capability" and "visibility of capability" are independent constructs from "having capability"?

**RQ4. MECE Stress Test.** Does the MECE claim hold? Can genuine learning problems be found that don't map cleanly to one dimension, or that fall between dimensions in ways the boundary tests can't resolve?

### 2.2 Motivation Deep-Dive (Track A/B — new in v2)

**RQ5. Motivation Assessment.** What validated instruments exist to measure the specific motivational states the LVA's Engagement levels describe? Can the LVA's six Engagement levels be operationalized using existing instruments (MSLQ, AMS, SDT scales) or does a new instrument need to be developed?

**RQ6. Motivation Fostering.** What are the evidence-based strategies for *moving learners up* the Engagement progression? This goes beyond diagnosing the current state to understanding the mechanisms of motivational change — what causes a Compliant learner to become Strategic, or a Strategic learner to become Committed?

**RQ7. Motivation at Scale.** How do motivation interventions differ when applied to individuals vs. cohorts vs. entire programs? The LVA currently prescribes individual-level interventions — what changes when you're trying to shift the engagement profile of 500 learners simultaneously?

### 2.3 Behavior Change Questions (Track B — new in v2)

**RQ8. COM-B Mapping.** How does the LVA map onto Michie et al.'s COM-B model (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation → Behavior)? The LVA's "Capability" dimension partially overlaps with COM-B's "Capability" but the frameworks define it differently. COM-B's "Opportunity" construct — the external factors that make behavior possible — has no LVA equivalent. Is this a gap?

**RQ9. Stages of Change Integration.** Does Prochaska's Transtheoretical Model (Precontemplation → Contemplation → Preparation → Action → Maintenance) illuminate anything about how learners move through the LVA's Engagement levels? The TTM was designed for behavior change, not learning engagement, but the parallel structure is suggestive.

**RQ10. Nudge Architecture.** Can the LVA's intervention recommendations be strengthened by incorporating nudge theory and choice architecture? The LVA prescribes "introduce choice and consequence" at Engagement L3 — this aligns with nudge theory but could be much more specific about how to design choice environments.

### 2.4 Collective and Social Dimensions (Track C/D — new in v2)

**RQ11. Team-Level Learning Gaps.** Can the LVA be applied to teams and groups, not just individuals? Fisher's research on collective dynamics (The Collective Edge) argues that group performance is not the sum of individual performance — groups have emergent properties. Does a team have its own Engagement, Capability, and Portability profile that's distinct from the aggregate of its members?

**RQ12. Communities of Practice.** How does Wenger's communities of practice framework interact with the LVA? CoP theory suggests learning is inherently social and situated — the learner develops capability *through* participation in a community, not as an individual who later joins one. Does this challenge the LVA's individual-learner unit of analysis?

**RQ13. Shared Mental Models and Group Cognition.** When the LVA diagnoses Capability at the team level, what constitutes "team capability"? Research on shared mental models suggests that team performance depends on members developing mutually compatible expectations — a form of collective capability that transcends individual skill.

**RQ14. Collective Intelligence.** How does collective intelligence research (Woolley et al.'s "c-factor," Brookings 2024 CI roundtable findings) inform the LVA? If groups have a measurable collective intelligence independent of individual IQ, the LVA may need a construct for this.

### 2.5 Organizational Learning (Track C/D — new in v2)

**RQ15. Learning Organization Dimensions.** How does the LVA map onto Senge's five disciplines (systems thinking, personal mastery, mental models, shared vision, team learning)? Senge's framework operates at the organizational level while the LVA operates at the learner level — can these be bridged?

**RQ16. Single-Loop vs. Double-Loop Learning.** Argyris & Schön's distinction between single-loop learning (adjusting actions within existing assumptions) and double-loop learning (questioning the assumptions themselves) maps provocatively onto the LVA. Is Capability L3 (Imitative) a single-loop state, while L6 (Fluent/Adaptive) requires double-loop learning? Can the LVA's diagnostic help organizations identify whether they're stuck in single-loop patterns?

**RQ17. Institutional Readiness.** The LVA currently diagnoses learner states and prescribes interventions, but many interventions fail because the institution can't execute them. Should the LVA add an institutional readiness diagnostic? A 2025 narrative review of learning organizations identified five interdependent dimensions — organizational culture, knowledge management, organizational memory, continuous feedback, and dynamic capabilities — that determine institutional capacity for learning transformation.

**RQ18. Kotter and Organizational Change.** How does Kotter's 8-step change model map onto the process of moving an institution from low LVA scores to high ones? The LVA tells you *what's wrong* — Kotter tells you *how to change it* at the organizational level. Can these be integrated into a unified change methodology?

### 2.6 Societal-Level Questions (Track D — new in v2)

**RQ19. Systems-Level Learning.** Can the LVA's three dimensions be applied to societal-level learning challenges — e.g., workforce reskilling at scale, national education reform, or collective response to technological disruption? What changes when the "learner" is a population rather than an individual?

**RQ20. Social Movements and Learning Transformation.** Research on social movements (ASA CBSM section, Gamson's Strategy of Social Protest) shows that collective behavior change follows different dynamics than individual change. A society can be "Engaged" with a learning challenge (public discourse, policy attention) without building "Capability" (actual systemic change) or "Portability" (sustained, visible outcomes). Does this analogy hold, and is it useful?

**RQ21. Keller's "Five-Fold" Commitment Principle.** Scott Keller's research (McKinsey) on team motivation demonstrates that when individuals write their own "lottery ticket" — articulating how a change applies to them personally — commitment increases roughly five-fold. This principle connects directly to the LVA's Engagement L3-L4 transition (Compliant → Strategic). Can this be generalized into a design principle across all three LVA dimensions?

### 2.7 Framework Extension Questions (Track C)

**RQ22. Affect and Belonging.** Does the LVA adequately account for *affect* and *belonging* as distinct from engagement? Pekrun's control-value theory, stereotype threat research, and belonging uncertainty research suggest these may constitute independent failure modes.

**RQ23. AI Augmentation Layer.** How should the LVA account for AI-mediated learning environments where the boundaries between learner capability and tool capability are blurred? When a learner performs fluently with AI assistance but cannot perform without it, where does this map?

**RQ24. The "Opportunity" Gap.** COM-B's "Opportunity" construct — the external environmental factors that enable or constrain behavior — has no explicit LVA equivalent. A learner may be Engaged, Capable, and own their Portability, but if the environment doesn't provide opportunity to apply or demonstrate, the learning has no outlet. Is this a fourth dimension or a contextual modifier?

## 3. Literature Review Strategy

### 3.1 Engagement Dimension — Theoretical Grounding

**Self-Determination Theory (SDT).** The LVA's Engagement levels track a progression similar to SDT's motivation continuum from amotivation through external regulation, introjected regulation, identified regulation, integrated regulation to intrinsic motivation — but use different cut-points. The 2024 meta-analysis of SDT-based interventions in education found significant positive effects on autonomous motivation across contexts. A 2025 practical tool for supporting medical learners' motivation (PMC, 2025) operationalizes SDT into actionable strategies, demonstrating how autonomy, competence, and relatedness can be deliberately fostered — directly relevant to the LVA's Engagement interventions. A 2025 Applied Psychology paper on "need crafting" (Olafsen, 2025) shows employees can self-initiate changes to optimize their work functioning based on basic psychological needs — suggesting the LVA's interventions could be designed for learner self-application, not just top-down delivery.

*Key gap:* The LVA's "Strategic" level (L4) maps to identified regulation in SDT. But SDT distinguishes identified from integrated regulation more precisely than the LVA does. The jump from Strategic (L4) to Committed (L5) may conflate two distinct motivational transitions.

**Interest Development Theory.** Hidi & Renninger's four-phase model (triggered situational interest → maintained situational interest → emerging individual interest → well-developed individual interest) offers a complementary lens. The LVA's early Engagement levels correspond to pre-interest or failed triggering states.

*Key gap:* The LVA doesn't explicitly address *situational* vs. *individual* interest. A learner at Engagement L4 (Strategic) might have strong individual interest in some topics and zero interest in others within the same program.

**Expectancy-Value Theory.** Eccles & Wigfield's model separates expectancy (can I succeed?) from value (do I want to?). The LVA's Engagement dimension primarily tracks value progression and motivation source, but the expectancy side — learner confidence and perceived competence — is largely absent. This is a significant extension point.

**Flow Theory.** Csikszentmihalyi's concept of flow maps most closely to the LVA's Aligned state (Engagement L6). The 2025 research on flow experience in self-directed e-learning confirms flow as a mediator between learning environment design and engagement.

**Motivation Assessment Instruments.** The research reveals a mature ecosystem of validated instruments that could operationalize the LVA's Engagement levels: the MSLQ (Motivated Strategies for Learning Questionnaire) — most widely used SRL instrument in higher education, recently revalidated in 2025 among clinical clerkship students; the Academic Motivation Scale (AMS) — 28 items assessing intrinsic motivation, extrinsic motivation, and amotivation aligned with SDT; the DSML (Diversity of Strategies for Motivation in Learning) — a newer 24-item quick-assessment tool that could serve as an early alert system. A key research task is to map these instruments' subscales onto the LVA's six Engagement levels and determine whether existing instruments can discriminate between LVA levels or whether a new instrument is needed.

### 3.2 Capability Dimension — Theoretical Grounding

**Memory Consolidation and Spaced Retrieval.** The Capability L1 (Unconsolidated) problem is one of the best-studied phenomena in cognitive science. A 2025 study in PMC provided new neuroscience evidence that spaced learning benefits are predicted by vmPFC re-encoding processes. Meta-analytic evidence shows effect sizes of g = 0.50 for retrieval practice vs. re-studying (Rowland, 2014, 159 comparisons). A 2025 comprehensive review confirmed spaced retrieval simultaneously optimizes encoding and consolidation. The LVA's proposed intervention is strongly supported, but specific design parameters (optimal spacing intervals, interleaving effects, retrieval difficulty calibration) need specification.

**Inert Knowledge and Transfer.** The 2025 European Journal of Work and Organizational Psychology publication on learning and transfer in organizations identifies three critical factors: intervention design, workplace environment, and learner characteristics. A systematic review of workplace e-learning transfer (2025) found that far transfer "hardly ever happens" without deliberate design — and only 6 of 31 papers provided sufficient information to assess all dimensions of transfer. The measurement problem may be as significant as the transfer problem itself.

*Key gap:* The LVA collapses "inert knowledge" and "context-bound competence" into sequential levels (L2 and L4), treating them as stages in a single progression. The research suggests these can be independent failure modes.

**Cognitive Load Theory and Expertise Reversal.** A 2025 meta-analysis confirmed that instructional support that helps novices actively hurts experts — scaffolding essential at one capability level becomes counterproductive at the next. This strongly supports the LVA's core insight that each level requires a different intervention.

**Deliberate Practice and Expert Performance.** Ericsson's framework provides the backbone for Capability L5-L7. The progression from effortful application through fluency to generativity mirrors the shift from conscious, controlled processing to automatized performance to productive expertise.

*Key gap:* The LVA's Generative level (L7) may conflate adaptive expertise (Hatano & Inagaki) and teaching ability. The research on expert blind spots suggests these can diverge.

**Dreyfus Model Comparison.** The five-stage model (Novice → Advanced Beginner → Competent → Proficient → Expert) aligns with but doesn't fully map to the LVA's Capability progression. The LVA adds Unconsolidated (pre-Dreyfus) and Generative (post-Dreyfus), which is a meaningful extension.

### 3.3 Portability Dimension — Theoretical Grounding

**Metacognition and Self-Regulated Learning.** Portability L1 (Tacit) is fundamentally a metacognitive failure. A 2025 Frontiers in Education paper proposes an AI-powered "cognitive mirror" framework for metacognitive development. The research shows metacognitive skills are teachable and structured reflection significantly improves articulation ability.

*Key gap:* The SRL literature argues metacognition is constitutive of capability — this challenges the MECE claim.

**ePortfolios and Competency Evidence.** A 2024 Frontiers in Medicine study demonstrated ePortfolios effectively support longitudinal assessment when tied to competency frameworks. A 2024 literature review identified six implementation steps. The consistent finding: ePortfolios work when genuinely student-owned but often degrade into compliance exercises — illustrating the LVA's L2 vs. L4 distinction.

**Micro-Credentials and Digital Badges.** 320.4 million badges issued in 2025 (1EdTech) represent a massive real-world experiment in Portability. But badge inflation may undermine signal value. Question: can Portability be achieved without the learner developing genuine self-awareness (L1)?

**Professional Identity Formation.** Portability L6 (Integrated) maps onto medical education's professional identity formation research. Cruess et al.'s framework describes an integration process where professional knowledge becomes indistinguishable from self-concept. But this process is non-linear — it involves crises, regressions, and identity conflicts.

### 3.4 Behavior Change Frameworks — New Review Area (v2)

**COM-B Model (Michie et al.).** The Capability-Opportunity-Motivation-Behavior model provides a comprehensive behavior change framework. COM-B's "Capability" maps partially to the LVA's Capability dimension, but COM-B distinguishes physical and psychological capability. COM-B's "Motivation" overlaps with the LVA's Engagement. The critical gap: COM-B's "Opportunity" — divided into physical opportunity (environment, resources, time) and social opportunity (cultural norms, social cues) — has no LVA equivalent. The Behaviour Change Wheel built on COM-B identifies nine intervention functions (education, persuasion, incentivisation, coercion, training, restriction, environmental restructuring, modelling, enablement) and seven policy categories. This provides a much more granular intervention taxonomy than the LVA currently offers.

**Transtheoretical Model (Prochaska).** The five stages of change (Precontemplation → Contemplation → Preparation → Action → Maintenance) may illuminate how learners move through the LVA's Engagement levels. Precontemplation ≈ Absent (L1), Contemplation ≈ Disoriented/Compliant (L2-L3), Preparation ≈ Strategic (L4), Action ≈ Committed (L5), Maintenance ≈ Aligned (L6). The TTM's concept of "decisional balance" — weighing pros and cons of change — is absent from the LVA but may explain the Strategic-to-Committed transition.

**Kamarova et al. (2025) Integration.** A 2024-2025 study published in the Journal of Organizational Behavior integrated behavior change and organizational change literatures, identifying 21 psychological mechanisms and distilling them to three non-overlapping mechanisms essential to adoption and maintenance of organizational change. This MECE approach to change mechanisms parallels the LVA's MECE approach to learning problems — potential for integration.

**Kotter's 8-Step Change Model.** Applied to higher education (Guiding change in higher education, Studies in Higher Education), Kotter's sequential model provides an organizational change overlay for LVA implementation. The eight steps (create urgency, form coalition, create vision, communicate, empower, create quick wins, build on change, anchor in culture) describe what institutions need to do to *implement* LVA-guided interventions, not just diagnose problems.

### 3.5 Collective and Organizational Learning — New Review Area (v2)

**Fisher's Collective Edge Research.** Colin M. Fisher (UCL School of Management) draws on his background as a jazz musician and organizational researcher to identify the hidden processes of helping groups and teams in situations requiring creativity, improvisation, and complex decision-making. Key concepts for the LVA: (1) Groups have emergent properties — team capability ≠ sum of individual capabilities. (2) Competition can motivate without dysfunction if properly channeled — relevant to Engagement. (3) The myth of the lone genius — almost no innovation comes from solitary individuals, suggesting the LVA's individual-learner focus may miss a critical dimension of how capability and generativity (L7) actually develop. (4) Common group pitfalls (conformity, polarization, prejudice, conflict) represent *collective* engagement and capability failures that don't reduce to individual diagnoses.

**Keller's Five-Fold Commitment Principle (McKinsey).** The lottery ticket experiment shows that when individuals choose for themselves, commitment increases roughly five-fold. The practical applications — cascading story-writing at a bank, IBM's ValuesJam with 50,000+ employees, Emerson Electric's four engagement questions — demonstrate that Engagement is not just an individual psychological state but an organizational design challenge. This has direct implications for how the LVA's Engagement interventions should be designed: not "tell learners why this matters" but "enable learners to discover why this matters to *them*."

**Senge's Five Disciplines.** Systems thinking, personal mastery, mental models, shared vision, and team learning. The LVA maps to personal mastery (Capability) and partially to shared vision (Engagement at organizational level), but mental models (the unconscious assumptions that constrain learning), systems thinking (seeing patterns and interdependencies), and team learning (group-level capability development) are largely absent. Senge's insight that organizational learning disabilities are *systemic* — they emerge from the structure, not from individual deficiencies — challenges the LVA's individual-learner diagnostic approach.

**Argyris & Schön's Double-Loop Learning.** Single-loop learning (adjusting within existing frames) maps to LVA Capability L3-L4. Double-loop learning (questioning the frames themselves) maps to L5-L7. The LVA doesn't name this distinction, but it's operating within the progression. Making it explicit would strengthen the framework's utility for organizational consultants who already use Argyris's language.

**Wenger's Communities of Practice.** CoP theory argues that learning is situated participation in social practice, not individual knowledge acquisition. The three components of a CoP — mutual engagement, joint enterprise, shared repertoire — suggest a social fabric that the LVA's individual diagnostic doesn't capture. A learner embedded in a strong CoP may advance faster through all three LVA dimensions; a learner isolated from community may stall regardless of intervention quality.

**Collective Intelligence Research.** Woolley et al.'s discovery of a "c-factor" — a measurable collective intelligence independent of individual IQ, predicted by social perceptiveness and equal conversational turn-taking — suggests that team-level capability is a real phenomenon, not just a metaphor. The 2024 Brookings roundtable on CI for sustainable development extended this to societal-level problem-solving. Shared mental models (SMMs) research shows that team performance depends on members developing mutually compatible expectations for shared action.

**Organizational Learning and Innovation (2024-2025).** A 2025 narrative review in MDPI Systems identified five interdependent dimensions of learning organizations: organizational culture, knowledge management, organizational memory, continuous feedback, and dynamic capabilities. A 2024 SAGE study showed learning organizations exhibit higher levels of innovation when mediated by employee resilience and work engagement — connecting organizational learning to individual engagement (LVA Dimension 1). McKinsey's 2025 Learning Perspective report emphasizes breaking down silos and creating systems for dynamic skill development — language that maps to the LVA's vision but at an organizational rather than individual level.

### 3.6 Cross-Domain Review Strategy

**Higher Education:** Student engagement (NSSE data and critiques), learning outcomes assessment (AAC&U VALUE rubrics, DQP framework), experiential learning (Kolb, reflection models), competency-based education (WGU, Purdue Global, SNHU), and the "assessment crisis."

**L&D / Workforce Learning:** Training transfer (Baldwin & Ford, Grossman & Salas), Kirkpatrick evaluation model and limitations, 70-20-10 model evidence, learning in the flow of work, organizational learning culture diagnostics. A 2025 Frontiers in Organizational Psychology study on workplace learning during onboarding integrates formal, informal, and self-regulated learning — demonstrating that the LVA's dimensions play out differently in workplace contexts.

**EdTech:** Adaptive learning effectiveness (2025 systematic review of 142 studies), AI-powered tutoring systems, learning analytics, and the "personalization paradox" — personalized systems may inadvertently create Context-Bound capability (L4).

**K-12 (transferable findings):** Formative assessment (Black & Wiliam), growth mindset (Dweck, with replication concerns), project-based learning transfer, and CASEL social-emotional learning frameworks.

## 4. Methodology

*Note: This is a research plan designed to be executed primarily through deep AI-assisted research — web research, literature synthesis, framework analysis, and simulated expert perspective-taking — rather than traditional human fieldwork. "Expert interviews" are replaced by deep research into what domain experts have published, said, and argued, then adopting their perspective to stress-test the LVA. Empirical validation studies are described as designs that could be proposed to partners, with the analytical frameworks built out in advance.*

### 4.1 Deep Literature Research Protocol

**Search strategy:** Systematic web research across academic databases (via Google Scholar, open-access journals, PubMed Central, ERIC, SSRN), institutional reports (OECD, WEF, McKinsey, Lumina Foundation, 1EdTech), and practitioner literature (HBR, ATD, EDUCAUSE, Chronicle of Higher Ed).

**Search terms by dimension and level:**

For Engagement: ("student engagement" OR "learner motivation" OR "self-determination theory" OR "intrinsic motivation" OR "interest development" OR "expectancy-value" OR "flow" OR "autonomy support") AND ("higher education" OR "workforce learning" OR "corporate training" OR "edtech" OR "adaptive learning")

For Capability: ("learning transfer" OR "skill acquisition" OR "retrieval practice" OR "spaced repetition" OR "desirable difficulties" OR "cognitive load" OR "deliberate practice" OR "expertise development" OR "procedural knowledge" OR "context variation" OR "adaptive expertise") AND ("higher education" OR "workplace" OR "instructional design" OR "edtech")

For Portability: ("metacognition" OR "self-regulated learning" OR "eportfolio" OR "micro-credentials" OR "competency-based" OR "professional identity" OR "self-articulation" OR "learning visibility" OR "credential portability" OR "learner agency") AND ("higher education" OR "workforce" OR "employability")

For Behavior Change: ("COM-B" OR "behaviour change wheel" OR "transtheoretical model" OR "stages of change" OR "nudge theory" OR "choice architecture" OR "organizational change") AND ("learning" OR "education" OR "training" OR "workforce development")

For Collective/Organizational Learning: ("collective intelligence" OR "team learning" OR "communities of practice" OR "shared mental models" OR "organizational learning" OR "learning organization" OR "double-loop learning" OR "systems thinking") AND ("higher education" OR "workplace" OR "L&D" OR "capability development")

For Motivation Assessment: ("MSLQ" OR "academic motivation scale" OR "motivation assessment" OR "engagement measurement" OR "self-determination" OR "intrinsic motivation scale") AND ("validation" OR "psychometric" OR "measurement" OR "instrument")

**Inclusion priority:** Meta-analyses and systematic reviews first, then landmark empirical studies, then recent (2024-2026) studies that extend or challenge established findings. Seminal theoretical works regardless of date. Grey literature from credible institutional sources.

### 4.3 Simulated Expert Perspectives

Instead of traditional interviews, adopt the published positions and analytical frameworks of leading researchers and practitioners in each domain, then use those perspectives to stress-test the LVA:

**Higher Ed Perspective:** Draw on the published work of assessment researchers (e.g., Kuh/NSSE, AAC&U VALUE rubric developers), CBE program designers (WGU, SNHU leaders), and experiential learning scholars. Research what these experts have argued about learning outcomes, engagement, and evidence of learning. Then: apply their critiques to the LVA. Where would an assessment director say the LVA is naive? Where would a CBE designer say it's redundant with what they already do?

**L&D / Workforce Perspective:** Draw on published work of training transfer researchers (Baldwin & Ford, Grossman & Salas, Brinkerhoff), CLOs from major organizations (McKinsey L&D reports, ATD research), and organizational development consultants. Research what they say about training effectiveness and ROI. Then: apply their frameworks to the LVA. How does the E-C-P separation compare to Kirkpatrick in a CLO's hands?

**EdTech Perspective:** Draw on published work of adaptive learning researchers, AI tutoring system evaluators, and learning analytics scholars. Then: identify where the LVA helps explain what technology should be doing and where it misses technology-specific learning phenomena.

**Organizational Change Perspective:** Draw on Kotter, Senge, Argyris, Kamarova et al. (2025), and behavioral science practitioners. Then: stress-test the LVA from the perspective of someone who has to implement change at the institutional level, not just diagnose individual learners.

**Behavior Change Perspective:** Draw on Michie (COM-B/BCW), Prochaska (TTM), and nudge theory practitioners. Then: map the LVA's interventions against the BCW's nine intervention functions and identify where the LVA is under-specified.

### 4.4 Validation Study Designs (to propose to partners)

Develop the full analytical framework for these studies so they're ready to execute when institutional partners are available:

**Study Design 1 — Discriminant Validity:** 100 real learning scenarios, rated by 3+ independent raters against LVA dimensions and levels. Target: Cohen's kappa > 0.75. Pre-identify the MECE stress-point scenarios.

**Study Design 2 — Predictive Validity:** Retrospective analysis design — match historical interventions at partner institutions to LVA dimensions and test whether dimension-matched interventions outperformed mismatched ones.

**Study Design 3 — Cross-Domain Portability:** Identical diagnostic protocol applied in higher ed, L&D, and edtech settings, comparing reliability and face validity.

**Study Design 4 — Multi-Level Application:** Team-level LVA diagnostic applied to intact teams, assessing whether team profiles differ from aggregated individual profiles.

**Study Design 5 — Motivation Instrument Mapping:** MSLQ, AMS, and custom LVA Engagement Scale administered to same sample, testing whether LVA levels can be discriminated by existing instruments.

## 5. Dimension-by-Dimension Research Priorities

### 5.1 Engagement — Specific Research Priorities

| LVA Level | Research Priority | Key Question | Behavior Change Lens |
|---|---|---|---|
| L1 Absent | Relevance perception; culturally responsive pedagogy; first-gen student engagement; structural barriers | Is "relevance mapping" sufficient, or does the LVA need to account for structural barriers (financial, familial, systemic) that prevent engagement regardless of relevance? | COM-B: Is this a *motivation* failure or an *opportunity* failure? If the learner lacks physical opportunity (access, time, resources), no amount of relevance mapping will help. |
| L2 Disoriented | Cognitive orientation; advance organizers (Ausubel); wayfinding in curricula | Does disorientation stem from poor design or insufficient prior knowledge? If the latter, this may be a Capability problem misdiagnosed as Engagement. | TTM: This resembles "Contemplation" — the learner is aware but can't find a path forward. |
| L3 Compliant | SDT interventions for external → identified regulation; autonomy-supportive teaching; choice architecture; nudge theory | The L&D literature on compliance training shows some compliance is appropriate. Distinguish "compliance as dysfunction" from "compliance as reasonable response to mandatory training." | Keller principle: Letting learners "write their own lottery ticket" could be the mechanism that moves Compliant → Strategic. |
| L4 Strategic | Utility value interventions (Hulleman); cost-benefit analysis of effort allocation; gaming the system research | Strategic engagement may be *rational* in poorly designed systems. Is the problem the learner's motivation or the system's incentive structure? | COM-B: "Reflective motivation" — the learner has calculated the ROI. The system needs to change the calculation. |
| L5 Committed | Community of practice theory (Wenger); student co-creation; belonging research (Walton & Cohen); Keller's five-fold commitment | The jump from Strategic to Committed may require a *belonging* mechanism the LVA doesn't name. | Fisher: Group dynamics may be the mechanism — commitment often emerges from meaningful group participation, not individual motivation work. |

## 6. Framework Extension Analysis

### 6.1 Candidate Dimension: Affect / Belonging

Pekrun's control-value theory identifies learning emotions (enjoyment, hope, pride, anger, anxiety, shame, boredom) that predict outcomes independent of motivation. Walton & Cohen's belonging uncertainty research demonstrates belonging is a causal factor. The CASEL framework adds five competencies partially absent from the LVA.

**Research question:** Are there learning problems that are clearly *affective* (not motivational) and *social* (not individual engagement) that the LVA's Engagement dimension fails to capture? If a learner is motivated and capable but anxious or alienated, is that Engagement L5 with an affect problem, or a distinct dimension?

**Recommendation:** Investigate splitting Engagement into *Motivation* (current dimension) and *Conditions* (affect, belonging, safety, psychological readiness).

### 6.2 Candidate Dimension: Opportunity / Environmental Enablement

COM-B's "Opportunity" construct — physical and social environmental factors — represents a genuine gap in the LVA. A learner can be Engaged (motivated), Capable (skilled), and Portable (self-aware and articulate) but unable to apply any of it because the environment doesn't provide the physical resources, social support, or structural access needed.

**Research question:** Should the LVA add an "Opportunity" or "Environmental Enablement" dimension? Or is this always an institutional/systemic context that modifies but doesn't constitute a learner-level diagnosis?

### 6.3 Candidate Dimension: Collective / Social Fabric

The convergence of Fisher (collective dynamics), Wenger (communities of practice), Woolley (collective intelligence), and Senge (team learning) suggests that learning has an irreducibly social dimension that the LVA's individual diagnostic doesn't capture. A team or community can have its own Engagement, Capability, and Portability profile — and that profile is not the average of its members' profiles.

**Research question:** Should the LVA be expanded from a 3-dimension individual diagnostic to a 3×4 matrix (three dimensions × four levels: individual, team, organization, society)? Or should it add a fourth "Collective" dimension that captures the social fabric requirements for learning?

### 6.4 Candidate Layer: AI Augmentation

AI tutors, writing assistants, and co-pilots create a new category of learning problem: apparent capability that is tool-dependent. The 2024-2025 research on AI in education shows adaptive systems improve performance metrics but evidence on transfer to unassisted contexts is thin.

**Research question:** Does the LVA need to distinguish *unassisted capability* from *augmented capability*? Or should AI augmentation be treated as a special case of Context-Bound (L4) — capability that only works in the AI-assisted context?

## 7. Emergent Questions

*Questions that have surfaced during the research process and deserve investigation. Organized by theme.*

### Questions About the LVA's Unit of Analysis

**Q1.** The LVA diagnoses individual learner states, but most learning happens in social contexts. When a learner's Engagement improves because they joined a study group or formed a relationship with a mentor, is that an Engagement intervention or a *social context* intervention? The LVA may need to distinguish between interventions that target the learner and interventions that target the learner's environment.

**Q2.** Fisher's Collective Edge argues that group performance has emergent properties not reducible to individual performance. If a team of individually-Fluent (Capability L6) learners performs poorly together due to coordination failures, where does this map in the LVA? It's not an individual Capability problem. Is it an Engagement problem (they're not committed to the team), a Portability problem (they can't make their capability visible to each other), or something outside the framework entirely?

**Q3.** Keller's five-fold commitment principle suggests that the *mechanism* for moving from Compliant (E3) to Committed (E5) is learner agency — "writing your own lottery ticket." If this is true, the LVA's Engagement interventions should be redesigned so that learners design their own Engagement rather than having it designed for them. Is this a design principle or a naivety — can it work in mandatory compliance training contexts?

### Questions About Sequential Progression

**Q4.** The LVA assumes each level presupposes the previous. But the professional identity literature describes crises and regressions — a learner at Portability L6 (Integrated) can experience a career disruption that drops them back to L2. Should the LVA model regression paths, not just progression?

**Q5.** A learner can be at different Engagement levels for different subjects within the same program (Strategic about career-relevant courses, Absent for required gen-eds). The LVA diagnoses "a learner in an experience" — but how granular is "an experience"? A program? A course? A session? A task?

**Q6.** Can Portability advance independently of Capability? An articulate person with shallow knowledge (high Portability, low Capability) is common in professional settings. The LVA assumes Portability requires Capability L3+ — but in practice, people can be excellent self-presenters of limited capability. Is this a framework failure or a feature (the LVA identifies this as a misdiagnosis waiting to happen)?

### Questions About Behavior Change Mechanisms

**Q7.** COM-B separates "Opportunity" from "Capability" and "Motivation." The LVA has no Opportunity construct. Is this because the LVA is a *learner* diagnostic (the learner doesn't have an Opportunity score — the environment does), or is it a genuine blind spot? If a learner is motivated and capable but the organization provides no context for application, the LVA would diagnose this as Capability L4 (Context-Bound) — but the problem is environmental, not capability-related.

**Q8.** The Behaviour Change Wheel identifies nine intervention functions: education, persuasion, incentivisation, coercion, training, restriction, environmental restructuring, modelling, enablement. The LVA's interventions are much less granular — each level gets a single intervention recommendation. Should the LVA adopt BCW's intervention taxonomy to provide more precise prescriptions?

**Q9.** Prochaska's "decisional balance" (weighing pros and cons of change) may explain the Strategic-to-Committed transition in Engagement. A learner at L4 has done the cost-benefit analysis and decided to engage strategically. To move to L5, the decisional balance needs to shift so that deep engagement appears more valuable than strategic optimization. What tips this balance? Belonging? Purpose discovery? Peer modeling?

### Questions About Organizational and Systemic Application

**Q10.** Senge's "learning disabilities" — organizational patterns that prevent learning (like "the enemy is out there" or "the fixation on events") — describe institutional-level Engagement failures that don't map to any individual learner's state. Should the LVA develop an institutional diagnostic alongside its learner diagnostic?

**Q11.** Argyris's "defensive routines" — organizational behaviors that protect against embarrassment or threat but prevent learning — may explain why institutions resist implementing LVA-recommended interventions. An institution can *diagnose* correctly (the LVA is good at this) but be systematically unable to *act* on the diagnosis because of defensive routines. How should the LVA account for this?

**Q12.** If the LVA is applied to a society's workforce reskilling challenge (e.g., a national response to AI-driven job displacement), what does "Engagement" mean at the societal level? Public discourse and policy attention? Individual motivation aggregated? Something else entirely? The social movements literature suggests collective engagement follows different dynamics than individual engagement — threshold effects, contagion, critical mass.

### Questions About Measurement

**Q13.** The MSLQ and AMS are validated for measuring motivational constructs in educational settings. But the LVA's Engagement levels describe a different taxonomy than either instrument — can existing instruments discriminate between, say, a Compliant (L3) and a Strategic (L4) learner? Or does the LVA need its own measurement tool?

**Q14.** How should "Capability" be measured when the LVA claims to diagnose the *type of construction failure*, not just the *level of achievement*? Standard assessments measure what a learner can do; the LVA claims to diagnose *why they can't do more*. This requires a different kind of assessment — one that tests not just performance but the nature of the performance (imitative vs. principled, context-bound vs. transferring).

**Q15.** Portability L1 (Tacit) is a metacognitive problem — the learner can't articulate what they know. How do you measure something the learner can't articulate? The assessment must infer metacognitive awareness from behavioral indicators, not self-report. What methods exist for this?

### Questions About AI and Technology

**Q16.** Adaptive learning systems personalize the difficulty, pacing, and content of learning to the individual — but this personalization may create Context-Bound capability (L4). The learner performs well inside the adaptive system but can't perform outside it. Is this an inherent risk of personalization, or a design failure that can be corrected?

**Q17.** AI-powered "cognitive mirror" tools (Frontiers in Education, 2025) could scaffold Portability L1-L3 by helping learners articulate their own capabilities. But does AI-assisted articulation count as "self-directed" (L4) or as "prompted" (L3)? If a learner needs an AI to help them articulate their capability, they haven't achieved genuine self-directed demonstration.

## 9. Key Risks and Mitigation

**Risk 1: The MECE claim doesn't hold.** If learning problems genuinely span dimensions, the core architecture is challenged.
*Mitigation:* The framework acknowledges "interaction effects." If MECE fails at the problem level, it may still hold at the *intervention* level.

**Risk 2: The sequential progression assumption is wrong.** If learners skip levels or regress, the diagnostic instrument becomes unreliable.
*Mitigation:* Test explicitly in Study 1. If non-sequential patterns are common, modify to use *states* rather than *stages*.

**Risk 3: The Portability dimension lacks discriminant validity.** If practitioners can't reliably distinguish Portability from Capability, the three-dimensional structure may not improve on simpler models.
*Mitigation:* Study 1 scenarios specifically test the Capability-Portability boundary.

**Risk 4: Domain-specific variation undermines universality.** If the LVA works in higher ed but not L&D, domain-specific adaptations are needed.
*Mitigation:* Study 3 detects this.

**Risk 5 (new in v2): Multi-level extension is premature.** Applying the LVA to teams and organizations before the individual-level framework is validated could produce confused results.
*Mitigation:* Phase multi-level work to begin after individual-level validation in Phase 3. Study 4 is exploratory, not confirmatory.

**Risk 6 (new in v2): Behavior change integration dilutes the framework.** Adding COM-B, BCW, TTM, and Kotter's model could make the LVA overly complex for practitioners.
*Mitigation:* Position behavior change frameworks as an *intervention layer* on top of the LVA diagnostic, not as modifications to the diagnostic itself. The LVA diagnoses; BCW/Kotter prescribe.
