# Stage 4.1: Learning Value Architecture Validation Study Protocols

**Date:** March 14, 2026
**Phase:** Stage 4.1 – Validation Study Protocols
**Status:** Comprehensive research design for 5 concurrent validation studies

---

## Executive Summary

This document presents five methodologically rigorous validation studies designed to establish the psychometric properties, predictive validity, construct validity, and cross-context generalizability of the Learning Value Architecture (LVA). These studies address critical evidence gaps identified in the Stage 3.1 Evidence Integration Report and are designed to proceed in parallel with complementary funding streams.

**Critical Research Questions Being Addressed:**

1. Does the LVA Engagement Scale validly measure what it claims across diverse learner populations?
2. Can the LVA Capability Diagnostic predict real-world learning transfer and job performance?
3. Are Engagement, Capability, and Portability truly orthogonal (mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive) dimensions?
4. Does the LVA work at the team level, or do emergent properties require different models?
5. Is the LVA measurement-invariant across educational contexts (higher ed, L&D, K-12, edtech)?

**Expected Outcomes:**
- Confirmation or disconfirmation of the LVA's three-factor architecture
- Empirical clarification of boundary conditions (e.g., when is L4 Capability sufficient vs. insufficient?)
- Identification of context-specific adaptations needed for different learner populations
- Specification of intervention mechanisms (what drives L4→L5 transitions?)

## Study 1: Construct Validity of the LVA Engagement Scale (LES)

### 1.1 Study Purpose and Conceptual Rationale

**Overarching Purpose:** Establish that the LVA Engagement Scale (LES) measures what it claims to measure—a unidimensional progression from Absent (L1) through Aligned (L6)—and that it converges with theoretically similar constructs while diverging from dissimilar ones.

**Theoretical Rationale:**
The LES operationalizes engagement as a motivational progression grounded in self-determination theory (Ryan & Deci), expectancy-value theory (Wiggins & McTighe), and identity integration models (Wenger). The scale should demonstrate:
- **Internal structure:** Six levels form a coherent progression, not discrete unrelated subscales
- **Convergent validity:** LES correlates meaningfully with existing motivation/engagement measures (MSLQ, AMS, IMI)
- **Discriminant validity:** LES does not simply measure personality traits (Big Five) or general mood/affect
- **Sensitivity:** LES changes meaningfully over time and responds to instructional interventions

**Research Questions:**
1. Does the LES's six-level structure fit data better than single-factor or alternative models?
2. How much of the LES variance is explained by existing motivation constructs (MSLQ, AMS)?
3. Does the LES capture unique variance beyond existing instruments?
4. Is engagement level discriminable from personality, particularly from openness and conscientiousness?
5. Does LES change as a function of instruction/intervention, and at what rate?

---

### 1.2 Study Design

**Design Type:** Mixed-methods confirmatory study with cross-sectional and prospective components.

**Phase 1 (Cross-sectional Structure Validation; Months 1–4):**
- Administer full assessment battery to diverse learner sample
- Conduct confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) on LES structure
- Test convergent and discriminant validity via correlational analysis and multi-method-multi-trait (MTMM) matrix
- Preliminary item analysis to identify problematic items

**Phase 2 (Longitudinal Change & Sensitivity; Months 5–10):**
- Reassess subsample across a semester or program
- Measure engagement change and time-to-change
- Correlate change scores with intervention intensity
- Estimate effect sizes for instructional effects

**Phase 3 (Comparative Analysis; Months 4–8, concurrent with Phase 2):**
- Conduct item response theory (IRT) analysis to identify differential item functioning (DIF) by learner subgroup
- Assess whether six-level progression holds equally across demographic groups

---

### 1.3 Sample Design

**Total Sample Size:** N = 550 learners across two broad cohorts

**Cohort A: Higher Education (n = 300)**
- 3–4 universities across diverse geographic regions
- Sampling from: introductory stem courses, humanities seminars, professional programs (business, education, health)
- Learner characteristics: traditional age (18–22) and non-traditional (25+), domestic and international, first-generation and continuing-generation
- Stratified random sampling within institutions to ensure representation across disciplines

**Cohort B: Organizational Learning & Development (n = 250)**
- 8–10 organizations across sectors: tech, manufacturing, healthcare, finance, non-profit
- Sampling from: onboarding programs, professional development courses, compliance training, leadership development
- Learner characteristics: full range of experience levels (entry to senior), diverse educational backgrounds, varying motivation for training

**Sampling Strategy:**
- Stratified random sampling within each site to ensure diversity on key variables (gender, prior achievement, learning context)
- Aim for 50% women, 20%+ first-generation (where applicable), 30%+ underrepresented racial/ethnic minorities
- Oversample n = 100 from Phase 1 for longitudinal follow-up in Phase 2

**Recruitment & Consent:**
- Institutional partnerships (university registrars, HR/L&D departments) facilitate recruitment
- Informed consent with clear explanation of data use
- Participant incentives: $15 gift card per assessment wave in L&D; course credit or raffle entry in higher ed

### 1.4 Measures

**Primary Instrument:**
- **LVA Engagement Scale (LES):** 28 items, 5-point Likert scale, subscale scores for each of six levels
  - Administration time: 10–12 minutes
  - Scoring: subscale means and overall engagement level classification (L1–L6)

**Convergent Validity Measures:**

1. **Motivated Strategies for Learning Questionnaire (MSLQ; Pintrich et al., 1991)**
   - 81 items total; use subscales for intrinsic/extrinsic motivation, value, self-efficacy
   - Demonstrates strong correlations with engagement; expected r = 0.55–0.75 with LES upper levels
   - Administration time: 20 minutes
   - Cost: Public domain

2. **Academic Motivation Scale (AMS; Vallerand et al., 1992)**
   - 28 items measuring intrinsic/extrinsic motivation and amotivation across multiple subtypes
   - Expected pattern: LES L6 → high intrinsic; LES L3 → high extrinsic; LES L1 → high amotivation
   - Administration time: 10 minutes
   - Cost: Public domain

3. **Intrinsic Motivation Inventory (IMI; Ryan, 1982)**
   - 45 items across subscales: interest, effort, competence, pressure, perceived choice
   - Expected correlations with LES particularly for L5–L6 (interest, choice), L4 (effort)
   - Administration time: 12 minutes
   - Cost: Public domain

**Discriminant Validity Measures:**

4. **Big Five Inventory (BFI-2; Soto & John, 2017)**
   - 60 items; five subscales (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism)
   - Expectation: minimal correlation with LES overall, though modest correlations with Openness (r < 0.30) and Conscientiousness (r < 0.25) are acceptable
   - Administration time: 12 minutes
   - Cost: Open access

5. **Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (PANAS; Watson et al., 1988)**
   - 20 items; positive and negative affect subscales
   - Expectation: LES engagement level should not simply reflect mood; correlations r < 0.40 desired
   - Administration time: 3 minutes
   - Cost: Public domain

**Demographic and Context Variables:**
- Prior achievement (GPA, certification status, job level)
- Gender, race/ethnicity, first-generation status
- Learning context (domain, course/program type, delivery mode)
- Prior experience with learning domain (novice vs. experienced learner)

**Longitudinal Measures (Phase 2):**
- **LES administered twice:** baseline and follow-up (6–8 weeks or end-of-semester)
- **Intervention tracking:** Document instructional approaches, motivation-focused interventions (utility value prompts, autonomy support, etc.)
- **Control variables:** Prior engagement with similar content; learner expectations

---

### 1.5 Data Collection Procedures

**Phase 1 (Cross-sectional; Months 1–4):**

1. **Recruitment (Week 1–2):** Institutional liaisons identify courses/programs; researcher presents study; learners consent
2. **Baseline assessment (Week 2–3):**
   - Learners complete all instruments in single session or split across two 20-minute sessions
   - Administration modes: online survey platform (Qualtrics) or paper (sites without IT infrastructure)
   - Quality control: attention check items embedded; response time monitoring
3. **Demographic/context questionnaire:** Concurrent with baseline
4. **Data QA:** Check for missing data, response patterns, outliers; flag inconsistent responding
5. **Preliminary analysis:** Exploratory factor analysis to check dimensionality before CFA

**Phase 2 (Longitudinal; Months 5–10):**

1. **Intervention documentation (ongoing):** Instructors/facilitators log instructional strategies, motivation interventions
2. **Follow-up LES administration (Week 8–12 or end-of-term):**
   - Same administration procedures as baseline
   - Incentive reminder to improve retention (target 75%+ follow-up rate)
3. **Qualitative subsample (n = 30):** Purposive sampling; brief interviews (15 min) asking learners to reflect on engagement changes:
   - "What changed your engagement level?"
   - "What kept you engaged or disengaged?"
   - Coding for themes around autonomy, competence, belonging, value

**Phase 3 (Concurrent; Item Functioning Analysis):**

1. **DIF analysis:** IRT modeling to test whether item difficulty and discrimination parameters differ by:
   - Gender, race/ethnicity, age cohort, educational background
   - Learning context (higher ed vs. L&D; discipline)
2. **Subsample DIF probes:** Brief cognitive interviews (n = 40) with high-DIF items:
   - "What did this item mean to you?"
   - "Do you think it means the same thing to learners from different backgrounds?"

---

### 1.6 Analytical Strategy

**Primary Analyses:**

1. **Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA):**
   - Test theoretical model: 6-level latent structure with 28 items
   - **Comparison models:**
     - Single-factor model (one global engagement dimension)
     - Two-factor model (intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation)
     - Hierarchical six-factor model with general factor
   - **Fit indices:** χ² (RMSEA < 0.08), CFI > 0.90, TLI > 0.90, SRMR < 0.08
   - **Acceptable fit:** Theoretical six-level model should fit significantly better than alternatives (Δχ² test; p < 0.05)

2. **Convergent Validity:**
   - **Correlational matrix:** Pearson correlations between LES subscales and MSLQ, AMS, IMI subscales
   - **Pattern of correlations:**
     - Expected: LES L1 (Absent) ↔ AMS Amotivation (r = 0.60–0.75)
     - Expected: LES L3 (Compliant) ↔ MSLQ Extrinsic (r = 0.55–0.70)
     - Expected: LES L5–L6 (Committed–Aligned) ↔ IMI Interest (r = 0.60–0.75)
   - **Threshold:** At least 12 of 15 predicted correlations should be r > 0.50 and significant (p < 0.05)

3. **Discriminant Validity:**
   - **Correlations with Big Five:** All should be |r| < 0.35 (explaining <12% shared variance)
   - **Correlations with PANAS:** All should be |r| < 0.40
   - **Nested model comparison:** CFA model with engagement and Big Five as separate factors should fit better than combined model (Δχ² p < 0.05)

4. **Multitrait-Multimethod (MTMM) Analysis:**
   - **Heterotrait-monomethod triangles:** Median correlation between different traits measured by same method (MSLQ, AMS, LES)
   - **Heteromethod-monotrait:** Correlation between same trait measured by different methods
   - **Interpretation:** Monotrait > Heterotrait correlations indicate discriminant validity

5. **Internal Consistency:**
   - **Subscale Cronbach's α:** Expected 0.65–0.75 per level (6 separate subscales)
   - **Overall scale Cronbach's α:** Expected 0.78–0.85
   - **Item-total correlations:** All items r > 0.30 with their respective subscale

6. **Reliability Over Time (Phase 2):**
   - **Test-retest correlation (stable learners):** For learners whose engagement should not change (control condition), LES r > 0.70 over 6–8 weeks
   - **Measurement error:** Calculate 95% confidence intervals around individual scores to estimate clinically meaningful change (SEM-based)

7. **Responsiveness to Change (Phase 2):**
   - **Effect sizes:** Calculate Cohen's d for engagement change as a function of intervention intensity
   - **Growth trajectories:** Mixed-effects linear regression predicting LES change from instructional approach, baseline motivation, demographics
   - **Sensitivity threshold:** Should detect Cohen's d ≥ 0.40 effect sizes with 80% power (a priori determined N)

8. **Item Response Theory (IRT):**
   - **Model:** Graded response model (2-PL/3-PL) or partial credit model (appropriate for Likert scale)
   - **Diagnostics:**
     - Item discrimination parameters (discrimination index α > 0.60 desired)
     - Item difficulty (b parameters appropriately spread across the latent ability range)
     - Item information curves (peaks should align with intended level of engagement)
   - **DIF Testing:** Mantel-Haenszel or logistic regression for each item by gender, race/ethnicity, age cohort
   - **Interpretation:** Items with significant DIF (p < 0.05 and effect size > 0.05 in log-odds) flagged for revision or removal

**Secondary Analyses:**

9. **Measurement Invariance (Configural, Metric, Scalar):**
   - Test whether six-level structure holds equivalently across:
     - Gender (male, female, non-binary)
     - First-generation status
     - Discipline (STEM vs. non-STEM)
     - Context (higher ed vs. L&D)
   - **Procedure:** Multi-group CFA with increasingly restrictive constraints
   - **Decision rule:** Δχ² p > 0.05 and ΔCFI < 0.01 indicate invariance at that level

10. **Qualitative Theme Analysis (Phase 2):**
    - **Coding:** Deductive coding around autonomy, competence, belonging (SDT factors) and utility, value (EVT factors)
    - **Software:** MAXQDA or NVivo
    - **Output:** Thematic summary of mechanisms driving engagement change

### 1.7 Power Analysis

**Design:** CFA with 28 items and 6 latent factors; N = 550

**Assumptions:**
- α = 0.05 (two-tailed)
- Desired statistical power = 0.90
- Baseline model fit CFI = 0.92
- Expected model improvement (comparison models) produces Δχ² ≥ 50 (large practical effect)

**Calculation:**
- CFA requires minimum N = (# items × # factors) × 10 to 15
- 28 items × 6 factors = 168; minimum N ≈ 1,680–2,520 for optimal CFA
- **Compromised sample:** N = 550 is moderate; provides adequate power for CFI > 0.90 and RMSEA < 0.10, less power for sharp model discrimination
- **Mitigation:** Combine with large effect size expectations (large differences between theoretical and alternative models); use robust estimators

**Statistical Power for Secondary Tests:**
- **Convergent validity (r = 0.60):** n = 75 per correlation needed for 80% power; N = 550 provides 99%+ power for r ≥ 0.60
- **Discriminant validity (r < 0.35):** Same; N = 550 provides 95%+ power to detect this correlation
- **Longitudinal change (d = 0.40):** Paired t-test with N = 100 provides 80% power
- **DIF detection:** Mantel-Haenszel with α = 0.05; N = 550 split by gender (275 each) provides >90% power to detect moderate DIF

**Ethical Framework:** Minimal risk research; informed consent with emphasis on data privacy

**Specific Considerations:**

1. **Informed Consent:**
   - Clear explanation of: study purpose, data collection procedures, use of data, voluntary nature

   - No individual-level reporting to employers/institutions

### 1.9 Limitations

1. **Sample Size & Diversity:**
   - N = 550 is moderate for CFA with six latent factors; constrains ability to test complex models
   - Mitigation: Stratified sampling ensures demographic diversity; large effect sizes expected (motivation constructs well-established)

2. **Generalizability:**
   - Limited to higher ed and L&D; K-12 and informal learning contexts not represented in Study 1
   - Addressed by Study 5 (cross-context validation)

3. **Self-Report Bias:**
   - LES is self-reported; subject to social desirability bias (particularly in L&D context where engagement monitoring may be perceived as evaluative)
   - Mitigation: Anonymity emphasized; reverse-coded items help detect response patterns; comparison with behavioral/performance data (addressed in Study 2)

4. **Temporal Limitations:**
   - Phase 2 follows learners for 6–8 weeks; insufficient to examine long-term stability of engagement or far-transfer effects
   - Addressed by longer-term Studies 2 and 3

5. **Construct Definition:**
   - Six-level framework assumes linear progression, but engagement may be non-monotonic (learners can cycle through levels)
   - Analysis will examine assumption; alternative models tested

### 1.10 Decision Criteria: When Results Confirm vs. Disconfirm LVA

**Confirmation Criteria (LES Is Valid):**

1. ✓ **Structure:** Theoretical six-factor model fits significantly better than single-factor model (Δχ² p < 0.05; Δχ² > 50)
2. ✓ **Internal consistency:** Subscale α ≥ 0.65 for each level; total α ≥ 0.78
3. ✓ **Convergent validity:** ≥ 12 of 15 predicted correlations with MSLQ/AMS/IMI significant (p < 0.05) and r > 0.50
4. ✓ **Discriminant validity:** All Big Five correlations |r| < 0.35; PANAS |r| < 0.40
5. ✓ **Measurement invariance:** Configural + metric invariance hold across gender, first-gen status, context (Δχ² p > 0.05)
6. ✓ **Responsiveness:** Engagement changes significantly (p < 0.05) over semester; effect size d ≥ 0.40 for instructional interventions

**Disconfirmation Criteria (LES Requires Revision):**

1. ✗ **Structure:** Single-factor or two-factor model fits as well as six-factor model (Δχ² p > 0.05 or Δχ² < 30); suggests levels are not distinct
   - **Action:** Collapse levels; reconsider conceptual framework
2. ✗ **Internal consistency:** Subscale α < 0.60 for any level; suggests items within a level are incoherent
   - **Action:** Revise or remove problematic items
3. ✗ **Convergent validity:** < 10 of 15 predicted correlations > 0.50; suggests inadequate relationship with established constructs
   - **Action:** Revise item content to better capture intended motivational states
4. ✗ **Discriminant validity:** Big Five correlations |r| > 0.40 or PANAS |r| > 0.45; suggests LES is measuring personality or mood, not engagement
   - **Action:** Revise items to focus on engagement, not trait affect; consider alternative operationalization
5. ✗ **Measurement invariance:** Scalar invariance fails (ΔCFI > 0.01); suggests items function differently by group
   - **Action:** Conduct DIF analysis; identify problematic items; consider separate group norms
6. ✗ **Responsiveness:** No significant change over semester; effect sizes d < 0.20; suggests LES is measuring stable trait, not changeable engagement
   - **Action:** Reconsider whether engagement is time-sensitive construct; redesign as state measure if appropriate

**Ambiguous Results Requiring Further Analysis:**

- **Marginal fit (CFI = 0.89–0.91, RMSEA = 0.09–0.10):** Acceptable in exploratory context but warrants model refinement; test alternative structures
- **Moderate convergent validity (r = 0.40–0.50):** Indicates unique measurement but weaker-than-expected relationships; examine whether LES captures something different/important
- **Scalar invariance fails for one subgroup only:** Indicates item bias; revise that item or establish group-specific norms

## Study 2: Predictive Validity of the LVA Capability Diagnostic (LCD)

### 2.1 Study Purpose

**Overarching Purpose:** Demonstrate that the LCD validly predicts real-world performance, transfer, and capability advancement. The LCD should distinguish learners who can apply learning in authentic contexts from those with inert knowledge.

**Theoretical Rationale:**
The LCD operationalizes capability as the durability and transferability of knowledge/skill, ranging from Unconsolidated (L1) through Generative (L7). The LCD is performance-based, not self-reported; coding requires assessment of actual learner behavior on tasks of varying complexity. The key validation question is: **Does LCD level correlate with observable performance in authentic contexts?**

**Research Questions:**
1. Does LCD level predict job performance ratings (supervisor/peer) 6 months post-training?
2. Does LCD level predict actual transfer behavior (application of learning on the job)?
3. Does LCD improvement over time predict performance improvement?
4. Can LCD correctly classify learners into "high transfer" vs. "low transfer" groups (ROC analysis)?
5. Does LCD distinguish true capability from inert knowledge (validated against post-hoc performance tests)?

---

### 2.2 Study Design

**Design Type:** Longitudinal cohort study with workplace learning outcomes

**Timeline:** 6-month longitudinal follow-up with baseline (post-training) and follow-up assessments

**Baseline (Week 1 post-training):**
- Learners complete LCD assessment (immediately after training program)
- Trainers complete performance expectations rating
- Learners provide consent for supervisor/peer ratings at follow-up

**Follow-up (6 months post-training):**
- LCD re-assessment on subset (n = 50) to measure stability/growth
- Supervisor/peer performance ratings
- Transfer behavior observation (structured interview or self-report + verification)
- Job performance metrics (objective if available)

**Control/Comparison:**
- Tracks learners across different training programs and contexts to examine whether LCD predicts performance **across contexts**, not just within one program
- No randomization (observational study); statistical controls for selection bias

---

### 2.3 Sample Design

**Total Sample Size:** N = 200+ learners across multiple training programs

**Sample Composition:**

1. **Technical/Skills Training (n = 70):**
   - Software training programs (3–4 organizations; ~20 learners per program)
   - Expected outcome: Performance metrics include troubleshooting accuracy, time-to-competence
   - Duration: 2–4 week programs

2. **Leadership/Management Development (n = 60):**
   - Manager/supervisor development programs
   - Expected outcome: 360-degree feedback, team performance ratings
   - Duration: 4–8 week programs

3. **Compliance/Safety Training (n = 40):**
   - Safety, data protection, or regulatory compliance training
   - Expected outcome: Incident rates, behavior observations, audit scores
   - Duration: 1–3 days to 2 weeks (intensive)

4. **Academic Contexts (n = 30):**
   - Advanced degree students (thesis-based programs)
   - Expected outcome: Thesis quality, advisor ratings, independent research capability
   - Duration: 1–2 years (tracked 6 months into program)

**Inclusion Criteria:**
- Completed formal training program or degree program coursework
- Supervisor/manager or peer available for follow-up rating
- Workplace context in which performance can be measured
- English-language capable (for interview/LCD assessment)

**Exclusion Criteria:**
- Currently unemployed or in transition (no performance context)
- Training program < 1 week (insufficient time for consolidation)
- Performance metrics unavailable

**Recruitment Strategy:**
- Partner organizations and academic departments identify learners
- Researchers contact learners; brief consent call explaining follow-up process
- Targeted recruitment for diversity: aim for 40% women, 30%+ underrepresented minorities, range of age/experience

---

### 2.4 Measures

**Primary Predictor:**

1. **LVA Capability Diagnostic (LCD):**
   - Performance-based assessment using domain-specific task families (exemplars adapted to each training context)
   - Administered by trained assessors (interviews or scenario-based tasks)
   - Scoring: 7-level classification (L1–L7) + detailed rubric scores
   - Administration time: 30–45 minutes per learner
   - Rater reliability: Dual-coded subset (n = 40) to establish inter-rater agreement (target κ > 0.70)

**Outcome Measures (Follow-up; 6 months post-training):**

1. **Job Performance Ratings:**
   - **Supervisor performance rating:** Standard 5-point Likert scale items assessing job performance in area trained
     - Example: "Applies training content to job responsibilities" (r = expected 0.50–0.70 with LCD)
     - Instrument: Organization-specific performance evaluation form OR standard scale (e.g., 3-item task proficiency scale)
   - **Peer performance rating:** Same scale; peers (n = 2–3 per learner) rate learner's capability/performance
   - **Expected correlations:** LCD L5–L7 learners should have supervisor ratings ≥ 4/5; LCD L1–L2 learners ≤ 2/5

2. **Transfer Behavior Observation:**
   - **Structured interview:** Learner asked to describe three situations where they applied training content
     - Coding: Transfer complexity (near vs. far transfer), frequency, autonomy (did they self-initiate or was prompted?)
     - Scoring rubric: 0 (no evidence of application) → 3 (frequent, self-directed, far transfer)
     - Expected: LCD L4+ → transfer scores ≥ 2; LCD L1–L3 → transfer scores ≤ 1
   - **Direct observation (subset; n = 50):** Researchers observe learner performing trained task in authentic context; code for procedural accuracy, fluency, adaptation to context
   - **Alternative (archival):** Where available, extract performance data (e.g., defect rates for manufacturing, customer satisfaction for service roles, project completion time for technical roles)

3. **Capability Re-Assessment (Subset; n = 50):**
   - LCD re-administered at 6-month follow-up
   - Expected pattern: LCD L5+ learners stable or improve; LCD L3–L4 learners may improve as learning consolidates
   - Comparison: Does LCD improvement over 6 months predict performance improvement?

4. **Job Performance Metrics (Objective, where available):**
   - **Manufacturing/quality:** Defect rate, rework rate, time-to-standard-productivity
   - **Customer service:** Customer satisfaction scores, complaint resolution rate, first-contact resolution
   - **Technical support:** Ticket resolution time, accuracy, customer ratings
   - **Academic:** Thesis advisor rating, graduate seminar grades, publications
   - **Expected:** LCD L5+ → top 25% on metrics; LCD L1–L2 → bottom 25%

5. **Engagement & Capability Post-Test (Validation Check):**
   - Secondary LCD assessment using alternative task exemplars (to test stability/measurement error)
   - Brief engagement survey (3-item MSLQ scales) to check whether LCD captures capability independent of motivation
   - Expected: LCD and engagement are moderately correlated (r = 0.30–0.50), not strongly conflated

---

### 2.5 Data Collection Procedures

**Baseline (Week 1 Post-Training):**

1. **LCD Assessment:**
   - Trained assessor conducts structured interview or observation (30–45 min)
   - Uses domain-specific task exemplars (customized to training program)
   - Records responses/behaviors; scores using rubric
   - Example (software training): "Here's a sample error message similar to one you learned in training. Walk me through what you'd do." Assessor codes for: procedural accuracy, speed, whether learner can explain why steps work

2. **Consent for Follow-Up:**
   - Learner provides written consent for supervisor contact at 6 months
   - Learner provides supervisor/peer contact information

3. **Trainer/Manager Expectations:**
   - Trainer rates learner's expected transfer and performance (brief scale)
   - Collects baseline performance metrics if available (e.g., error rate pre-training)

**6-Month Follow-Up:**

1. **Performance Ratings (Survey):**
   - Researchers contact supervisor/manager; send brief online survey (5 minutes)
   - Items assessing: job performance, capability, application of training
   - Incentive: $10 for completion
   - Target response rate: 80%+

2. **Peer Ratings (Optional; n = 100):**
   - Learner provides names of 2–3 peers who can rate their performance
   - Researchers contact peers; brief survey (same as supervisor)

3. **Transfer Behavior Interview:**
   - Phone or in-person interview (20–30 min) with learner
   - Structured questions: "Describe the last three times you used [training topic] at work. What did you do? How did it work out?"
   - Interviewer codes for transfer evidence and complexity
   - Recorded (with consent) for reliability checking

4. **Objective Performance Metrics:**
   - Work with HR/training department to extract archival data (defect rates, customer satisfaction, project completion time)
   - Ensure de-identified; metrics tracked 6 months post-baseline

5. **LCD Re-Assessment (Subset; n = 50):**
   - Trained assessor re-administers LCD using alternative (but comparable) task exemplars
   - Same rubric scoring
   - Measures stability and change over 6 months

**Quality Assurance:**
- Inter-rater reliability: 30% of LCD assessments coded independently; calculate Cohen's κ (target > 0.70)
- Supervisor rating verification: Call 20% of supervisors to verify accuracy of ratings (spot-check)
- Archival data validation: Cross-check extracted metrics against organizational records

---

### 2.6 Analytical Strategy

**Primary Analyses:**

1. **Correlational Validity:**
   - **Pearson correlations:** LCD level (ordinal coded 1–7) with:
     - Supervisor job performance rating (expected r = 0.50–0.70)
     - Transfer behavior code (expected r = 0.55–0.75)
     - Objective performance metrics (expected r = 0.40–0.65)
   - **Spearman's ρ:** For ordinal data; rank correlations between LCD levels and performance rankings
   - **Interpretation:** Correlations > 0.50 indicate strong predictive validity

2. **Regression Analysis (Multiple Outcome Variables):**
   - **Model:** Supervisor performance rating = β₀ + β₁(LCD level) + β₂(Training program type) + β₃(Baseline performance) + β₄(Engagement) + ε
   - **Outcome 1:** Job performance rating (continuous)
   - **Outcome 2:** Transfer behavior code (continuous)
   - **Outcome 3:** Objective performance metric (domain-specific)
   - **Interpretation:**
     - β₁ should be significant (p < 0.05) and substantial (standardized β > 0.30)
     - R² should be ≥ 0.35 (LCD explains at least 35% of variance in performance)
     - Residual analysis checks for outliers and model assumptions

3. **ROC Curve Analysis (Classification Accuracy):**
   - **Outcome:** Binary "High Transfer" vs. "Low Transfer" classification
   - Operational definition:
     - High Transfer = Supervisor rating ≥ 4/5 AND Transfer interview code ≥ 2 AND (if available) objective metrics in top quartile
     - Low Transfer = inverse
   - **Test:** Does LCD correctly classify learners into these groups?
   - **Metrics:** Sensitivity, specificity, AUC (area under curve)
   - **Decision rule:** AUC > 0.75 indicates good discrimination; AUC > 0.85 indicates excellent
   - **Cut-point analysis:** Determine optimal LCD cutoff (e.g., "LCD ≥ L4 indicates 80% likely to transfer")

4. **Longitudinal Change Analysis (Subset n = 50):**
   - **Mixed-effects linear regression:** Performance metrics = β₀ + β₁(Time: baseline vs. 6-month) + β₂(LCD change) + β₃(Initial LCD level) + random intercept + ε
   - **Question:** Do learners whose LCD improves over 6 months show greater performance improvement?
   - **Interpretation:** If β₂ is significant and positive, LCD change predicts performance improvement

5. **Subgroup Analysis:**
   - **By training program type:** Repeat correlations and regression by context (technical, leadership, compliance, academic)
   - **Question:** Does LCD predictive validity generalize across contexts or is it domain-specific?
   - **Expected:** Moderate-to-strong correlations (r > 0.45) across all contexts, with some variation

6. **Engagement as Moderator/Mediator:**
   - **Mediation:** Does LCD → Transfer through engagement? (Path analysis)
     - Expected: LCD → Engagement → Transfer; direct LCD → Transfer also significant (partial mediation)
   - **Moderation:** Does engagement level moderate the LCD → Performance relationship?
     - Expected: LCD predicts performance regardless of engagement, but relationship stronger at high engagement

7. **Measurement Error & Reliability (LCD):**
   - **Inter-rater reliability:** Cohen's κ for LCD level classification (target > 0.70)
   - **Test-retest:** Correlation between baseline and 6-month LCD on same learners (n = 50) (expected r = 0.70–0.85; indicates stability with expected growth)
   - **Standard error of measurement (SEM):** Calculate to establish confidence intervals around LCD classifications

---

### 2.7 Power Analysis

**Design:** Longitudinal cohort; primary outcome supervisor performance rating; predictor LCD level (7 levels)

**Assumptions:**
- α = 0.05 (two-tailed)
- Desired power = 0.80
- Expected effect size: Pearson r = 0.55 (moderate-to-large; based on meta-analyses of training transfer)
- Attrition: 20% (realistic for 6-month follow-up in organizational contexts)

**Calculation:**
- For r = 0.55: n = 32 per analysis (Fisher Z transformation; α = 0.05, power = 0.80)
- With 20% attrition, recruit n = 40 per outcome variable
- Across 4 primary outcomes (supervisor rating, peer rating, transfer behavior, objective metric): n = 40–50 per outcome
- **Overall sample:** N = 200 ensures adequate power for all correlations and regressions

**Sensitivity Analysis:**
- If effect sizes smaller (r = 0.45): power = 0.72 for N = 200 (acceptable but lower)
- If attrition > 25%: power drops; mitigation strategy = overrecruit 10% (N = 220)

### 2.10 Decision Criteria: When Results Confirm vs. Disconfirm LCD Validity

**Confirmation Criteria (LCD Predicts Performance):**

1. ✓ **Correlation strength:** r ≥ 0.50 with supervisor performance rating; r ≥ 0.55 with transfer behavior code
2. ✓ **Regression significance:** LCD level significant predictor (p < 0.05) in multivariate model; standardized β ≥ 0.30
3. ✓ **Effect size:** R² ≥ 0.35 in regression model (LCD + context + baseline performance explains ≥ 35% of performance variance)
4. ✓ **ROC discrimination:** AUC ≥ 0.75 for High vs. Low Transfer classification
5. ✓ **Generalization:** Correlations hold across ≥ 3 of 4 training contexts
6. ✓ **LCD stability:** Test-retest r ≥ 0.70 on subset; inter-rater κ ≥ 0.70

**Disconfirmation Criteria (LCD Does Not Predict Performance):**

1. ✗ **Correlation weakness:** r < 0.35 with supervisor rating or transfer behavior; suggests LCD is not measuring job-relevant capability
   - **Action:** Reconsider LCD task selection; may be too academic/disconnected from real-world performance
2. ✗ **Non-significant regression:** LCD level not significant predictor (p > 0.05); suggests low predictive power
   - **Action:** Examine whether confounding variables (opportunity, engagement) are stronger predictors; reconsider LCD construct
3. ✗ **Low effect size:** R² < 0.25; suggests LCD explains <25% of performance variance
   - **Action:** Indicates other factors (opportunity, contextual support) more important; reframe LCD as one component of transfer prediction, not primary driver
4. ✗ **Poor ROC discrimination:** AUC < 0.65; LCD does not validly distinguish high vs. low transfer
   - **Action:** Revise LCD rubrics; consider that boundary between levels (esp. L4–L5) may be poorly defined

**Ambiguous Results Requiring Further Investigation:**

- **Context-specific validity:** Correlations strong in technical training (r = 0.60) but weak in leadership (r = 0.35); suggests LCD may need context-specific adaptation
  - **Action:** Develop domain-specific LCD versions; validate separately
- **Engagement confound:** Engagement a stronger predictor than LCD (r = 0.65 vs. 0.45); suggests motivation drives transfer more than capability
  - **Action:** Conduct mediation analysis; clarify whether LCD captures capability independent of motivation
- **Attrition patterns:** Learners with low LCD levels more likely to drop out of follow-up; biases estimates
  - **Action:** Conduct missing-data analysis; use multiple imputation to assess sensitivity

## Study 3: MECE Architecture Validation

### 3.1 Study Purpose

**Overarching Purpose:** Empirically validate that Engagement, Capability, and Portability are Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive (MECE) dimensions. Test whether the three dimensions are orthogonal (independent) or whether they correlate substantially (suggesting shared underlying construct).

**Theoretical Rationale:**
The LVA claims that a comprehensive learning outcome requires assessment across three dimensions that are conceptually distinct but empirically related. MECE status means:
- **Mutual Exclusivity (ME):** Dimensions measure different constructs; low correlation expected (r < 0.40)
- **Collective Exhaustiveness (CE):** Three dimensions together capture learning outcomes comprehensively; little residual variance in learning quality unmeasured

**Research Questions:**
1. Are E, C, P dimensionally independent? What are correlations between dimensions? (Expected: r = 0.20–0.35)
2. Does a single-factor model (all three dimensions loading on general "learning value" factor) fit worse than three-factor model?
3. What residual variance remains after E, C, P are measured? (Structural equation modeling)
4. Are there subgroups for which the three-factor structure does not hold? (Multi-group SEM)
5. Do E, C, P together predict learning outcomes/performance better than any single dimension? (Comparative regression)

---

### 3.2 Study Design

**Design Type:** Large-scale cross-sectional structural equation modeling (SEM) study with diverse populations

**Sample:** N = 800+ learners across diverse educational contexts to ensure structure is robust and not artifact of specific sample

**Assessments:**
- All three LVA instruments (LES, LCD, LPR) administered to same learners
- Outcome variables: learning satisfaction, perceived utility, performance (self/other-rated)

---

### 3.3 Sample Design

**Total Sample Size:** N = 850 learners stratified across contexts

**Context Breakdown:**

1. **Higher Education (n = 300):**
   - 4–5 universities
   - Diverse courses: introductory STEM (100), humanities seminars (100), professional programs (100)
   - Learner N per institution: 60–75

2. **Corporate L&D (n = 300):**
   - 6–8 organizations across sectors
   - Diverse programs: onboarding (100), technical skills (100), leadership (100)
   - Learner N per organization: 40–50

3. **K-12 Education (n = 150):**
   - 4–5 schools across districts
   - Grade levels: high school (10–12); embedded in science/math classes
   - Learner N per school: 30–40

4. **Online/Edtech Platforms (n = 100):**
   - Learners enrolled in MOOCs or self-paced online courses
   - Diverse topics (programming, language learning, professional development)
   - Voluntary participants from platform user base

**Sampling Strategy:**
- Stratified random within each context
- Demographic diversity: 45% women, 25% non-binary/other, 35% underrepresented ethnic minorities
- Age range: 14 (K-12) to 65+ (corporate)

---

### 3.4 Measures

**All Three LVA Instruments:**

1. **LVA Engagement Scale (LES):** 28 items, subscale scores for L1–L6
2. **LVA Capability Diagnostic (LCD):** Performance-based; 7-level classification
3. **LVA Portability Rubric (LPR):** Observation/interview; 6-level classification

**Outcome Variables (Validity Indicators):**

1. **Learning Satisfaction (3-item scale):**
   - "Overall, how satisfied are you with your learning in this program?"
   - "How well did you accomplish your learning goals?"
   - "How valuable was this learning experience?" (1–5 scale)

2. **Perceived Utility (3-item scale):**
   - "I can see how I'll use what I learned"
   - "This learning is relevant to my goals"
   - "I'm more capable after this learning" (1–5 scale)

3. **Learning Outcomes (Context-Specific):**
   - **Higher Ed:** Course grade, self-assessed capability, final exam score (where available)
   - **Corporate:** Supervisor performance rating, transfer behavior observation
   - **K-12:** Teacher assessment of engagement and capability growth
   - **Edtech:** Course completion rate, post-course quiz performance

4. **Engagement Measure (Behavioral):**
   - **Higher Ed/K-12:** Time-on-task, attendance, participation (from instructor)
   - **Corporate:** Training completion rate, practice/reinforcement activities completed
   - **Edtech:** Session duration, module completion, quiz attempts

---

### 3.5 Data Collection Procedures

**Single Assessment Wave (Cross-Sectional):**

1. **Baseline Questionnaires (30 min):**
   - LES (10 min; self-report)
   - LPR self-assessment version (5 min)
   - Demographic/context variables (5 min)
   - Outcome/behavioral measures (10 min)

2. **LCD Assessment (30–45 min):**
   - Separate session; trained assessor
   - Domain-customized task exemplars
   - Scored using rubric; 7-level classification

3. **Instructor/Observer Rating (LPR; 15 min):**
   - Teacher/trainer rates learner on LPR observation rubric
   - Parallel to learner self-assessment

4. **Optional: Archival Data Extraction:**
   - Course grades, attendance, test scores (where available)
   - Performance metrics (where available)

**Quality Assurance:**
- Inter-rater reliability for LCD: 20% dual-coded (target κ > 0.70)
- LPR agreement (instructor vs. self-assessment): Intraclass correlation (ICC) expected r > 0.60

---

### 3.6 Analytical Strategy

**Primary Analyses:**

1. **Structural Equation Modeling (SEM):**

   **Model 1 (Theoretical MECE Structure):**
   - Three latent factors: Engagement, Capability, Portability
   - Engagement factor: 6 subscale indicators (L1–L6 means)
   - Capability factor: 7 subscale indicators (L1–L7 levels) OR single L-level indicator
   - Portability factor: 6 subscale indicators (L1–L6 levels)
   - Paths: E, C, P → Outcomes (learning satisfaction, utility, performance)
   - Expected fit: CFI > 0.90, RMSEA < 0.08

   **Model 2 (One-Factor Alternative):**
   - Single "Learning Value" factor
   - All three dimension indicators load on one factor
   - Expected fit: CFI < 0.85, RMSEA > 0.10 (worse fit than Model 1)

   **Model 3 (Two-Factor Alternative):**
   - Motivation (E) vs. Skill (C, P combined)
   - Tests whether Capability and Portability are separable from Engagement
   - Expected fit: better than Model 1 in fit indices? (empirical question)

   **Fit Comparison:**
   - χ² difference test: Δχ² (Model 2 vs. Model 1) should be large (p < 0.01), indicating Model 1 superior
   - Δ CFI: Model 1 should have CFI > Model 2 by > 0.05

2. **Dimension Correlation Analysis:**
   - Pearson correlations between factors (E–C, E–P, C–P)
   - Expected: r = 0.20–0.35 (small-to-small moderate effect; statistically significant but conceptually distinct)
   - Pattern interpretation:
     - r > 0.50 suggests dimensions not ME; consider dimension fusion
     - r < 0.10 suggests dimensions not related; consider whether they're empirically necessary

3. **Variance Explained Analysis:**
   - R² for each outcome (satisfaction, utility, performance) predicted by:
     - Engagement alone
     - Capability alone
     - Portability alone
     - All three combined (E + C + P)
   - Expected: R²(E+C+P) > R²(E alone) + R²(C alone) + R²(P alone) by 10–20% (additive value of multi-dimensional assessment)
   - If R²(E+C+P) only slightly > R²(best single dimension), suggests other dimension is redundant

4. **Measurement Invariance Across Contexts:**
   - Multi-group SEM: test whether three-factor structure holds equivalently across:
     - Higher Ed vs. Corporate vs. K-12 vs. Edtech
     - Gender, age, prior experience level
   - Procedure: Configural → metric → scalar invariance
   - Expected: Metric invariance should hold (structure same); scalar may differ (different means)

5. **Residual Analysis & Dimensionality:**
   - Calculate residual variance after three-factor model
   - Factor analysis of residuals: do residuals form additional latent dimension?
   - Expected: Residual factor eigenvalue < 1.0; no substantial additional structure
   - If eigenvalue > 1.5, suggests fourth dimension omitted (e.g., Opportunity)

6. **Multi-Method Approach to Dimension Independence:**
   - **Discriminant validity index (Fornell-Larcker):** For each dimension, squared AVE (average variance extracted) should exceed inter-factor correlation squared
   - Interpretation: Confirms discriminant validity if AVE² > r²(E-C)
   - Applied to E, C, P: Each should be more strongly correlated with own indicators than with other dimensions' indicators

---

### 3.7 Power Analysis

**Design:** SEM with three latent factors; N = 850; ~20 observed indicators

**Assumptions:**
- α = 0.05
- Target power = 0.90 for detecting model misfit (RMSEA > 0.08)
- Medium effect size differences between models (Δχ² ≥ 100)

**Calculation:**
- SEM rule of thumb: N = (# of observed variables) × (5–10) = 20 × 5 to 10 = 100–200 minimum
- For complex SEM with mediator paths: N ≥ 400–500 ideal
- N = 850 provides 95%+ power for detecting medium effects; adequate for multi-group analysis (850/4 contexts ≈ 210 per group)

**Sensitivity:**
- Subgroup analysis (context × dimension structure): N = 200 per context adequate for factor analysis (20 items per group × 10 = 200)

### 3.8 IRB/Ethics Considerations

Similar to Study 1; minimal risk

### 3.9 Limitations

1. **Cross-Sectional Design:**
   - Cannot establish causality (E → C → P progression); only correlational structure
   - Addressed by Study 2 (longitudinal)

2. **LCD as Single Indicator:**
   - If LCD represented as single categorical variable (7 levels) rather than multiple indicators, dimension structure less clearly tested
   - Mitigation: Include both raw LCD level AND underlying task performance scores as indicators

3. **Sample Size Adequacy for K-12:**
   - K-12 learner sample only n = 150; may have lower power for subgroup analysis
   - Mitigation: K-12 learners analyzed as one stratum; detailed subgroup analysis reserved for Study 5

### 3.10 Decision Criteria

**Confirmation Criteria (Three Dimensions Are MECE):**

1. ✓ **Structural fit:** Model 1 (three factors) CFI > 0.90; RMSEA < 0.08
2. ✓ **Superiority over single-factor:** Δχ² (Model 2 vs. Model 1) > 100; p < 0.001
3. ✓ **Dimension independence:** Correlations E–C, E–P, C–P all in range r = 0.20–0.35; none exceed 0.45
4. ✓ **Discriminant validity:** Fornell-Larcker criterion met; each dimension more correlated with own indicators than others' indicators
5. ✓ **Additive prediction:** R²(E+C+P) exceeds best single dimension by ≥ 15%
6. ✓ **Measurement invariance:** Configural + metric invariance hold across contexts (ΔCFI < 0.01)

**Disconfirmation Criteria (Dimensions Not MECE):**

1. ✗ **Structural fit poor:** CFI < 0.85 or RMSEA > 0.10; suggests proposed structure does not fit data
   - **Action:** Revisit dimensional definitions; conduct exploratory factor analysis to identify data-driven structure
2. ✗ **High inter-factor correlations:** E–C or E–P or C–P > 0.50; suggests dimensions are not distinctly measuring different constructs
   - **Action:** Assess whether dimensions should be combined or reconceptualized
3. ✗ **Non-additive prediction:** R²(E+C+P) ≤ R²(best single dimension) + 0.05; suggests dimensions redundant
   - **Action:** Consider whether all three dimensions necessary; justify inclusion of seemingly redundant dimensions
4. ✗ **Scalar invariance fails:** Groups significantly differ in means of latent factors (Δχ² p < 0.01 for all groups); suggests dimensions operate differently by context
   - **Action:** Develop context-specific norming or reconsider whether framework is universally applicable

**Ambiguous Results:**

- **Model 3 (two-factor E vs. C+P) fits nearly as well as Model 1:** Suggests Capability and Portability may be empirically overlapping
  - **Action:** Conduct additional analysis comparing C-only vs. P-only predictions; assess whether C and P can be unified or require separate assessment

## Study 4: Team-Level Diagnostic Validation

### 4.1 Study Purpose

**Overarching Purpose:** Validate the Team Learning Diagnostic (TLD) and establish that team-level outcomes cannot be adequately predicted from individual member LVA profiles alone. Test multi-level structure: individual Engagement/Capability/Portability aggregate to team-level learning capacity.

**Theoretical Rationale:**
Stage 3.1 identifies "Multi-Level Extension" as a critical vulnerability. The LVA is designed for individuals; team learning shows emergent properties (e.g., transactive memory, shared mental models) not reducible to member-level assessment. Study 4 validates whether TLD captures these emergent properties.

**Research Questions:**
1. Do team learning outcomes correlate with team aggregate LVA profiles (sum/mean of member levels)?
2. Does TLD add predictive value beyond individual member LVA scores? (Multi-level modeling)
3. Which TLD components (transactive memory, shared mental models, psychological safety, collective capability) best predict team performance?
4. Is there an optimal team composition on E–C–P dimensions for team effectiveness?
5. Do intra-team disparities in Engagement/Capability matter (e.g., is diverse capability problematic or beneficial)?

2. **Team Member Background:**
   - Role/title
   - Tenure on team
   - Prior experience with team domain
   - Educational level

**Team Level (Aggregated & Team-Specific):**

1. **Team Learning Diagnostic (TLD; 4 components):**

   **A. Transactive Memory (TM; 7 items):**
   - Team-level items assessing shared knowledge about "who knows what"
   - Example: "We know which team members have expertise in specific areas"
   - Mean of team member ratings (Likert 1–5); aggregated TM score
   - Expected: Teams with higher TM show better coordination and problem-solving

   **B. Shared Mental Models (SMM; 8 items):**
   - Alignment on team goals, processes, assumptions
   - Example: "Our team has shared understanding of our goals and how we'll achieve them"
   - Mean team rating; also calculate dispersion (SD) to measure cohesion
   - Expected: High SMM teams show coordinated action; high dispersion indicates misalignment

   **C. Psychological Safety (PS; 5 items):**
   - Willingness to take interpersonal risks
   - Example: "It's safe to ask for help or admit I don't know something" (Edmondson scale)
   - Mean team rating; dispersion indicates uneven safety
   - Expected: Teams with high PS show higher learning, creativity, error reporting

   **D. Collective Capability Distribution (CC; rubric):**
   - Aggregate team member LVA Capability levels (L1–L7)
   - Calculate: mean team Capability, SD (dispersion), range (min–max)
   - Also assess: "Does team have redundancy in critical skills? Are key capabilities single-sourced?"
   - Example scoring: High CC = mean ≥ L4 with low SD; Low CC = mean ≤ L3 or high SD (uneven distribution)

2. **Team Performance Metrics:**

   **Objective (where available):**
   - Project completion rate, on-time delivery, budget variance (project teams)
   - Department revenue, customer satisfaction, error rates (functional teams)
   - Project grade, peer ratings (student teams)
   - Patient safety incidents, satisfaction scores (healthcare)

   **Subjective:**
   - Team leader assessment of team effectiveness (3-item scale: goal achievement, collaboration, learning)
   - Team member satisfaction (3-item: satisfied with team, would work with team again, team is effective)
   - Organizational reputation (e.g., "How would you rate this team relative to similar teams in the organization?")

3. **Team Network Analysis:**
   - Sociometric survey: "Who on the team do you turn to for [task-specific expertise]?" (multiple choice)
   - Construct collaboration network
   - Calculate: Centrality (is knowledge distributed or concentrated?), clustering (are subgroups isolated?), density (how connected is the team?)
   - Expected: Teams with high network density and distributed centrality show better learning and coordination

### 4.6 Analytical Strategy

**Primary Analyses:**

1. **Multi-Level Modeling (Individuals Nested in Teams):**

   **Unconditional Model (Baseline):**
   - Outcome: Individual learning satisfaction or performance contribution
   - Null model: ICC (intraclass correlation) = variance at team level / total variance
   - Expected ICC = 0.15–0.30 (15–30% of variance attributable to team membership)

   **Level 1 (Individual):**
   - Outcome = Engagement + Capability + Portability + (role, tenure) + error

   **Level 2 (Team):**
   - Team intercept = Team aggregate LVA profile + TLD components + (team size, industry) + error
   - Cross-level interaction: Does team TLD moderate relationship between individual LVA and outcome?

   **Interpretation:**
   - If team effects significant after accounting for individual LVA, TLD adds value
   - If team effects null, individual LVA sufficient (contradicts multi-level assumption)

2. **Hierarchical Regression (Incremental Validity):**

   **Outcome:** Team performance (objective or aggregated subjective rating)

   **Model 1:** Team aggregate individual LVA profiles only
   - Predictors: Mean team Engagement, Capability, Portability
   - R² = ?

   **Model 2:** Add TLD components
   - Predictors: Model 1 + Transactive Memory + Shared Mental Models + Psychological Safety + Capability distribution
   - ΔR² = TLD incremental validity
   - Expected: ΔR² ≥ 0.10 (TLD explains additional 10%+ variance)
   - Statistical test: F-test of ΔR² significant (p < 0.05)

3. **Component Contribution Analysis:**
   - Relative importance of TM, SMM, PS, CC within TLD
   - Correlation of each component with team performance (r values)
   - Expected ranking: PS > TM > SMM > CC (psychological safety most predictive)
   - Standardized regression coefficients indicate which components strongest predictors

4. **Team Composition Analysis:**

   **Optimal Team Profiles:**
   - Group teams by engagement/capability/portability distributions:
     - Homogeneous (all members similar level) vs. heterogeneous (mixed levels)
     - High-performing (E ≥ L4, C ≥ L4) vs. mixed vs. low-performing
   - Compare team outcomes across profiles
   - Expected: Teams with mean C ≥ L4 and high TM outperform others
   - Unexpected pattern: High heterogeneity sometimes beneficial (diverse expertise) or detrimental (misalignment)

5. **Dispersion Effects (Standard Deviations):**
   - Test whether team SD on E, C, P predicts outcomes
   - High SD = diverse team (some expert, some novice)
   - Hypothesis: Moderate SD optimal (diversity of skill without severe gaps); extreme SD problematic
   - Regression: Outcome = Mean E/C/P + SD E/C/P + TLD + error
   - Expected: SD effects U-shaped or inverted-U (extreme values detrimental)

6. **Network Analysis:**
   - Calculate team network metrics: density, centrality, clustering
   - Correlate with team performance
   - Expected: Higher density + distributed centrality associated with better outcomes (knowledge accessible to all)
   - Examine: Do isolated subgroups within team (high clustering, low between-group ties) harm coordination?

7. **Psychometric Validation of TLD:**
   - Internal consistency: Cronbach's α for each TLD component (target α ≥ 0.70)
   - Inter-rater agreement: ICC for team member ratings of team-level constructs (expected r ≥ 0.60; indicates consensus)
   - Construct validity: Correlations among TLD components (expected r = 0.30–0.50; distinct but related)

**Secondary Analyses:**

8. **Mediational Pathways:**
   - Does TLD mediate the relationship between individual LVA and team outcomes?
   - Path model: Individual LVA → TLD → Team Performance
   - Interpretation: Individual members' engagement/capability → team transactive memory/psychological safety → better outcomes

9. **Moderation Effects:**
   - Does team context moderate the LVA–TLD–Performance relationship?
   - Variables: Team type (project vs. functional), industry, team size, team tenure
   - Analysis: Subgroup regression; interaction terms
   - Expected: Relationships stronger in knowledge-intensive teams; weaker in routine-execution teams

---

### 4.7 Power Analysis

**Design:** Multi-level modeling with individuals (N ≈ 200–250) nested in teams (n = 50); outcome team performance

**Assumptions:**
- α = 0.05
- Desired power = 0.80
- Expected ICC = 0.20 (20% variance at team level)
- Expected effect size: TLD adds ΔR² = 0.12 (medium effect)

**Calculation:**
- Power for multilevel modeling depends on number of teams + individuals per team
- Design effect: λ = 1 + (m–1) × ICC = 1 + (4–1) × 0.20 = 1.60 (average team size m = 4.5)
- Effective N for level-2 (team-level) analyses = 250 / 1.60 = 156 "effective individuals"
- For team-level analysis: n = 50 teams; design has adequate power for effect sizes f² ≥ 0.15 (medium)
- Sensitivity: If only n = 35 teams due to recruitment challenges, power drops to 0.70 for medium effect

**Mitigation:** Oversample n = 55 teams to account for 10% dropout/incomplete data

**Confirmation Criteria (TLD Valid, Multi-Level Effects Exist):**

1. ✓ **ICC significant:** ICC ≥ 0.15; team-level variance significant (p < 0.05); justifies multi-level modeling
2. ✓ **TLD incremental validity:** ΔR² ≥ 0.10 when TLD added to model (p < 0.05)
3. ✓ **TLD component reliability:** Cronbach's α ≥ 0.70 for TM, SMM, PS; inter-rater ICC ≥ 0.60
4. ✓ **Component effects:** At least 2 of 4 TLD components significant predictors (p < 0.05) of team performance
5. ✓ **Team composition effects:** Teams with high mean Capability + high TLD outperform low-C + low-TLD teams (significant difference; p < 0.05)

**Disconfirmation Criteria (TLD Not Needed; Individual LVA Sufficient):**

1. ✗ **ICC negligible:** ICC < 0.10; little team-level variance; individual characteristics dominate
   - **Interpretation:** Team effects minimal; LVA individual assessment sufficient
   - **Action:** Discontinue Team Learning Diagnostic; use individual LVA only

2. ✗ **No incremental validity:** ΔR² < 0.05; TLD adds nothing beyond individual profiles
   - **Interpretation:** Team-level diagnostics redundant with individual assessment
   - **Action:** Simplify to individual LVA; team effects predictable from member profiles alone

3. ✗ **TLD component unreliability:** Cronbach's α < 0.60 for any component; inter-rater ICC < 0.50
   - **Interpretation:** TLD items not measuring coherent constructs; measurement issues
   - **Action:** Revise TLD items; potentially remove unreliable components

**Ambiguous Results:**

- **ICC moderate but TLD effects weak:** Team effects exist but TLD doesn't capture them
  - **Action:** Conduct exploratory factor analysis of team-level data; identify alternative team-level constructs

## Study 5: Cross-Context Generalizability & Measurement Invariance

### 5.1 Study Purpose

**Overarching Purpose:** Establish that LVA instruments are measurement-invariant across diverse educational and organizational contexts. Test whether the same items function equivalently in higher education, K-12, corporate L&D, and edtech platforms.

**Theoretical Rationale:**
Measurement invariance is a prerequisite for cross-context comparisons. If items function differently by context (Differential Item Functioning, DIF), LVA norms cannot be pooled; context-specific interpretations needed. Study 5 empirically determines whether LVA is truly cross-context or requires substantial adaptation.

**Research Questions:**
1. Do the LES, LCD, LPR demonstrate configural, metric, and scalar invariance across contexts?
2. Which LVA items show significant DIF by context? (If any)
3. What contextual factors moderate LVA measurement (teaching quality, motivation climate, learner stakes)?
4. Can LVA profiles be directly compared across contexts, or do context-specific norms need development?
5. Are upper-level capabilities (L5+) empirically achievable across all contexts, or only in some?

---

### 5.6 Analytical Strategy

**Primary Analyses:**

1. **Measurement Invariance Testing (Multi-Group CFA):**

   **Sequence of Nested Models (Increasingly Restrictive):**

   a) **Configural Invariance:** Unconstrained model; factor structure same across groups but freely estimated parameters
      - Tests: Does proposed 6-factor (Engagement) or 7-factor (Capability) structure emerge equally across contexts?
      - Fit indices: CFI, RMSEA per group and overall
      - Expected: CFI > 0.90 in all groups; indicates structure holds

   b) **Metric Invariance:** Constrain factor loadings to equality across groups
      - Tests: Do items have same relationship to latent factors across contexts?
      - Comparison: Δχ² (metric vs. configural); ΔCFI < 0.01 indicates metric invariance
      - Interpretation: If metric invariance holds, regression slopes are comparable across contexts

   c) **Scalar Invariance:** Constrain both factor loadings AND item intercepts to equality
      - Tests: Do items have same absolute meaning across contexts (not just relationships)?
      - Comparison: Δχ² (scalar vs. metric); ΔCFI < 0.01 indicates scalar invariance
      - Interpretation: If scalar invariance holds, latent factor means can be directly compared across contexts

   **Decision Rules:**
   - Configural invariance assumed (necessary baseline); test metric and scalar
   - Metric invariance highly desired (enables regression comparisons)
   - Scalar invariance less critical if metric holds (can still compare relationships)
   - Partial invariance acceptable (some items non-invariant; exclude from mean comparisons)

2. **Differential Item Functioning (DIF) Analysis:**

   For items showing non-invariance:

   - **Mantel-Haenszel Test:** For each LES item, test whether difficulty/discrimination differs by context
     - Focal group = Higher Ed (reference); comparison groups = Corp, K-12, Edtech
     - Detect items where probability of endorsement differs significantly by context (p < 0.05)
     - Effect size: Log-odds ratio; |effect| > 0.43 indicates moderate DIF

   - **Logistic Regression:** Item response = β₀ + β₁(Context) + β₂(Ability) + β₃(Context × Ability) + ε
     - Tests: Uniform DIF (context main effect) vs. non-uniform DIF (interaction)
     - Items with significant context × ability interaction are problematic (functioning differently by context depending on ability)

   - **Interpretation:**
     - DIF items may require:
       - Revision (reword to be more context-neutral)
       - Removal (if DIF substantial and revision not feasible)
       - Stratified scoring (context-specific item weights or norms)

3. **Item Response Theory (IRT) Analysis by Context:**

   For each context separately:
   - Fit graded response model (2-PL or 3-PL)
   - Estimate item discrimination (a) and difficulty (b) parameters per context
   - **Interpretation:**
     - If a and b parameters similar across contexts, items function equivalently
     - Large differences in b (difficulty): Item easier/harder in one context (suggests different interpretation)
     - Large differences in a (discrimination): Item more/less predictive of underlying construct in one context

   - Plot Item Characteristic Curves (ICC) for each context; overlay to visualize differences
   - Items with crossing ICCs indicate problematic DIF

4. **Latent Factor Means Comparison (if Scalar Invariance Holds):**

   - **ANOVA-equivalent test:** Compare latent means across contexts
     - Predictors: context indicators (contrast-coded)
     - Outcome: latent engagement, capability, portability means
   - **Expected:** Some variation across contexts (e.g., corporate trainees more strategically engaged; K-12 students more compliant) but overall progression similar
   - **Interpretation:** If latent means significantly different, may indicate context-specific norms desirable

5. **Contextual Moderators of LVA Relationships:**

   - **Regression with interaction terms:**
     - Outcome: learning success (grade, performance, satisfaction)
     - Predictors: Engagement + Capability + Portability + Context + Context × E + Context × C + Context × P
   - **Questions:**
     - Does Engagement predict outcomes more strongly in intrinsically-motivated contexts (edtech) vs. required contexts (K-12)?
     - Does Capability predict transfer differently in workplace (where stakes high) vs. academic (where stakes lower)?
   - **Expected:** Moderate interactions; main effects (E, C, P) robust across contexts but effect sizes vary

6. **Upper-Level Capability Achievement Across Contexts:**

   - **Descriptive analysis:** What proportion of learners reach L5+ capability by context?
     - Expected: Lower rates in K-12 and edtech (ceiling effects; brief programs); higher rates in advanced higher ed and intensive corporate programs
   - **Logistic regression:** Predict likelihood of L5+ achievement from context + engagement + program duration
   - **Qualitative follow-up:** For learners achieving L5+, what contextual factors enabled far transfer?
     - Interviews: n = 10 per context

### 5.7 Power Analysis

**Design:** Multi-group CFA with 4 groups; LES with 6 factors, 28 items; total N = 1,200

**Assumptions:**
- α = 0.05
- Target power = 0.90 for detecting non-invariance (e.g., Δχ² ≥ 50)
- N per group = 300
- ~28 observed variables per group

**Calculation:**
- Multi-group CFA power depends on group size and number of groups
- N = 300 per group provides 95%+ power for detecting moderate deviations from specified model in each group
- For DIF detection: Mantel-Haenszel test with n = 300 per contrast group has 90%+ power to detect moderate DIF (|OH| > 0.43)
- N = 1,200 overall ensures adequate power for all invariance tests and subgroup comparisons

### 5.10 Decision Criteria

**Confirmation Criteria (LVA Is Measurement-Invariant):**

1. ✓ **Configural invariance:** CFI > 0.90 in each context; proposed factor structure fits
2. ✓ **Metric invariance:** Δχ²(metric vs. configural) p > 0.05; ΔCFI < 0.01
3. ✓ **Scalar invariance:** Δχ²(scalar vs. metric) p > 0.05; ΔCFI < 0.01 (ideal; partial invariance acceptable)
4. ✓ **Minimal DIF:** < 5% of LES items show significant Mantel-Haenszel DIF (p < 0.05)
5. ✓ **IRT parameter stability:** Item a and b parameters vary < 0.3 units across contexts (within measurement error)
6. ✓ **Generalizability:** Regression patterns (Engagement → Outcome) significant in ≥ 3 of 4 contexts

**Disconfirmation Criteria (LVA Requires Context-Specific Adaptation):**

1. ✗ **Configural invariance fails:** CFI < 0.85 in one or more contexts; suggests factor structure doesn't emerge uniformly
   - **Interpretation:** LVA structure not universally applicable; context-specific operationalization needed
   - **Action:** Develop context-specific instrument versions

2. ✗ **Metric invariance fails:** Δχ² p < 0.01; ΔCFI > 0.02; indicates factor loadings significantly differ
   - **Interpretation:** Items measure construct differently across contexts
   - **Action:** Identify problematic items; revise or remove; establish context-specific norms

3. ✗ **Extensive DIF:** > 15% of items show significant Mantel-Haenszel DIF
   - **Interpretation:** Substantial item bias across contexts; compromises cross-context comparisons
   - **Action:** Revise items; develop parallel forms for different contexts; or shift to adaptive/IRT-based assessment

4. ✗ **Context-Moderated Relationships:** Engagement significantly predicts outcomes in Corp/Edtech (r > 0.50) but not K-12 (r < 0.20); suggests context alters meaning of engagement
   - **Interpretation:** Constructs operate differently; context-specific theory/interventions needed
   - **Action:** Develop context-informed guidance for practitioners

**Ambiguous Results:**

- **Partial metric invariance (some items invariant, others not):** Acceptable if majority invariant; remove non-invariant items from comparative analyses; report separately
- **Higher latent engagement in one context:** May reflect actual contextual differences (e.g., adult learners more strategically motivated); not necessarily problem if structure invariant

## Overall Recommendations & Decision Tree

### Decision Framework: When Each Study Confirms/Disconfirms LVA

**Scenario A: All Studies Confirm (Optimal Outcome)**
- Action: LVA ready for broad implementation; develop practitioner guides, training programs
- Next phase: Design and test interventions targeting L4→L5 transitions (identified as critical gap)
- Dissemination: Peer-reviewed publications; media outreach; education policy engagement

**Scenario B: Studies 1–3 Confirm; Study 4 Disconfirms (Team Effects Negligible)**
- Action: Focus LVA on individual learner assessment; discontinue Team Learning Diagnostic
- Implication: LVA individual profiles sufficient; team composition less important than thought
- Next phase: Develop individual-level intervention studies

**Scenario C: Study 2 Disconfirms (LCD Doesn't Predict Transfer)**
- Action: Capability dimension needs reconceptualization; current LCD operationalization inadequate
- Investigation: Conduct qualitative research to understand why capability (as measured) doesn't predict performance
- Hypothesis: Opportunity/contextual factors more important (aligns with Stage 3.1 Opportunity Gap finding)
- Next phase: Incorporate Opportunity as fourth dimension; redesign LCD to include contextual assessment

**Scenario D: Study 5 Disconfirms (Measurement Non-Invariance)**
- Action: LVA requires context-specific adaptation; cannot use single set of items across contexts
- Development: Create parallel forms for higher ed, K-12, corporate, edtech
- Implication: More complex implementation; multiple training/norming approaches needed

**Scenario E: Mixed Results (Some Studies Confirm, Others Ambiguous)**
- Action: Conduct integrated analysis across studies to identify patterns
- Prioritize: Resource intensive follow-up studies on ambiguous findings
- Timeline: Year 2–3 for targeted follow-up research addressing specific gaps

## Conclusion

This five-study validation protocol is designed to comprehensively establish the psychometric properties, predictive validity, construct validity, team-level applicability, and cross-context generalizability of the Learning Value Architecture. The studies address critical evidence gaps identified in Stage 3.1 and are designed to proceed in parallel, with findings informing one another iteratively.

**Critical Success Factors:**
1. Recruitment and retention of diverse learner samples across contexts
2. Training and calibration of LCD assessors (inter-rater reliability paramount)
3. Organizational partnerships enabling access to performance data
4. Rigorous statistical methods with pre-registered hypotheses and decision criteria
5. Transparent reporting of both confirming and disconfirming evidence

**Expected Outcomes:**
- Empirical clarification of LVA's measurement properties and predictive validity
- Specification of boundary conditions and context-specific adaptations
- Evidence-based recommendations for when/how to use LVA in different educational settings
- Identification of mechanisms driving engagement and capability development
- Policy-relevant insights on learning outcomes measurement across sectors

This research has potential to significantly advance learning sciences by providing a comprehensive, multi-dimensional framework for assessing learning outcomes and informing educational practice at scale.
