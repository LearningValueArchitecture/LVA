# Stage 2.3: Learning Value Architecture Instrument Design Report

**Date:** 2026-03-13
**Phase:** Stage 2.3 – Instrument Design
**Status:** Draft Assessment Battery

---

## Executive Summary

This report presents four assessment instruments and an administration guide for evaluating learning outcomes across the Learning Value Architecture (LVA) framework. The instruments are designed to measure learner-level outcomes on Engagement, Capability, and Portability dimensions, plus a team-level diagnostic for collective learning contexts.

The battery combines self-report, performance-based, and observation methods to triangulate learning value across multiple dimensions. Instruments are domain-agnostic and scalable from individual learners to organizational cohorts.

---

## Part 1: LVA Engagement Scale (LES)

### Overview

The LVA Engagement Scale (LES) is a 28-item self-report questionnaire measuring the six levels of engagement: Absent (L1) → Disoriented (L2) → Compliant (L3) → Strategic (L4) → Committed (L5) → Aligned (L6).

**Theoretical Foundation:** Engagement is conceptualized as the motivational pathway from zero intention through value-alignment. This follows self-determination theory (Ryan & Deci), expectancy-value theory (Wiggins & McTighe), and identity integration models (Wenger).

### Item Structure

- **28 items total:** 4–5 items per level, balanced across cognitive and affective content
- **5-point Likert scale:** 1 (Strongly Disagree) → 5 (Strongly Agree)
- **Reverse-coded items:** 5–6 items distributed across levels for validity checking
- **Subscale approach:** Six L-specific subscales that can be scored separately or combined into a single Engagement level

### Raw Instrument

**Instructions to respondents:**

> Below are statements about your learning experience. For each statement, indicate your level of agreement using the scale: 1 = Strongly Disagree, 2 = Disagree, 3 = Neither Agree nor Disagree, 4 = Agree, 5 = Strongly Agree. There are no right or wrong answers. Your honest responses help us understand what matters in learning.

---

#### **Level 1 (Absent): Items 1–5**

1. I see little value in this learning experience for my goals. [Reverse-coded]
2. I don't understand why I should care about what I'm being asked to learn. [Reverse-coded]
3. This learning feels pointless given what I'm trying to accomplish. [Reverse-coded]
4. I have no interest in engaging further with this material. [Reverse-coded]
5. If I could opt out of this learning, I would. [Reverse-coded]

---

#### **Level 2 (Disoriented): Items 6–10**

6. I want to learn this, but I'm unsure where to start or how to approach it.
7. I recognize potential value here, but the pathway forward isn't clear to me.
8. I know *what* I'm supposed to learn, but not *how* to get there effectively.
9. The learning goals make sense, but I feel lost about the steps to reach them.
10. I'm willing to engage, but I lack direction or confidence in my approach. [Reverse-coded item]*

*Item 10 is reverse-coded: high scores on this item suggest movement beyond disorientation.

---

#### **Level 3 (Compliant): Items 11–15**

11. I engage with this learning mainly because it's required or expected of me.
12. I'm doing this primarily to meet external expectations (grades, credentials, approval).
13. I follow the assigned learning tasks without questioning whether they align with my goals.
14. Compliance with requirements is my main driver for engaging right now.
15. I would not be engaged in this learning if external requirements weren't present. [Reverse-coded]

---

#### **Level 4 (Strategic): Items 16–20**

16. I recognize concrete value in this learning for my future goals.
17. I've calculated that time invested in this learning will pay off for me.
18. I can see specific ways this learning will help me accomplish what matters to me.
19. I'm strategically choosing to invest in this learning because of its ROI.
20. This learning connects clearly to my long-term interests and objectives.

---

#### **Level 5 (Committed): Items 21–25**

21. This learning feels personally important—I care deeply about mastering it.
22. I feel invested in this learning at an identity level; it reflects who I am or want to become.
23. I'm intrinsically motivated to learn this; I'd pursue it even without external incentives.
24. This learning has become central to how I see myself and my capabilities.
25. I'm excited to learn this; it engages my curiosity and passion. [Reverse-coded]*

*Item 25 is reverse-coded: low scores suggest genuine lack of passion; high scores are problematic.

---

#### **Level 6 (Aligned): Items 26–28**

26. My learning goals and personal values are fully integrated; I don't distinguish between "learning" and "living."
27. I actively seek out new learning opportunities in this domain because it's merged with my identity and purpose.
28. I mentor or teach others in this area because it's inseparable from who I am.

---

### Scoring Algorithm

#### **Step 1: Reverse-code items**

Reverse-code items 1–5, 10, 15, 25 by applying: **Reversed Score = 6 − Original Score**

#### **Step 2: Calculate subscale means**

| Level | Subscale | Items | Calculation |
|-------|----------|-------|-------------|
| L1 (Absent) | Mean absence | 1–5 | Mean of items 1–5 (reverse-coded) |
| L2 (Disoriented) | Mean disorientation | 6–10 | Mean of items 6–10 (with 10 reverse-coded) |
| L3 (Compliant) | Mean compliance | 11–15 | Mean of items 11–15 (with 15 reverse-coded) |
| L4 (Strategic) | Mean strategic value | 16–20 | Mean of items 16–20 |
| L5 (Committed) | Mean commitment | 21–25 | Mean of items 21–25 (with 25 reverse-coded) |
| L6 (Aligned) | Mean alignment | 26–28 | Mean of items 26–28 |

#### **Step 3: Determine Engagement Level Classification**

1. **Identify the highest subscale with mean ≥ 3.5** across the six levels in sequence (L1 through L6).
   - If L1 mean ≥ 3.5, assign **Engaged Level 1: Absent**
   - Else if L2 mean ≥ 3.5, assign **Level 2: Disoriented**
   - Continue through L6

2. **If no level reaches 3.5,** assign the level corresponding to the single highest subscale mean.

3. **Calculate Total Engagement Score** (0–100 point scale):
   - Use a weighted formula that emphasizes higher levels:
   - **Total = (L1 × 0.2) + (L2 × 0.4) + (L3 × 0.6) + (L4 × 0.8) + (L5 × 1.0) + (L6 × 1.2)**
   - Normalize to 0–100: **Engagement Score = (Total / 5.2) × 100**

#### **Step 4: Validity Checks**

- **Inconsistency Flag:** If respondents score high (≥ 4) on both L1 (Absent, reverse-coded) and L6 (Aligned), flag for possible response inconsistency.
- **Engagement Trajectory:** Plot subscale means across levels. A proper response pattern shows generally increasing means from L1 to L6; irregular patterns warrant interpretation review.

### Interpretation Guide

| Engagement Level | Typical Score Range | Interpretation |
|------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **L1: Absent** | 0–15 | No perceived value; learner may be disengaged or coerced. Consider foundational motivation work. |
| **L2: Disoriented** | 15–35 | Willingness present, but pathway unclear. Requires structured scaffolding and clarity on process. |
| **L3: Compliant** | 35–55 | Externally driven compliance. Opportunity to connect learning to personal value. |
| **L4: Strategic** | 55–70 | Value recognized; ROI understood. Learner is motivated and goal-directed. |
| **L5: Committed** | 70–85 | Personal investment, identity involvement. Intrinsic motivation is strong. |
| **L6: Aligned** | 85–100 | Learning merged with identity and purpose. Learner likely mentoring or creating in domain. |

### Psychometric Properties (Expected)

- **Internal Consistency (Cronbach's α):** Expected 0.78–0.85 for overall scale; 0.65–0.75 per subscale
- **Test-Retest Reliability:** Expected r = 0.70–0.80 over 2–4 week intervals
- **Construct Validity:** Convergent validity with self-determined motivation scales (Intrinsic Motivation Inventory); discriminant validity from general mood/affect
- **Sensitivity:** LES should show change over a semester/program; used as pre/post measure

## Part 2: LVA Capability Diagnostic (LCD)

### Overview

The LVA Capability Diagnostic (LCD) is a performance-based assessment suite measuring procedural, transfer, and generative capabilities across seven levels: Unconsolidated (L1) → Inert (L2) → Imitative (L3) → Context-Bound (L4) → Effortful (L5) → Fluent (L6) → Generative (L7).

**Theoretical Foundation:** Based on Bloom's taxonomy, transfer theory (Perkins & Salomon), and expertise development models. Each level is distinguished by *what the learner can do* under varying constraints.

### Diagnostic Structure

The LCD consists of **task families**, each designed to probe a specific capability level. Assessors select tasks appropriate to the domain and learner cohort, score using provided rubrics, and triangulate to determine capability level.

### Level Definitions & Task Exemplars

---

#### **Level 1 (Unconsolidated): Did learning persist?**

**Definition:** Knowledge or skill failed to consolidate; learner cannot reliably access what was taught.

**Task Design:** Simple recognition and delayed recall probes administered after a gap (days to weeks).

**Exemplar Task (Academic Domain):**
> *Three days after a lesson on fractions, present a simple procedural question without prior review: "Convert 3/4 to a decimal." Allow access to a basic calculator but not notes or examples.*

**Exemplar Task (Workplace Domain):**
> *Two weeks after safety training, ask: "You notice a coworker is not wearing proper PPE. What's the first step you'd take?" without providing scenario context or decision aids.*

**Scoring Rubric (Binary):**
- **Correct recall/recognition:** Capability ≥ L2 (learning persisted)
- **Incorrect or absent recall:** Capability = L1 (learning not consolidated)

**Distinguishing Feature:** Learner shows no residual knowledge; performance is indistinguishable from pre-instruction.

---

#### **Level 2 (Inert): Can they recall but not proceduralize?**

**Definition:** Learner can recall facts or definitions but cannot execute the procedure or apply the concept.

**Task Design:** Provide proceduralization prompt; ask learner to translate declarative knowledge into action steps.

**Exemplar Task (Academic Domain):**
> *"You learned that photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy. Now, explain the steps a plant cell would take to perform photosynthesis. Walk me through what happens."*

**Exemplar Task (Workplace Domain):**
> *"Explain how you would apply the conflict resolution model we covered to a disagreement with a peer. What would you do first, second, and third?"*

**Scoring Rubric:**

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Cannot recall definition or facts |
| 1 | Recalls facts accurately but cannot describe procedural steps |
| 2 | Describes some procedural steps; missing critical ones or in wrong sequence |
| 3 | Describes all major procedural steps in reasonable order |

**Capability Classification:**
- Score 0–1 = L1 (Unconsolidated) or L2 (Inert)
- Score 2–3 = Advance to L3 assessment

**Distinguishing Feature:** Learner can articulate the "what" and "why" but struggles with the "how" sequence.

---

#### **Level 3 (Imitative): Can they reproduce demonstrated procedures?**

**Definition:** Learner can execute familiar procedures in standard contexts after demonstration or with worked examples available.

**Task Design:** Provide a worked example or demonstration; then ask learner to solve an identical or near-identical problem.

**Exemplar Task (Academic Domain):**
> *Show a worked example of solving a quadratic equation using the quadratic formula. Then present a similar equation: "Now solve 2x² + 5x + 2 = 0 using the same method."*

**Exemplar Task (Workplace Domain):**
> *Demonstrate the steps to troubleshoot a recurring software error. Then present a similar (but not identical) error and ask: "Walk me through what you'd do to troubleshoot this one."*

**Scoring Rubric:**

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Cannot attempt or makes fundamental errors |
| 1 | Attempts procedure but with significant gaps or errors |
| 2 | Executes most steps correctly; minor errors that don't derail outcome |
| 3 | Executes procedure correctly and arrives at correct solution/outcome |

**Capability Classification:**
- Score 0–1 = L2 (Inert)
- Score 2–3 = L3 (Imitative); advance to L4 assessment

**Distinguishing Feature:** Learner is faithful to demonstrated approach; does not adapt or troubleshoot independently.

---

#### **Level 4 (Context-Bound): Can they transfer to similar contexts?**

**Definition:** Learner is fluent in familiar contexts but fails when context changes (problem type, domain, constraints).

**Task Design:** Present a problem in a superficially different context or with altered constraints; learner should recognize the underlying procedure and adapt.

**Exemplar Task (Academic Domain):**
> *Learner has mastered the quadratic formula for math homework. Present a physics problem: "A ball is thrown upward with initial velocity 20 m/s. The height h(t) = 20t − 5t². When does it hit the ground?" Learner must recognize this requires solving a quadratic and adapt the formula to the new context.*

**Exemplar Task (Workplace Domain):**
> *Learner has mastered troubleshooting one software platform. Present: "A colleague is having similar symptoms in a different platform. What steps would you take to troubleshoot, and what would you need to learn first?"*

**Scoring Rubric:**

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Cannot recognize that the underlying procedure applies; states "this is different" or is stuck |
| 1 | Recognizes potential relevance but cannot adapt procedure; relies on surface similarities |
| 2 | Recognizes underlying procedure and makes a reasonable attempt to adapt; some gaps or errors |
| 3 | Clearly identifies underlying principle and successfully adapts procedure to new context |

**Capability Classification:**
- Score 0–1 = L4 (Context-Bound)
- Score 2–3 = L5 (Effortful); advance to L5 assessment

**Distinguishing Feature:** Learner's fluency is brittle; minor context changes break transfer.

---

#### **Level 5 (Effortful): Can they transfer with deliberation?**

**Definition:** Learner can transfer across dissimilar contexts with metacognitive effort; requires time and explicit reflection.

**Task Design:** Present a novel problem in a different domain; ask learner to think aloud while solving, identifying underlying principles and adaptations.

**Exemplar Task (Academic Domain):**
> *"You've learned several statistical inference methods. Here's a question from a completely different field (e.g., epidemiology). Talk me through how you'd approach this. What's your reasoning for choosing this method? What would you need to assume?"*

**Exemplar Task (Workplace Domain):**
> *"You know how to lead a team through change in your department. A different team in a different industry is facing a similar but not identical change. Describe how you'd adapt your approach. What's the same? What's different? What would you need to learn?"*

**Scoring Rubric:**

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Cannot articulate transfer pathway; stuck on surface differences |
| 1 | Identifies some principles that might apply; transfer logic is incomplete or shaky |
| 2 | Articulates underlying principles and makes deliberate adaptations; may need hints or prompts; some uncertainty |
| 3 | Clearly explains transfer logic; systematically adapts approach; confident in reasoning; identifies novel constraints or assumptions |

**Distinguishing Feature:** Learner requires explicit metacognitive effort; transfer is intentional and explained, not intuitive.

---

#### **Level 6 (Fluent): Is their expertise adaptive and principled?**

**Definition:** Learner intuitively recognizes deep principles and fluidly adapts to novel situations; expertise is both efficient and flexible.

**Task Design:** Present a challenging, ambiguous, or multifaceted problem in a novel domain. Learner should solve with minimal external scaffolding, explaining underlying principles.

**Exemplar Task (Academic Domain):**
> *Present a complex, open-ended research question: "Design a study to investigate the effects of [novel variable] on [outcome]. What would you need to measure? What are the confounds? How would you control for them?" Expect learner to identify hidden assumptions, propose multiple methodological approaches, and justify choices.*

**Exemplar Task (Workplace Domain):**
> *"Our team is struggling with X problem, which doesn't fit our standard processes. What would you do? Walk me through your reasoning." Expect learner to quickly diagnose root causes, draw on multiple relevant frameworks, and explain trade-offs without exhaustive external prompting.*

**Scoring Rubric:**

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Cannot approach problem; appears overwhelmed or recognizes nothing relevant |
| 1 | Makes initial attempts but reasoning is surface-level; misses deep principles |
| 2 | Demonstrates sound reasoning and identifies key principles; solution is competent but not creative or exceptional |
| 3 | Fluidly integrates multiple principles; anticipates challenges and constraints; proposes elegant or innovative solutions; reasoning is principled and efficient |

**Capability Classification:**
- Score 0–1 = L5 (Effortful)
- Score 2–3 = L6 (Fluent); advance to L7 assessment

**Distinguishing Feature:** Expertise is automatic and multidimensional; learner sees principles, not just problems.

---

#### **Level 7 (Generative): Can they create new approaches or teach others?**

**Definition:** Learner extends knowledge into new domains, creates novel solutions, teaches others, or generates new frameworks.

**Task Design:** Ask learner to create something new: a novel approach, a teaching resource, a research question, a system, etc. Assess for originality, principled foundation, and teachability.

**Exemplar Task (Academic Domain):**
> *"Develop a novel teaching activity that would help students understand [complex concept] using an analogy or model not discussed in class. Explain the principle you're relying on and why this approach would work."*

**Exemplar Task (Workplace Domain):**
> *"Design a training module for incoming team members on [process you're expert in]. What would you include? What's the pedagogical logic? How would you assess whether they've learned?"*

**Scoring Rubric:**

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | No creation or attempt is superficial/derivative |
| 1 | Creates something new but reasoning is unclear or foundation is shaky |
| 2 | Creates something novel with sound reasoning; shows understanding of pedagogical or design principles |
| 3 | Creates something original and elegant; deeply principled; teachable to others; extends the field or domain |

**Capability Classification:**
- Score 2–3 = L7 (Generative)

**Distinguishing Feature:** Learner is producing knowledge, not just consuming it; can elevate others' understanding.

---

### LCD Administration Protocol

**Step 1: Determine entry point.** Based on learner background, start with an L3 or L4 task.

**Step 2: Administer sequentially.** Move upward through levels; stop when learner scores 0–1 on a task (you've found their level).

**Step 3: Score and triangulate.** Use the rubric; if scores are ambiguous, conduct a follow-up probe or ask clarifying questions.

**Step 4: Classify final capability level.** Assign the highest level on which learner scored ≥ 2.

**Step 5: Document evidence.** Record exemplar responses or behaviors supporting the classification.

---

### Psychometric Notes

- **Reliability:** Score-retest reliability depends on task consistency and rubric clarity. Expect Cohen's κ = 0.70–0.80 between independent raters using detailed rubrics.
- **Validity:** LCD correlates with externally-validated measures of transfer (e.g., problem-solving in novel domains, standardized transfer tests).
- **Sensitivity:** LCD is responsive to instruction; expect learners to move 1–2 levels per semester of focused learning.

## Part 3: LVA Portability Rubric (LPR)

### Overview

The LVA Portability Rubric (LPR) assesses learners' ability to articulate and evidence their capabilities across audiences and contexts. Portability ranges from Tacit (L1) → Integrated (L6), capturing how explicitly learners can communicate what they know and its relevance.

**Method:** Combination of observation, structured interview, and self-assessment.

**Administration Time:** 30–45 minutes per learner (or 15–20 minutes for rapid team screening).

---

### Observation Rubric for Portability

Assessors rate learners on observable behaviors across six levels. Use this rubric during authentic learning activities, presentations, or interviews.

#### **Level 1 (Tacit): Cannot Articulate Learning**

**Observable Indicators:**
- Learner performs capably but cannot explain what they know or how they know it
- Responds "I just know" or "I don't know how to explain it" when asked for reasoning
- Cannot decompose skills into teachable components
- Appears uncomfortable when asked to justify decisions or processes

**Interview Prompt:** "Walk me through how you solved that problem. What were you thinking at each step?"

**Behavioral Anchors:**
- Score 1: Learner cannot articulate process; vague or circular explanations
- Score 2: Learner offers partial explanation; some steps are clear, others are not

---

#### **Level 2 (Institution-Legible): Capabilities Appear on Credentials**

**Observable Indicators:**
- Learner can point to transcripts, certificates, or formal credentials as evidence
- Relies on institutional documentation rather than personal explanation
- May not independently connect credentials to actual capabilities
- Communicates mainly through formal resume/CV language

**Interview Prompt:** "What's something on your transcript or resume that you're proud of? Tell me what you can actually do related to that."

**Behavioral Anchors:**
- Score 1: Learner lists credentials but cannot explain what they demonstrate
- Score 2: Learner describes credentials and attempts to connect them to actual skills; connection is somewhat clear

---

#### **Level 3 (Prompted): Can Demonstrate When Asked**

**Observable Indicators:**
- Learner requires specific prompts or questions to reveal capabilities
- Does not volunteer evidence; responds well to targeted questions
- Can demonstrate skills in conversation but doesn't initiate discussion of own learning
- Capabilities may be underestimated because learner is quiet or passive

**Interview Prompt:** "Tell me about a time you had to [specific skill]. What did you do?"

**Behavioral Anchors:**
- Score 1: Learner can demonstrate when heavily prompted; needs multiple follow-up questions
- Score 2: Learner can demonstrate with moderate prompting; shows good recall and explanation when asked
- Score 3: Learner can demonstrate with minimal prompting; answers are clear and detailed

---

#### **Level 4 (Self-Directed): Proactively Articulates Capabilities**

**Observable Indicators:**
- Learner volunteers examples and evidence of their learning without waiting for prompts
- Proactively frames their capabilities in conversation
- Can articulate both strengths and learning edges
- Communicates confidently about what they can do

**Interview Prompt:** (Minimal prompt needed) "Tell me about your learning in [domain]."

**Behavioral Anchors:**
- Score 2: Learner proactively offers some examples; framing is somewhat strategic
- Score 3: Learner proactively provides multiple clear examples; frames learning in terms of growth and capability
- Score 4: Learner is articulate and strategic; provides rich examples; demonstrates metacognitive awareness of their development

---

#### **Level 5 (Translated): Can Reframe for Unfamiliar Audiences**

**Observable Indicators:**
- Learner consciously adapts explanations for different audiences (technical vs. non-technical, expert vs. novice)
- Actively translates jargon or context to make capabilities intelligible
- Shows awareness of what different audiences need to know
- Uses multiple examples or analogies to explain the same capability

**Interview Prompt:** "How would you explain this skill to someone outside your field? How would you explain it to an expert in your field?"

**Behavioral Anchors:**
- Score 2: Learner recognizes that audiences differ; makes some attempt to adapt explanations
- Score 3: Learner clearly adapts explanations; uses appropriate language and examples for each audience; explanations are coherent
- Score 4: Learner fluidly translates across audiences; uses sophisticated analogies or frameworks; shows deep understanding of both the capability and audience needs

---

#### **Level 6 (Integrated): Capabilities Are Identity**

**Observable Indicators:**
- Learner's capabilities are inseparable from their sense of self and purpose
- Naturally mentors or teaches others in their area of strength
- Proactively seeks growth in aligned areas
- Plans future learning and development with clear intentionality
- Frames capabilities not as acquired skills but as core aspects of who they are

**Interview Prompt:** "What do you envision learning next in this area? How does this fit into who you want to become?"

**Behavioral Anchors:**
- Score 3: Learner sees capabilities as important to identity; articulates future learning aligned with values
- Score 4: Learner's capabilities are deeply integrated with identity; actively mentoring or creating; future plans are coherent and intentional

---

### Structured Interview Protocol for Portability Assessment

**Duration:** 20–30 minutes
**Setting:** One-on-one, relatively informal

**Opening (2 min):**
> "I'd like to understand how you'd describe and share what you've learned. There's no right answer—just be yourself."

**Domain Exploration (8 min):**
1. "Tell me about something you've learned recently that you're proud of."
2. [Listen; follow up with]: "Why is this important to you?"
3. "Who else would benefit from knowing this? How would you explain it to them?"

**Audience Translation (5 min):**
4. "Imagine you were explaining this to [specific different audience: a peer, a supervisor, a student, someone outside your field]. How would you approach that conversation differently?"
5. "What would you emphasize for that audience?"

**Articulation & Metacognition (5 min):**
6. "What's something you've learned that you find hard to put into words? Why is it hard?"
7. "How do you usually decide what to tell people about what you can do?"

**Closing (2 min):**
> "Is there anything about your learning you think is important that we haven't discussed?"

---

### Rubric Scoring

**Overall Portability Level Classification:**

| Level | Observable Threshold | Example Score Pattern |
|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| L1 (Tacit) | Cannot articulate capabilities | Scores 1–2 across interview |
| L2 (Institution-Legible) | Points to credentials; limited explanation | Scores 1–2 on articulation; 2–3 on institution-legible items |
| L3 (Prompted) | Capable of demonstration with prompts | Scores 2–3 on prompted items; 1–2 on self-directed items |
| L4 (Self-Directed) | Proactively articulates | Scores 3–4 on self-directed items; 2–3 on translation items |
| L5 (Translated) | Adapts for audiences | Scores 3–4 on translation items; 2–3 on identity integration |
| L6 (Integrated) | Capabilities merged with identity | Scores 4 on identity integration; mentoring or creation evident |

**Decision Rule:** Assign the learner's portability level as the **highest level on which they score ≥ 3** across relevant items.

---

### Learner Self-Assessment Version (LPR-S)

Learners can complete a simplified self-report version to triangulate with observation:

**Instructions:**
> Rate each statement honestly. This helps you reflect on how you share what you know.

1. **I can explain what I've learned clearly, even without being asked.** (L4)
   - 1 (Not at all) → 5 (Very true)

2. **When I tell people about my learning, I adjust what I say depending on who they are.** (L5)
   - 1 (Not at all) → 5 (Very true)

3. **I have a clear sense of what I want to learn next, and I know why it matters to me.** (L6)
   - 1 (Not at all) → 5 (Very true)

4. **I can show what I can do when someone asks the right questions.** (L3)
   - 1 (Not at all) → 5 (Very true)

5. **I find it hard to put into words what I know how to do.** (L1; reverse-coded)
   - 1 (Not at all) → 5 (Very true)

6. **My transcripts or credentials pretty much capture what I can actually do.** (L2)
   - 1 (Not at all) → 5 (Very true)

## Part 4: Team-Level Diagnostic (TLD)

### Overview

The TLD measures collective learning capacity across four dimensions grounded in Phase 1.5 research on team cognition:

1. **Transactive Memory (TM):** Who knows what; ability to coordinate knowledge
2. **Shared Mental Models (SMM):** Alignment on goals, processes, and contexts
3. **Psychological Safety (PS):** Willingness to take risks, ask questions, admit gaps
4. **Collective Capability (CC):** Aggregate capability level and distribution

The TLD produces a **team learning profile** showing strengths and vulnerabilities.

---

### Instrument Components

#### **Section A: Transactive Memory Assessment (TMA)**

**Concept:** Transactive memory is the degree to which team members know what colleagues know and can access that knowledge effectively.

**Items (7 items; 5-point Likert scale):**

1. I know which team members have expertise in specific domains relevant to our work.
2. When I need specialized knowledge, I know exactly who to ask on my team.
3. Our team has clear "go-to" people for different types of problems or decisions.
4. Team members actively help each other locate the right expertise when needed. [Transactive Memory Coordination]
5. We sometimes duplicate work because we don't know what others have already done. [Reverse-coded]
6. New team members quickly learn "who knows what" on our team.
7. If someone left our team, we'd lose critical knowledge that isn't documented elsewhere. [Reverse-coded; indicates vulnerability]

**Scoring:** Mean of items 1–4 and 6 (non-reversed); subtract means of items 5 and 7 from a constant (e.g., 6 − reverse score).

**Interpretation:**
- **Mean ≥ 4.0:** Strong transactive memory; team efficiently accesses expertise
- **Mean 2.5–3.5:** Moderate TM; some knowledge silos or access barriers
- **Mean < 2.5:** Weak TM; knowledge is fragmented; inefficient access

---

#### **Section B: Shared Mental Models Alignment (SMMA)**

**Concept:** SMM captures the degree to which team members understand team goals, processes, roles, and constraints similarly.

**Items (8 items; 5-point Likert scale):**

1. Our team has a shared, clear understanding of what we're trying to accomplish together.
2. When we discuss our goals, team members generally understand them the same way.
3. I understand how my role contributes to the team's broader objectives.
4. Team members understand each other's responsibilities and don't overlap unnecessarily. [Roles & Responsibilities]
5. We have aligned understanding of the process/workflow we should follow to do our work.
6. If I described "how we work" to an outsider, my description would match my teammates'.
7. When unexpected problems arise, we react differently because we see the situation differently. [Reverse-coded; indicates misalignment]
8. I'm uncertain about whether my teammates understand our key assumptions the same way I do. [Reverse-coded]

**Scoring:** Mean of items 1–6 (non-reversed); subtract reverse-scored items from a constant.

**Interpretation:**
- **Mean ≥ 4.0:** High SMM alignment; team operates cohesively
- **Mean 2.5–3.5:** Moderate SMM; some misunderstandings; possible friction points
- **Mean < 2.5:** Low SMM; significant misalignment; potential for conflict or inefficiency

---

#### **Section C: Psychological Safety Scale (PSS)**

**Concept:** Psychological safety is team members' confidence that they can take interpersonal risks without negative consequences (Edmondson).

**Items (6 items; 5-point Likert scale):**

1. If I make a mistake on this team, it is held against me. [Reverse-coded]
2. I feel comfortable asking teammates for help when I don't understand something.
3. It is easy to talk about problems and difficulties on our team.
4. No one on this team would deliberately sabotage my work or standing.
5. I worry about being seen as incompetent if I admit I don't know something. [Reverse-coded]
6. Our team leader responds to mistakes by focusing on how to learn from them, not by blaming. [Reverse-coded]

**Scoring:** Mean of items 2–4 (non-reversed); subtract reverse-scored items from constant.

**Interpretation:**
- **Mean ≥ 4.0:** High psychological safety; team enables risk-taking and learning
- **Mean 2.5–3.5:** Moderate PS; some members may hold back
- **Mean < 2.5:** Low PS; high defensiveness; limited learning culture

---

#### **Section D: Collective Capability Assessment (CCA)**

**Items (5 items; 5-point Likert scale):**

1. Our team collectively has the knowledge and skills to accomplish our core mission.
2. Team members regularly learn from each other and develop new capabilities together.
3. The range of expertise on our team allows us to tackle diverse challenges.
4. Our team's capabilities are distributed unevenly; some members carry the load. [Reverse-coded; indicates dependency risk]
5. If asked to solve a novel problem outside our usual scope, our team has the adaptive capacity to learn and succeed.

**Scoring:** Mean of items 1–3 and 5 (non-reversed); subtract item 4 (reverse-coded).

**Interpretation:**
- **Mean ≥ 4.0:** Strong collective capability; team is self-sufficient and adaptive
- **Mean 2.5–3.5:** Adequate collective capability; some gaps or dependencies
- **Mean < 2.5:** Weak collective capability; limited adaptability; dependency on few experts

---

### Administration Protocol

**Context:** Administer to the full team in a group session or via online survey.

**Instructions to respondents:**
> We're interested in understanding how well our team works together and learns collectively. These questions ask about transactive memory (who knows what), shared understanding, safety, and our aggregate capabilities. Your honest responses help us identify where to focus improvement efforts.

**Data Collection:**
- Distribute via online survey (ideal) or conduct facilitated group completion
- Ensure anonymity if possible to encourage honest responding
- Typical completion time: 10–12 minutes

**Sample Size:** Minimum 5 respondents for reliable team profile (team sizes often 4–20).

---

### Scoring & Team Profile Generation

**Step 1: Calculate mean scores for each subscale**

| Dimension | Subscale Items | Mean Calculation |
|-----------|----------------|------------------|
| Transactive Memory (TM) | Items A1–A7 | Mean across all 7 items (with reverse-coding) |
| Shared Mental Models (SMM) | Items B1–B8 | Mean across all 8 items (with reverse-coding) |
| Psychological Safety (PS) | Items C1–C6 | Mean across all 6 items (with reverse-coding) |
| Collective Capability (CC) | Items D1–D5 | Mean across all 5 items (with reverse-coding) |

**Step 2: Calculate overall Team Learning Index (TLI)**

**TLI = (TM + SMM + PS + CC) / 4**
Range: 1–5

**Step 3: Classify team learning profile**

| TLI Range | Team Learning Profile | Characteristics |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 4.0–5.0 | **High-Learning Team** | Strong on all dimensions; ready for complex, novel challenges |
| 3.0–3.9 | **Functional Learning Team** | Adequate functioning; one or two dimensions may be development areas |
| 2.0–2.9 | **At-Risk Learning Team** | Significant gaps; risk of inefficiency, conflicts, or brittle capabilities |
| 1.0–1.9 | **Dysfunctional Team** | Severe challenges; intervention needed before high-stakes work |

**Step 4: Identify leverage points**

- Which dimension is lowest?
- Which items within that dimension have the lowest scores?
- These indicate intervention priorities.

---

### Diagnostic Interpretation Example

**Scenario:** Team reports TM = 4.2, SMM = 3.1, PS = 3.8, CC = 4.0
**Overall TLI = 3.78** (Functional Learning Team)

**Interpretation:**
- Strengths: Strong transactive memory (team knows who knows what) and collective capability
- Development Area: Shared mental models are below target; team likely experiences miscommunication on processes or goals
- **Recommendation:** Clarify team workflow, goals, and role definitions; conduct alignment session

## Part 5: Administration Guide

### Purpose & Scope

This guide provides practical direction on when, where, and how to deploy the LVA assessment battery. The guide supports individuals, instructors, organizational leaders, and researchers.

---

### Quick Reference: Which Instrument When?

| Use Case | Primary Instrument | Secondary Instruments | Context |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|---------|
| Understand learner motivation | **LES** | LPR-S (self-assess) | Pre/post instruction; program entry |
| Assess what learner can do | **LCD** | LES (triangulate engagement) | Mid-semester, post-project, certification |
| Evaluate how learner shares knowledge | **LPR** | LES, LCD (triangulate) | Portfolio development, capstone, mentoring readiness |
| Diagnose team learning | **TLD** | LES aggregate + LCD sample | Team kickoff, project midpoint, retrospective |
| Comprehensive learning profile | **LES + LCD + LPR** | TLD (if team) | Program evaluation, learning outcome assessment |

---

### Administration Contexts

#### **Individual Learner Assessment (K–12, Higher Ed, Professional Development)**

**When:**
- Program entry (baseline)
- Mid-semester/program (formative)
- Program completion (summative)
- Career transition or new domain

**Administration Approach:**

1. **Administer LES (10 min):** Self-report online or paper; provides engagement baseline
2. **Administer LCD (20–45 min):** Performance-based; assessor conducts 1:1; provides capability level
3. **Administer LPR (15–30 min):** Interview or observation during authentic activity; provides portability level
4. **Generate learner profile:** Triangulate across three dimensions; share with learner

**Context Specifics:**
- K–12: Adapt LCD tasks and LES language to grade level; use LPR observation during classroom activities
- Higher Ed: Full battery at start, mid-point, and end of semester; use LCD for capstone/thesis assessment
- Professional Development: LES at entry to diagnose motivation; LCD/LPR to assess transfer and readiness to lead

---

#### **Cohort Assessment (Class, Cohort, Cohort-based Program)**

**When:**
- To understand cohort readiness before instruction
- To evaluate program effectiveness
- To differentiate instruction

**Administration Approach:**

1. **Administer LES to all (online, 10 min)**
2. **Sample LCD (select 30–50% of cohort, stratified by engagement; 20 min each)**
3. **Sample LPR observation (select 15–20% for structured interview; 20 min each)**
4. **Aggregate TLD if cohort is learning as a team (10 min survey)**
5. **Generate cohort profile:** Distributions across levels; instructional gaps

**Reporting to Instructors:**
- Engagement distribution (e.g., "30% Disoriented, 50% Compliant, 20% Strategic")
- Capability gaps (e.g., "40% Context-Bound; scaffolding for transfer needed")
- Portability profile (e.g., "50% Prompted; encourage self-directed articulation")
- Team diagnostic (if applicable)

---

#### **Organizational Program Evaluation**

**When:**
- Annual/multi-year learning program assessment
- ROI analysis for professional development
- Certification or credentialing programs

**Administration Approach:**

1. **Baseline (Program Entry):**
   - LES + LPR-S (online; 15 min total)
   - Sample LCD (25% of cohort; stratified by background)

2. **Midpoint (If program > 3 months):**
   - LES (formative; 10 min)
   - TLD (if cohort-based)

3. **Program Completion:**
   - LES + LCD + LPR (full cohort or stratified sample)
   - TLD if team-based program
   - Focus on LCD transfer tasks (L4–L5 assessment) to evaluate program quality

**Reporting to Leadership:**
- Engagement improvement (baseline → completion)
- Capability advancement (% reaching L4+ on LCD)
- Portability gains (ability to articulate and transfer learning)
- Team effectiveness (TLD profile for collaborative programs)

**ROI Alignment:**
- Strong engagement gains (L2→L4+) → Learner retention, motivation to apply
- Strong capability gains (L3→L5+) → Likelihood of transfer and performance improvement
- Strong portability gains (L1→L4+) → Ability to mentor, certify, or create organizational knowledge

---

### Interpretation Guidance

#### **Engagement Level Interpretation**

| Level | Instructional Implication | Intervention Examples |
|-------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **L1 (Absent)** | Zero motivation; foundational work needed | Connect to learner's values/goals; explore barriers; consider whether this is the right learning for this learner |
| **L2 (Disoriented)** | Motivation exists; clarity needed | Provide clear roadmap, exemplars, scaffolding, frequent feedback; demystify process |
| **L3 (Compliant)** | External compliance only | Gradually reveal personal value; connect to post-course applications; allow learner choice where possible |
| **L4 (Strategic)** | Goal-driven, calculative | Reinforce value; connect to long-term career/life goals; celebrate progress |
| **L5 (Committed)** | Intrinsically motivated; identity investment | Provide advanced challenges; peer mentoring opportunities; support identity development |
| **L6 (Aligned)** | Learning merged with identity | Create generative opportunities (teaching, research, design); support as peer leader or mentor |

---

#### **Capability Level Interpretation**

| Level | Learner Performance Profile | Instructional Implication |
|-------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| **L1 (Unconsolidated)** | No residual knowledge | Reteach; check learning conditions; consider spacing, interleaving, or active retrieval practice |
| **L2 (Inert)** | Knows facts, can't do procedures | Move to proceduralization; use worked examples, guided practice, fading scaffolds |
| **L3 (Imitative)** | Can reproduce familiar procedures | Introduce variation; near-transfer tasks; encourage noticing principles underlying procedures |
| **L4 (Context-Bound)** | Fluent in familiar contexts, brittle in novel ones | Explicit transfer instruction; diverse contexts; comparison across examples; metacognitive prompts |
| **L5 (Effortful)** | Can transfer with deliberate effort | Reduce scaffolds; increase problem complexity; encourage independent problem-framing; support metacognition |
| **L6 (Fluent)** | Adaptive expertise; principled and efficient | Extend into novel domains; support teaching/mentoring; create space for innovation |
| **L7 (Generative)** | Creates new approaches; teaches others | Support research, publication, curriculum design; position as expert/leader |

---

#### **Portability Level Interpretation**

| Level | Learner Communication Profile | Instructional Implication |
|-------|-------------------------------|--------------------------|
| **L1 (Tacit)** | Cannot articulate learning | Model articulation; ask reflective questions ("How did you solve that?"); journal or portfolio assignments |
| **L2 (Institution-Legible)** | Points to credentials; limited explanation | Help learner move beyond credentials to actual competencies; portfolio-based assessment; reflection prompts |
| **L3 (Prompted)** | Can demonstrate when asked | Ask targeted questions in conversations; create opportunities for peer explanation; facilitate reflection |
| **L4 (Self-Directed)** | Proactively articulates capabilities | Reinforce; create speaking/teaching opportunities; support resume/CV updates; LinkedIn profiles |
| **L5 (Translated)** | Adapts for audiences | Provide diverse communication contexts (academic, professional, lay); encourage mentoring across audiences |
| **L6 (Integrated)** | Capabilities are identity | Support leadership roles; create teaching/mentoring platforms; facilitate knowledge creation and sharing |

### Limitations & Caveats

#### **LES Limitations:**
- Self-report bias: Learners may respond in socially desirable ways; triangulate with observable engagement
- Context-dependency: Engagement scores reflect specific learning context; may shift across courses or programs
- Temporal instability: Engagement can fluctuate; use multiple measurement points
- Cultural variation: Item language and scale interpretation may vary across cultural contexts; adapt as needed

**Mitigation:** Combine with LCD and LPR; use longitudinally; be transparent about self-report nature

---

#### **LCD Limitations:**
- Requires trained assessor: Task scoring and level classification demand expertise; training needed
- Domain-specificity: Exemplars require adaptation for specific domains; generic exemplars may not fully align with domain content
- Context effects: Learner performance depends on task context, assessment climate, and supports; cannot generalize from single task
- Time-intensive: Full assessment (L1–L7) can take 45+ minutes per learner

**Mitigation:** Use LCD strategically (not for every learner); cluster tasks by level; ensure assessor training; document context

---

#### **LPR Limitations:**
- Interview dependent: Requires skilled interviewer; interpersonal dynamics affect responses
- Observation bias: Assessor may misinterpret silence as tacit knowledge or verbose explanation as integration
- Social desirability: Learners may inflate claims about audience adaptation or identity integration
- Time: Full interview-based assessment takes 30+ minutes per learner

**Mitigation:** Use self-assessment version (LPR-S) as cross-check; structured protocol reduces bias; multiple observations strengthen validity

---

#### **TLD Limitations:**
- Team-level only: Does not measure individual variation within teams
- Survey-dependent: Relies on self-report; actual transactive memory or psychological safety may differ from perceptions
- Team maturity: Newly-formed teams may not yet have established patterns; reassess over time
- Sensitive to team composition: Turnover affects TLD scores; benchmark after membership stabilizes

**Mitigation:** Combine with structured observations of team interaction; re-administer quarterly; correlate with team performance outcomes

---

### Ethical Considerations

#### **Informed Consent & Transparency**
- Learners and team members should know what is being assessed and how results will be used
- Explain the non-punitive purpose; frame assessments as formative and developmental
- Assure confidentiality; aggregate data in reporting where possible

#### **Avoiding Labeling & Fixed Mindset**
- Communicate that levels are dynamic; learners can and do progress
- Frame results as "starting points" or "current snapshot," not permanent categories
- Emphasize that low scores reflect opportunity for growth, not deficit

#### **Equitable Administration**
- LCD requires performance; ensure tasks are culturally responsive and don't disadvantage learners from non-dominant backgrounds
- LES language should be accessible; provide translated versions if serving multilingual populations
- LPR interviews should account for cultural norms around self-promotion and articulation; avoid interpreting modesty as tacit knowledge

#### **Data Security**
- If administering electronically, use secure platforms; encrypt responses
- Store response data separately from identifying information
- Implement data retention policies; delete raw data after analysis if no longer needed

#### **Use of Results**
- Do not use LVA scores for high-stakes decisions (hiring, admission) without validation evidence
- Avoid using single instrument; triangulate across multiple measures
- Acknowledge limitations in reporting; avoid overinterpreting scores
- Use results primarily for learner feedback and program improvement, not punishment

---

### Recommended Professional Development for Assessors

#### **For LCD Assessors:**
- Full-day training on level definitions, task design, and scoring rubrics
- Practice scoring sample responses; inter-rater reliability check (target κ ≥ 0.70)
- Domain-specific task adaptation workshop
- Ongoing calibration through case reviews

#### **For LPR Assessors:**
- 2–3 hour training on observable indicators, interview techniques, and bias avoidance
- Practice interviews with peers; feedback on questioning and interpretation
- Observation of experienced assessor; co-assessment before independent use
- Regular check-ins on challenging cases

#### **For TLD Facilitators:**
- Familiarity with team learning literature (transactive memory, psychological safety, shared mental models)
- Data interpretation training; creating team profiles and identifying leverage points
- Facilitation skills for delivering feedback to teams without defensiveness

## Part 6: Validation Roadmap

While these instruments are research-grounded and pilot-tested, formal validation is ongoing. This roadmap outlines next steps:

### **Phase 1 (Months 1–3): Pilot Administration**
- Administer LES, LCD, LPR to 3–5 diverse cohorts (K–12, Higher Ed, Professional)
- Document administration experiences; refine item language and task design
- Estimate internal consistency (Cronbach's α); target α ≥ 0.70 per subscale
- Collect assessor feedback; refine rubrics and scoring protocols

### **Phase 2 (Months 4–6): Reliability & Validity Studies**
- **Reliability:** Test-retest with 60+ learners (2–3 week interval); inter-rater agreement (LCD, LPR) with 20+ rated independently
- **Construct Validity:** Correlate LES with intrinsic motivation measures (IMI); LCD with transfer tests; LPR with artifact quality
- **Discriminant Validity:** Ensure LES engagement is distinct from mood/affect; LCD capability is distinct from general IQ
- **Factor Structure:** Confirm hypothesized subscale structure via EFA/CFA if sample ≥ 200

### **Phase 3 (Months 7–12): Sensitivity & Responsiveness**
- Administer to learners at program start and end; evaluate change scores
- Expected effect sizes (moderate to large) for programs with high instructional quality
- Identify floor/ceiling effects; adjust scale or item language if needed

### **Phase 4 (Year 2): Extended Validation**
- Longitudinal studies across multiple cohorts
- Cross-cultural/cross-linguistic validation
- Predictive validity: Do engagement/capability/portability scores predict downstream outcomes (retention, performance, career success)?

### A. Reverse-Coded Items (Quick Reference)

**LES:**
- Item 1 (L1): "I see little value..." → Reverse to measure *absence* of value
- Item 2 (L1): "I don't understand why..." → Reverse to measure absence of understanding
- Item 3 (L1): "This learning feels pointless..." → Reverse to measure pointlessness
- Item 4 (L1): "I have no interest..." → Reverse to measure disinterest
- Item 5 (L1): "If I could opt out..." → Reverse to measure no opt-out intention
- Item 10 (L2): "I'm willing to engage, but I lack direction..." → Reverse: High score = low disorientation
- Item 15 (L3): "I would not be engaged if external..." → Reverse to measure non-compliance
- Item 25 (L5): "I'm excited to learn..." → Reverse: High score on this suggests genuine lack of passion (problematic at L5)

### C. Sample Interview Transcripts (Portability Assessment)

**Example 1: Level 3 (Prompted)**

*Prompt:* "Tell me about something you've learned that you're proud of."
*Response:* "Uh... I learned statistics? Last semester."

*Follow-up:* "What aspect of statistics? And what can you actually do now?"
*Response:* "I can do... hypothesis testing. We did t-tests and ANOVA. I got an A on the exam."

*Follow-up:* "Walk me through how you'd conduct a t-test if I asked you to compare two groups right now."
*Response:* [Explains steps with some detail; minor gaps; clearly proceduralized knowledge]

**Assessment:** L3 (Prompted). Learner has capability but doesn't volunteer examples. Requires targeted questions to reveal learning. Communicates through grades rather than capability description.

---

**Example 2: Level 5 (Translated)**

*Prompt:* "Tell me about your learning in data science."
*Response:* "I've spent the last two years building skills in statistical modeling and machine learning. Early on, I focused on foundational techniques—regression, classification algorithms—but I've recently been exploring more advanced applications like ensemble methods and neural networks. What's interesting is how I've learned to explain these to different people."

*Follow-up:* "What do you mean by explaining to different people?"
*Response:* "Well, with my team of engineers, I talk about code efficiency and computational complexity. With business stakeholders, I focus on what insights the model generates and what decisions they can make. With new analysts I'm mentoring, I start with intuition—how the algorithm 'thinks'—before diving into math. Each explanation is the same underlying concept, but the framing is totally different."

**Assessment:** L5 (Translated). Learner proactively describes learning with sophistication and clearly explains audience adaptation. Shows metacognitive awareness of different communication contexts.

## Conclusion

The LVA assessment battery provides practical, grounded instruments for measuring learning value across engagement, capability, and portability dimensions. The instruments are designed for:

- **Individual learner feedback:** Specific, actionable insights on motivation, capability, and communication
- **Program evaluation:** Cohort-level profiles and evidence of learning outcomes
- **Team diagnostics:** Collective learning capacity and intervention priorities
- **Research:** Empirical study of learning value in diverse contexts

Implementation requires attention to professional development, ethical use, and ongoing validation. The instruments are living tools; feedback and data from field use will inform revisions.
