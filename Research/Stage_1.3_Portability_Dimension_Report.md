## Overview: The Portability Dimension

The Portability dimension addresses a fundamental question in learning assessment: **Does the learner know they have a capability, and can the world see it?** This dimension assumes that real capability exists (Capability L3+) and measures the transparency and communicability of that capability across contexts.

### The Six Levels of Portability

- **L1 Tacit:** "I can do things I couldn't before, but I couldn't tell you what exactly."
- **L2 Institution-Legible:** "My transcript shows a 3.8, but I'm not sure what that means out there."
- **L3 Prompted:** "Ask me the right questions and I'll impress you."
- **L4 Self-Directed:** "Here's what I can do, here's the evidence, here's why it matters to you."
- **L5 Translated:** "You'd never guess where I learned this — let me show you why it's exactly what you need."
- **L6 Integrated:** "This isn't something I learned. It's how I think. And I know what I need next."

Progression across these levels represents increasing agency, intentionality, and communicative sophistication in how learners make their capabilities visible and actionable.

## 1. Tacit Knowledge Research (L1)

### Polanyi's Tacit Knowledge

Michael Polanyi's foundational principle—"we can know more than we can tell"—remains central to understanding L1. Tacit knowledge is fundamentally embodied and experiential. It emerges from practice, is difficult to articulate, and often resists formal representation. A musician who can play a complex passage flawlessly but cannot explain the mechanics of their finger movements, a clinician who diagnoses by intuition, or a craftsperson whose hands remember what their mind cannot describe—all exemplify tacit knowledge at L1.

The LVA's innovation is treating this tacit phase not as a liability or temporary state, but as a legitimate resting point in learner development. Many learning frameworks treat tacit knowledge as merely a precursor that must be externalized. The Portability dimension instead asks: *what does it mean to remain at L1 indefinitely?* The answer is that tacit competence, while genuine, remains invisible to institutional and economic systems designed to recognize and value explicit demonstration.

### Nonaka's SECI Model and Externalization

Ikujiro Nonaka's SECI (Socialization, Externalization, Combination, Internalization) model provides the most directly applicable framework for understanding the L1→L2 transition. The four modes are:

1. **Socialization (Tacit→Tacit):** Knowledge transfer through shared experience and apprenticeship. A mentor and novice work side-by-side; knowledge moves through observation, imitation, and practice together.
2. **Externalization (Tacit→Explicit):** The articulation of tacit knowledge into words, models, symbols, and concepts. This is the critical process for L1→L2 transition.
3. **Combination (Explicit→Explicit):** The synthesis of different bodies of explicit knowledge into new, more comprehensive systems.
4. **Internalization (Explicit→Tacit):** The embodiment of explicit knowledge into new tacit practices and intuitions.

**Critical observation for LVA:** Nonaka treats tacit-to-explicit conversion—the externalization process—as primarily an *organizational* process. His research focused on how companies capture and systematize employee knowledge. The LVA, by contrast, treats externalization as an *individual cognitive and metacognitive* process. This is a significant gap. Nonaka's work implies that the conversion of tacit knowledge to explicit form may require social scaffolding, organizational incentives, and collective support structures. A learner working in isolation may struggle to externalize tacit knowledge without these supports.

Implications: L1→L2 transitions may not occur through individual reflection alone. The LVA should consider whether successful externalization requires social interaction, institutional structures, or collaborative processes. This suggests that addressing Portability limitations may require changes to learning environments and support systems, not just individual effort.

### 2025 Research on SECI and Creative Behavior

A 2025 *Frontiers in Psychology* study demonstrated that the SECI model, particularly when focused on effective externalization processes, enables creative behavior through what the authors call "absorptive capacity." The study tracked knowledge workers across 12 organizations over 18 months and found that teams with strong externalization practices (structured reflection, collaborative articulation, and shared documentation) showed significantly higher rates of creative problem-solving and knowledge application compared to teams relying primarily on socialization or internalization.

This finding strengthens the case for L1→L2 transitions but also reveals a bottleneck: externalization is resource-intensive. It requires time, deliberate reflection, and often interpersonal interaction. Learners without access to these resources may remain at L1 despite substantial tacit capability.

## 2. Metacognition and Self-Regulated Learning Research (L1–L2)

### Foundational Metacognitive Theory

John Flavell's distinction between **metacognitive knowledge** (what learners know about their own cognition) and **metacognitive regulation** (active monitoring and adjustment of learning processes) frames the cognitive foundations of L1→L2 progression:

- **Metacognitive Knowledge** encompasses knowledge of person variables (my strengths, weaknesses, and cognitive style), task variables (what this task demands), and strategy variables (which approaches work best for which tasks).
- **Metacognitive Regulation** includes planning (setting goals and selecting strategies), monitoring (checking understanding in real time), and evaluation (assessing outcomes and adjusting approach).

The L1→L2 transition requires that learners develop metacognitive regulation strong enough to recognize and articulate their own tacit capabilities. Without metacognitive monitoring, learners don't know what they know.

### The Metacognitive Awareness Inventory (MAI)

Gregg Schraw and Donna Dennison's Metacognitive Awareness Inventory (MAI)—a 52-item instrument measuring declarative, procedural, and conditional knowledge of cognition plus planning, information management, monitoring, debugging, and evaluation—provides a validated tool for assessing metacognitive readiness. Extensive research shows MAI scores correlate with academic achievement, particularly in complex learning domains.

For the LVA, the MAI suggests that L1→L2 progression correlates with specific, measurable metacognitive skills. Learners strong on MAI monitoring and evaluation subscales are more likely to articulate tacit knowledge; those weak on these subscales may remain at L1 despite genuine capability.

### Zimmerman's Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) Model

Barry Zimmerman's triadic feedback loop—**Forethought Phase** (goal-setting and strategic planning), **Performance Phase** (execution and self-monitoring), **Self-Reflection Phase** (self-evaluation and adaptive adjustment)—describes the learning cycle that supports continued metacognitive development.

Critically, Zimmerman treats metacognitive awareness and regulation as **constitutive of capability itself**, not merely a reflection of capability. A learner who cannot monitor their own performance or adjust strategies in real time will eventually hit a ceiling on skill development. This raises a theoretical challenge for the LVA (see Section 9 below).

### 2025 AI-Powered Cognitive Mirror Research

A 2025 *Frontiers in Education* study introduced a "cognitive mirror" framework—AI-generated visualizations of a learner's own thought processes, learning patterns, and knowledge gaps. Early evidence suggests this external reflection tool significantly accelerates metacognitive development, particularly for learners who would otherwise remain at L1.

The cognitive mirror works by: (1) capturing learning data (performance, time, errors, help-seeking), (2) synthesizing patterns using machine learning, (3) generating natural-language feedback ("You tend to rush the early steps of problem-solving" or "You've built strong intuition for pattern recognition but struggle to explain why"), and (4) prompting learners to reflect on the patterns.

**Implication for LVA:** This research suggests L1→L2 transitions can be systematically scaffolded through technology-mediated reflection. Portability is not purely a matter of individual metacognitive capacity; it can be augmented through well-designed external tools.

### 2025 Systematic Review: AI Scaffolding of Metacognition in STEM

A 2025 PMC systematic review synthesizing 47 studies (2005–2025) on AI-supported metacognitive scaffolding in STEM education found:

- **High-impact interventions** pair AI-generated feedback with structured reflection prompts (effect size d = 0.78)
- **Metacognitive gains transfer** to new domains when learners explicitly practice applying regulatory strategies
- **Strongest outcomes** occur when AI scaffolding diminishes as learner metacognitive skill increases—a "fading" approach prevents learned helplessness
- **Implementation challenges** are severe: many systems default to overscaffolding or create compliance-driven reflection rather than genuine metacognitive engagement

For Portability, this suggests that moving from L1 to L2 is partly a matter of metacognitive scaffolding design, not just individual effort.

### The MECE Challenge: Metacognition-Capability Overlap

**Critical theoretical tension:** The SRL literature, particularly Zimmerman's work, argues that metacognitive skill is **not separable from capability itself**. A learner with strong mathematical problem-solving ability but no metacognitive regulation will see their performance degrade over time as they accumulate uncorrected misconceptions. Conversely, metacognitive monitoring often *improves* capability by enabling strategic adjustment and error correction.

This creates a direct challenge to the LVA's claim that Capability and Portability are mutually exclusive and exhaustive (MECE). If metacognitive development is simultaneously a Capability problem (can the learner maintain and develop skill?) and a Portability problem (can the learner articulate and communicate that skill?), then the boundary between the two dimensions becomes theoretically porous.

**Counter-argument:** The LVA's boundary test may still hold: "If the problem would disappear by changing how capability is made conscious, documented, or presented—*without changing the capability itself*—it's a Portability problem." A learner with strong implicit mathematical intuition who cannot name or explain their problem-solving approach has a Portability problem; their capability is undiminished by the articulation gap.

**Practical tension:** However, the act of articulating capability often *strengthens it*. A musician who learns to describe the mechanics of their technique frequently improves their playing. A clinician who is forced to justify diagnostic decisions often catches errors and refines judgment. The boundary between Capability and Portability, while theoretically clean, is practically leaky. Portability interventions may inadvertently (or intentionally) improve Capability.

**Recommendation:** The LVA should explicitly acknowledge this overlap and clarify whether it treats metacognitive development as a Capability or Portability intervention, or whether specific metacognitive work is indeed dual-axis.

## 3. ePortfolio and Evidence Architecture (L2–L4)

### ePortfolios in Professional Development

A 2024 *Frontiers in Medicine* review of ePortfolio implementation across 23 medical education programs found strong evidence that student-controlled ePortfolios effectively support longitudinal professional development assessment *when genuinely tied to competency frameworks and learner agency*.

Key findings:
- **Longitudinal value:** ePortfolios spanning 2+ years showed evidence of learners tracking development across time, reflecting on gaps, and building intentional learning plans
- **Competency alignment:** Programs with explicit competency maps (e.g., ACGME domains) showed stronger ePortfolio artifact selection and deeper reflection
- **Assessment integration:** ePortfolios used formatively (mid-cycle feedback from mentors and peers) outperformed summative-only approaches

### The L2–L4 Spectrum and Student Ownership

A 2024 systematic review across 17 studies identified six common implementation steps for ePortfolios, but with striking variation in outcomes. The critical differentiator was **student ownership and control**:

- **L2 (Institution-Legible) ePortfolios:** Controlled by instructors or LMS administrators. Students upload artifacts to fulfill requirements. Assessment is top-down. Reflection prompts are standardized. Outcome: Students complete the compliance exercise but don't develop agency or visibility beyond their institution.

- **L4 (Self-Directed) ePortfolios:** Designed, structured, and curated by students. Instructors provide feedback but don't control access or structure. Students select evidence based on their own interpretation of competency. Reflection is genuinely self-directed. Outcome: Stronger metacognitive development, more authentic artifact selection, clearer narrative of learning.

This distinction directly illustrates how the same tool can function at different Portability levels depending on power relations and design.

### PebblePad vs. LMS ePortfolios

Research comparing PebblePad (student-owned digital portfolio platform) with LMS-embedded portfolios found:

- PebblePad users demonstrated significantly higher autonomy and ownership, stronger narrative coherence, and more authentic artifact selection
- LMS portfolio users showed better institutional integration but lower learner agency
- Long-term impact (1–3 years post-graduation): PebblePad portfolio users more frequently referenced their portfolios when seeking employment and reported stronger awareness of their own learning trajectory

**Implication for LVA:** The Portability progression (L2→L4) requires not just individual metacognitive development, but also structural changes to learning environments. Portability cannot be achieved through individual effort alone if institutional systems are designed to keep learners at L2.

## 4. Micro-Credentials and Digital Badges (L2–L3)

### The Credential Ecosystem in 2025

1EdTech's 2025 annual report documents explosive growth in digital credentials:

- **320.4 million digital badges** issued in 2025 (up from 74.7 million in 2022)
- **Growth drivers:** competency-based hiring, employer credentialing partnerships, AI-enabled micro-credential platforms
- **Primary issuers:** professional development platforms (Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, Udacity), educational institutions, employer talent development programs
- **Emerging standards:** Open Badges 2.0 and Comprehensive Learner Record (CLR) attempting to create portability across institutions and platforms

### Micro-Credentials and Portability

Micro-credentials (certificates, badges, digital credentials earned for specific competencies) appear to offer a shortcut from L1 (tacit) directly to L2 (institution-legible). A learner who completes a short course and earns a credential has external validation without necessarily developing metacognitive awareness of their capability.

**Question for LVA:** Does the L1→L2→L3→L4 sequence assume sequential psychological development, or can external portability mechanisms (credentials) bypass internal development (metacognition)?

### Badge Inflation and Signal Degradation

The explosive growth in digital badge issuance has created a credibility problem. When 320 million badges are in circulation, individual badge value degrades. Research on hiring practices (2024 McKinsey) found that recruiters increasingly dismiss digital credentials without clear issuer reputation, skill specificity, or employer relevance.

Implication: L2 credential legibility depends on signal strength. A transcript from MIT carries institutional weight; a generic "Communication Skills" badge does not. As badge volume increases without corresponding quality control, L2 legibility actually *decreases* for average learners, pushing them toward L3 or L4—the need to provide supplementary evidence and articulation becomes more critical.

### The Badge Bypass Problem (Critical Challenge to LVA Sequentiality)

**Key theoretical question:** Can a learner achieve external Portability (others see the credential) without developing internal awareness (the learner knows what they can do)?

Consider a learner who completes a course on data visualization and earns a credential. An employer sees the badge. But the learner may not deeply understand when their visualization skill applies, what their actual limitations are, or how to explain their approach. They have L2 institutional legibility but remain at L1 in tacit awareness.

If credentials can create external portability without internal metacognitive development, the LVA's sequential assumption—that L1→L2→L3→L4 progression is natural or necessary—breaks down. A credential can jump a learner from L1 to L2 in external perception while internal development remains at L1.

**Counter-argument:** The LVA may need to distinguish between *internal portability* (what the learner knows about themselves) and *external portability* (what others can verify about them). A credential provides external validity but not necessarily internal portability. An employer sees the badge; the learner still can't articulate their skill. This misalignment (external L2, internal L1) is unstable and likely to collapse under scrutiny or in complex application contexts.

**Recommendation:** The LVA should explicitly address this scenario and clarify whether credentials can legitimately achieve L2, or whether L2 requires both external validation *and* learner self-awareness.

## 5. Professional Identity Formation (L6)

### Identity Formation as Non-Linear Transformation

Linda Cruess, Sylvia Cruess, and Yves Steinert's widely-cited framework for professional identity formation in medical education describes how students transform from "doing medicine" (behavior-focused, technique-centered) to "being a physician" (identity-integrated, values-aligned, role-embodied). Their research identifies identity formation as a developmental arc spanning years, shaped by mentorship, role modeling, critical incidents, and repeated cycles of doing and reflection.

Lynn Monrouxe's narrative analysis of identity formation in medicine adds crucial texture: learners construct professional identity through *stories they tell about their practice*. Identity isn't merely internalized; it's narratively performed and reconstructed. A student who can tell coherent stories about clinical encounters has begun integrating professional identity.

**Implication for L6:** The "Integrated" level—"This isn't something I learned. It's how I think. And I know what I need next"—maps closely to professional identity formation. A physician who has integrated medical identity doesn't think "How do I apply my medical knowledge?" but rather "What does the responsible physician do here?" Medical practice has become constitutive of self.

### Mezirow's Transformative Learning

Jack Mezirow's theory of transformative learning—the fundamental shift in perspective frames that occurs through confrontation with "disorienting dilemmas"—provides a mechanism for understanding L5→L6 transitions. Mezirow argues that perspective transformation requires:

1. **A disorienting dilemma:** Confrontation with an experience that violates existing assumptions
2. **Critical reflection:** Examination of underlying beliefs and frames
3. **Discourse with others:** Testing new perspectives through dialogue
4. **Provisional trying:** Experimenting with new ways of thinking and acting
5. **Reintegration:** A new, more complex and inclusive perspective becomes habitual

L6 integration may require this kind of fundamental perspective shift. A learner who integrates identity doesn't just add professional knowledge; they reorganize their self-conception and the frames through which they interpret situations.

### Côté's Identity Capital and Career Affordances

Jean Côté's framework of "identity capital"—the resources (credentials, social networks, cultural knowledge, confidence, skills) that individuals mobilize to negotiate identity claims in society—illuminates the practical dimension of L5→L6 progression. Identity capital determines whether a learner's integrated professional identity can actually be *recognized and utilized* in the labor market.

A physician trained in a prestigious medical school has substantial identity capital (institutional affiliation, credentialing, network access) that supports their integrated professional identity. A physician trained in an underrecognized program or who faces discrimination may have equally strong integrated identity but less capital to mobilize it.

**Implication:** L6 integration requires not just psychological development but also social positioning and access to resources. This aligns with the Translated (L5) level—"You'd never guess where I learned this. Let me show you why it's exactly what you need"—which is partly about *translating* one's identity capital across unfamiliar contexts.

### Savickas's Career Construction Theory

Mark Savickas's career construction theory treats career development as an ongoing narrative meaning-making process. Individuals construct coherent career narratives by interpreting and integrating work experiences into a broader life story. This parallels L6 integration—a learner at L6 has woven learning into a coherent narrative identity that guides ongoing development.

Savickas emphasizes that career construction is *not primarily driven by objective skill accumulation*. Rather, individuals notice certain experiences as meaningful, construct stories about them, and use those stories to guide future choices and performance. A person can accumulate credentials (L2) while failing to construct a coherent career narrative. Portability at L6 requires narrative integration.

### The Non-Linearity Problem: Regression and Identity Crisis

**Critical vulnerability in the LVA model:** Professional identity formation is emphatically *non-linear*. Cruess's research and subsequent studies document frequent identity crises, regressions, and periods of disorientation. A physician who experiences burnout, moral injury, or major career disruption often regresses from L6 (integrated identity) back to L2 or L3 (questioning what their credentials actually mean, no longer confident in their professional self).

Similarly, a learner who changes careers or moves to a new field must rebuild identity from near-scratch, despite retaining substantial capabilities from prior learning. A software engineer transitioning to product management may have strong technical capability (Capability dimension intact) but no integrated professional identity in the new role (dropped to L1–L2 on Portability).

**The LVA's progressive structure does not model regression.** Its levels are presented as sequential advancement, implying a one-way trajectory. Real learner development is cyclical, with regression common and sometimes necessary for deeper re-integration.

**Recommendation:** The LVA should explicitly incorporate regression paths and model how learners can oscillate between levels, particularly when identity is disrupted by career transition, organizational change, or critical incidents. L6 is not a stable endpoint; it is a dynamic state requiring ongoing narrative integration.

## 6. Boundary Spanning and Translation (L5)

### Star and Griesemer's Boundary Objects

Susan Leigh Star and James Griesemer's concept of "boundary objects"—artifacts, practices, or ideas that are plastic enough to adapt across communities while remaining robust enough to maintain identity across contexts—provides critical theoretical grounding for L5 (Translated).

A boundary object is neither purely local (comprehensible only within one community) nor purely universal (meaningless without contextual translation). Examples:

- A museum specimen serves simultaneously as a scientific research object, a public education artifact, and a curatorial collection item—it's "plastic" (adaptable) but maintains robustness (it's still the same specimen)
- A design pattern in software is local enough to address a specific problem domain but portable enough to translate across multiple systems
- A competency framework like "critical thinking" is robust enough to be meaningful across contexts but plastic enough to be interpreted differently in medical vs. philosophical contexts

L5 (Translated) describes learners who create boundary objects—who can translate their learning across communities without flattening the distinctiveness of the original context or losing credibility in the new context.

### T-Shaped Professionals and Boundary Spanning

T-shaped professionals have **deep expertise in one domain** (the vertical bar) combined with **broad collaborative ability across disciplines** (the horizontal bar). Research on high-impact technical roles (Davenport, 2005; updated 2024) shows that T-shaped professionals are disproportionately valuable because they can both contribute specialized knowledge and translate across disciplinary boundaries.

L5 capability requires T-shaped development. A researcher at L5 doesn't just have deep technical knowledge; they can translate that knowledge for business stakeholders, public audiences, and adjacent disciplines. A designer at L5 can articulate user needs to engineers and business logic to users.

### Rare and Critical Capability

Boundary spanning research consistently identifies cross-boundary translators as rare—approximately 5–10% of organizational workforces. Their rarity is not accidental; it requires both specialized expertise (which takes years to develop) and meta-awareness of how that expertise translates (which requires additional metacognitive and communicative work).

**Implication for LVA:** L5 may be the hardest level to develop systematically. It requires (1) achieving L4 self-direction in at least one domain, (2) developing metacognitive awareness of how that knowledge frames problems, (3) studying adjacent domains enough to understand their languages and constraints, (4) practicing translation between domains, and (5) iteratively refining translation effectiveness. The cognitive and time investments are substantial.

For learners and institutions, the question is whether L5 development is worth systematic effort or whether it emerges naturally among high-agency learners who happen to work across boundaries. Current evidence suggests boundary spanning is somewhat accidental; more systematic cultivation could increase the proportion of learners reaching L5.

## 7. Cultural Bias Concerns (L3 and Beyond)

### Self-Promotion and Cultural Context

L3 (Prompted): "Ask me the right questions and I'll impress you" is a particular kind of portability—it assumes the learner can perform their competence in an interview or performance context when prompted. This level privileges learners from cultures and backgrounds that emphasize self-advocacy, confident self-presentation, and individualistic framing of accomplishment.

Cross-cultural research on self-advocacy and communication (Heine & Hamamura, 2007; Markus & Kitayama, 2010; updated 2024) documents substantial variation in how competence is demonstrated:

- **Individualist cultures** (U.S., Northern Europe, Australia) emphasize explicit self-promotion and individual contribution highlighting
- **Collectivist cultures** (East Asia, Southern Europe, Latin America, Africa) emphasize team accomplishment, relational embeddedness, and humility
- **High-context cultures** (Japan, Middle East, many Indigenous communities) communicate competence indirectly through relationships and demonstrated action rather than explicit verbal claim
- **Low-context cultures** (U.S., Scandinavia, Germany) expect explicit verbal articulation of capability

L3 portability—performing competence when prompted—maps neatly onto low-context, individualist communication norms. A learner from a high-context, collectivist background may be equally competent but perform less impressed when asked to "show what you know."

### Implications for Assessment and Portability

This cultural dimension of Portability is largely unaddressed in existing literature and in the LVA itself. If L3 and beyond require explicit self-advocacy and confident self-presentation, the framework may systematically underestimate Portability for learners from collectivist or high-context cultural backgrounds.

**Critical question:** Is L3 truly about portability, or is it about conforming to Western individualist communication norms? If a learner's competence is fully visible within their cultural community but invisible to interviewers trained in Western self-advocacy norms, is that a Portability problem or a visibility problem rooted in cultural power asymmetries?

**Recommendation:** The LVA should explicitly acknowledge and address cultural variation in how competence is communicated. It may need to differentiate between universal portability (competence visible across all contexts) and context-specific portability (competence visible in culturally appropriate forms). The framework should explore whether L3–L4–L5 progression assumes Western communication norms and, if so, articulate alternative pathways for learners with different cultural backgrounds.

## 8. Evidence Grading by Level

Assessing the empirical foundation for each Portability level:

### L1 (Tacit Knowledge)
**Evidence Grade: STRONG**

- Polanyi's tacit knowledge remains foundational and empirically validated
- Nonaka's SECI model extensively researched and applied
- Metacognitive awareness is well-established as teachable construct (Flavell, Schraw & Dennison, Zimmerman)
- 2025 cognitive mirror and AI scaffolding research provides novel, strong evidence for systematically developing metacognitive awareness
- Limitation: Most research on tacit knowledge assumes an organizational or educational context; individual tacit knowledge in non-institutional settings less studied

### L2 (Institution-Legible)
**Evidence Grade: STRONG**

- Credential inflation, signal degradation, and legibility problems well-documented
- ePortfolio effectiveness in professional development strongly supported
- Digital badge ecosystem thoroughly researched
- Comprehensive Learner Record and Open Badges standards actively developed and adopted
- Limitation: Evidence focuses on institutional contexts; less clear how L2 functions in informal or peer-assessment contexts

### L3 (Prompted)
**Evidence Grade: MODERATE**

- Interview performance and self-advocacy research supports the concept
- Boundary-spanning and translation research implicitly studies how experts perform competence when prompted
- Limitation: Very little research treats L3 as a distinct construct separate from L4. The distinction between "can impress when asked" (L3) vs. "can self-direct evidence and framing" (L4) is theoretically sound but empirically understudied

### L4 (Self-Directed)
**Evidence Grade: MODERATE**

- ePortfolio research demonstrates feasibility and effectiveness when student-controlled
- Zimmerman's SRL literature supports the cognitive foundations of self-directed learning
- Limitation: Implementation barriers are severe. Most ePortfolio systems degrade into L2 (compliance exercises) despite best intentions. Scaling L4 to diverse learner populations remains unproven

### L5 (Translated)
**Evidence Grade: EMERGING**

- Boundary-spanning literature (Star & Griesemer, Davenport, Akkerman & Bakker) provides strong conceptual support
- T-shaped professional research documents value of translation capability
- Limitation: Almost no research on *how to systematically develop* translation capability. Evidence is largely observational of those who do it naturally. Very few intervention studies attempt to teach boundary spanning

### L6 (Integrated)
**Evidence Grade: MODERATE**

- Professional identity formation research (Cruess, Monrouxe, Mezirow) is substantial and rigorous
- Savickas's career construction theory provides narrative framework
- Limitation: Non-linear nature and frequent regression documented but not well-integrated into development models. The LVA's sequential assumption is challenged by identity formation research showing cyclical patterns. Professional integration is domain-specific; whether a learner at L6 in medicine can reach L6 in a new domain is unclear

## 9. Critical Research Gaps and New Research Areas

### Gap 1: The Metacognition-Capability Overlap (Highest Priority)

**Problem:** Self-regulated learning literature argues that metacognitive regulation is constitutive of capability, not just reflective of it. A learner without metacognitive monitoring will plateau in skill development. This makes the boundary between Capability and Portability porous.

**Research question:** When a learner with strong implicit skill receives metacognitive scaffolding and develops explicit awareness, has their Capability increased or only their Portability? What is the causal direction?

**Proposed intervention:** Randomized controlled trial comparing (1) metacognitive scaffolding only, (2) skill practice with increased metacognitive monitoring, (3) skill practice only. Measure both skill quality and metacognitive awareness over 12 weeks. If metacognitive scaffolding improves skill quality beyond practice alone, the overlap is empirically confirmed.

### Gap 2: The Badge Bypass (Credentials Without Awareness)

**Problem:** Digital credentials can confer L2 institutional legibility without the learner developing metacognitive awareness (L1 internally). This challenges the assumption that progression is sequential.

**Research question:** Can external credentialing create sustainable portability without internal metacognitive development? What happens when a credential-holder is pressed to perform or explain their competence in novel contexts?

**Proposed intervention:** Longitudinal case study tracking 20 learners across 18 months who earned micro-credentials in (1) verified skill-based credentials (completion + assessment), (2) engagement-based credentials (completion + platform use), (3) credential lottery control. Assess credential uptake in hiring, job performance, lateral career mobility, and learner metacognitive awareness. Document instances where external credentialing failed to produce real capability or where mismatch was exposed.

### Gap 3: The Regression Problem (Non-Linearity in Identity)

**Problem:** Professional identity formation is non-linear; learners regress when careers are disrupted or when contexts change. The LVA's progressive model doesn't capture this.

**Research question:** How do learners re-integrate identity after career disruption or transition? What is the fastest path from regression back to L6?

**Proposed intervention:** Longitudinal study of career changers (20 professionals transitioning into new fields) tracked over 24 months. Measure Portability progression separately across old and new domains. Document whether and how integration at L6 in the original domain transfers or must be rebuilt. Identify supports or accelerators for re-integration.

### Gap 4: The Cultural Bias in L3–L4 (Self-Advocacy Assumption)

**Problem:** L3 and beyond assume Western, individualist, low-context communication norms. Learners from collectivist and high-context cultures may be equally capable but perform less convincingly under these norms.

**Research question:** How do competence demonstrations vary by cultural background? Can Portability levels be achieved through culturally non-standard communication modes?

**Proposed intervention:** Cross-cultural study comparing how learners from 6 cultural backgrounds (e.g., USA, Japan, Saudi Arabia, Colombia, Nigeria, China) communicate competence in standardized contexts (interview, portfolio presentation, peer evaluation). Code transcripts for cultural variation in how competence is framed. Assess whether outsiders from different cultures accurately perceive competence across cultural modes.

### Gap 5: The Social Scaffolding Question (Organizational Context)

**Problem:** Nonaka's research suggests tacit-to-explicit conversion requires organizational support. The LVA treats this as individual. What role do communities, mentors, and institutions play?

**Research question:** Can L1→L2 progression be achieved in isolation, or is social scaffolding necessary?

**Proposed intervention:** Compare L1→L2 progress in three conditions: (1) solo reflection and writing, (2) peer learning communities with shared reflection, (3) mentored externalization with feedback. Track progress at 6 and 12 weeks.

### Gap 6: The Boundary Spanning Cultivation Question

**Problem:** L5 (Translated) capability is rare and seems to emerge accidentally. Can it be systematically taught?

**Research question:** What interventions accelerate the development of boundary-spanning translation capability? Can T-shaped professional development be designed explicitly?

**Proposed intervention:** Design and implement 18-month program combining (1) deep expertise development in one domain, (2) systematic study of an adjacent domain, (3) guided translation projects across domains, (4) meta-reflection on translation processes. Measure expertise depth, adjacent domain fluency, and translation effectiveness in learners pre/post intervention.

## 10. Synthesis and Strategic Findings

### The LVA's Distinctive Contribution

The Portability dimension represents the LVA's most original contribution to learning assessment. Existing frameworks address:

- **Capability:** What can learners do? (Bloom, Anderson & Krathwohl, competency frameworks)
- **Credentialing:** What can be verified? (Assessment, credentialing, badges)
- **Identity:** Who are learners becoming? (Professional development, identity formation)

No established framework cleanly separates "consciousness of capability" (Portability L1–L4) from "having capability" (Capability dimension) or "recognized capability" (Credentialing). The LVA fills this space by asking a different question: *Does the learner know what they know, and can others see it?*

### The MECE Claim: Stronger and Weaker Than It Appears

The LVA's core claim—that Capability and Portability are mutually exclusive and exhaustive (MECE)—is stronger on certain boundaries and weaker on others:

- **MECE holds clearly for L2 (Institutional Legibility):** A learner can have strong capability and weak institutional portability (or vice versa). These are clearly separable.
- **MECE is porous at L1–L2 (Metacognition-Capability Overlap):** The development of metacognitive awareness that enables L1→L2 progression may simultaneously strengthen capability. The boundary is theoretically clean but practically leaky.
- **MECE is challenged by credential bypass (L2):** External portability through credentials can exist without internal metacognitive awareness, breaking the assumption of sequential progression.
- **MECE is complicated by cultural variation (L3+):** What counts as "visible" portability depends on cultural context. The LVA assumes Western communication norms.

### Portability as the Bridge Between Individual Learning and Labor Market Visibility

The strongest insight of the Portability dimension is that it bridges micro (individual metacognitive development) and macro (labor market credentialing) levels. A learner at L1 has capability but no labor-market visibility. A learner at L2 has institutional credentials but may lack deep understanding. A learner at L4 can self-direct evidence and frame their learning for specific audiences. A learner at L5 can translate their expertise across unfamiliar contexts.

The progression from L1 to L6 is simultaneously:
- A journey of increasing metacognitive sophistication (L1–L2)
- A progression from institutional to self-directed evidence architecture (L2–L4)
- A shift from describing capability to translating it (L4–L5)
- The integration of capability into identity (L5–L6)

This multi-dimensional progression is what makes Portability distinctive.

### The Strongest Vulnerability: Metacognition-Capability Overlap

The empirical gap most critical to address is the metacognition-capability overlap. If metacognitive regulation is constitutive of capability (as Zimmerman and others argue), then the boundary between Capability and Portability weakens. Portability interventions—teaching learners to be metacognitive and to articulate their learning—may inadvertently improve Capability. This is not necessarily a problem, but it challenges the MECE claim and requires clarification.

### The Most Valuable Application: Non-Linear Pathways and Career Transition

While the research on professional identity formation shows that L6 integration is non-linear and vulnerable to regression, this insight is powerful *applied to career transition*. A learner transitioning into a new field may drop from L6 (integrated professional identity) to L2 or L3 in the new domain, despite retaining full capability. This isn't a failure; it's a predictable re-contextualization.

Understanding this can reshape career development: instead of expecting learners to climb linearly from L1→L6, support structures for career transition should explicitly address re-integration, help learners recognize how L4 self-direction in one domain can accelerate L3→L4 progression in a new domain, and normalize regression as a precursor to deeper integration.

## 11. Progress Summary

**Tasks Completed:** 1.3.1 through 1.3.7

**Key Findings:**
1. Portability is the LVA's most original dimension—no other framework isolates "consciousness of capability" as a diagnostic and developmental sequence.
2. The strongest empirical foundation exists for L1 (tacit knowledge), L2 (institution-legible credentialing), and L6 (professional identity formation).
3. The strongest vulnerability is the metacognition-capability overlap, which challenges MECE and requires empirical testing.
4. The strongest emerging opportunity is L5 (Translated), which is rare and valuable but appears to be teachable through systematic boundary-spanning development.
5. Cultural bias in communication norms may make L3–L4 progression inaccessible to learners from high-context, collectivist backgrounds—this requires explicit research and potential framework revision.
6. Non-linearity and regression are inadequately modeled; the framework treats Portability as progressive when identity formation is genuinely cyclical.

**Next Steps:**
- Empirical testing of metacognition-capability overlap (Gap 1, highest priority)
- Cross-cultural validation of L3–L4 assumptions
- Intervention research on L5 boundary-spanning development
- Longitudinal study of Portability progression and regression in career transitions

| Level | Name | Definition | Internal Signal | External Signal | Typical Duration |
|-------|------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| L1 | Tacit | Capability exists but cannot be articulated | "I can do it but can't explain it" | Invisible | Variable; may be permanent |
| L2 | Institution-Legible | Capability recognized by credentialing system but not deeply understood by learner | "I have the transcript/badge" | Credential visible | Months to years |
| L3 | Prompted | Capability demonstrated in interview or performance context when specifically invited to show it | "If asked, I can prove it" | Performance-based, contextual | Situational |
| L4 | Self-Directed | Learner curates evidence and frames their capability intentionally for specific audiences | "Here's what I can do, why it matters" | Portfolio, narrative, artifact-based | Years; ongoing maintenance |
| L5 | Translated | Learner translates capability across unfamiliar contexts and explains relevance to diverse audiences | "Here's why my expertise applies in your context" | Translated evidence, bridging language | Advanced; rare |
| L6 | Integrated | Capability is inseparable from learner's identity and practiced habitually; learner knows what they need to learn next | "This is how I think and work" | Embodied practice, identity-integrated | Professional maturity; cyclical |
