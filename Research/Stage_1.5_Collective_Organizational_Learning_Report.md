# Stage 1.5: Collective Organizational Learning Report
## Extending the Learning Value Architecture from Individual to Collective Levels

**Research Date:** March 13, 2026
**Framework:** Learning Value Architecture (LVA)
**Focus:** Multi-level learning diagnosis and organizational learning theory integration

---

## Executive Summary

The Learning Value Architecture currently diagnoses individual learner states across three dimensions: **Engagement** (motivation and alignment), **Capability** (competence development), and **Portability** (ability to articulate and transfer learning). This report examines whether and how the LVA should extend to team, organizational, and societal levels.

**Key Finding:** The LVA's individual-learner focus is both its greatest strength (diagnostic precision for targeted intervention) and its most significant limitation (inability to capture emergent collective phenomena). Across multiple research traditions—from organizational learning theory to collective intelligence research—evidence shows that teams and organizations exhibit learning properties that cannot be predicted from or reduced to individual member capabilities.

**Recommendation:** Implement a **minimum viable extension** immediately via an institutional readiness overlay, while pursuing a **research-grounded long-term target** of a multi-level diagnostic model (3×4 matrix: three LVA dimensions across four ontological levels—individual, team, organization, society).

## 1. Fisher's Collective Edge — Key Arguments

Colin M. Fisher (UCL School of Management), former jazz musician turned organizational researcher, argues in *The Collective Edge* (2025) that group capability is fundamentally different from aggregated individual capability:

### Core Claims

**Emergent Properties:** Groups have properties that cannot be reduced to member characteristics. Team performance is not the sum of individual performance. A group of individually motivated individuals can fail collectively if coordination fails; conversely, a group can exceed what its members could accomplish alone through synergy.

**The Myth of the Lone Genius:** Fisher directly challenges the innovation narrative. Almost no innovation of any measurable worth comes from a solitary individual. Even solo authors, artists, and scientists have collaborators and supporters. The genius narrative obscures the collective infrastructure that enabled the breakthrough.

**Competition as Motivation:** Competition can motivate without creating dysfunction, when properly channeled. Fisher distinguishes between competitive environments that drive effort (positive) and those that create destructive conflict (negative). The difference lies in the institutional conditions.

**Collective Failures, Not Individual Ones:** Common group pathologies—conformity, polarization, prejudice, open conflict, and mass stupidity—are *collective* failures. An individual with sound judgment can fail to voice dissent in a conforming group; an intelligent group can collectively make a stupid decision. The failure is not at the individual level.

**Leadership as Condition-Setting:** Good group leadership means putting research-backed *conditions* in place, not commanding outputs. Fisher argues the leader's role is to structure the environment—clarifying roles, managing information flow, creating psychological safety—so that the group's capability emerges.

### Implications for LVA Extension

1. **Diagnostic Gap:** If a team of individually-Fluent (Capability L6) members performs poorly due to coordination failures, poor information flow, or role ambiguity, the LVA has no diagnostic for this. The framework would rate members as "capable" while the team fails.

2. **Generativity is Social:** Fisher argues that Generativity (Capability L7—creating new forms, novel combinations) may be *inherently* social. Innovation almost always emerges from groups because new ideas require both creative conceptualization and critical feedback, collective sense-checking, and iterative refinement.

3. **Team-Level Engagement:** The LVA's Engagement dimension could extend to teams. Team-level engagement is distinct from individual motivation. It involves:
   - Collective commitment to a shared purpose
   - Group energy and psychological safety
   - Equitable voice and mutual respect
   - Shared accountability for outcomes

4. **Team Portability:** At the team level, Portability raises new questions: Can a team make its collective capability visible? Can it articulate what the group knows that no individual member knows? This is not aggregated individual knowledge—it is knowledge emergent from the group's interaction patterns.

## 2. Senge's Five Disciplines — Organizational Learning

Peter Senge's *The Fifth Discipline* (1990) identifies five disciplines that characterize learning organizations:

### 1. Systems Thinking
Seeing patterns and interdependencies. Understanding feedback loops, stock-and-flow dynamics, and unintended consequences. The LVA does not include systems thinking.

**LVA Gap:** An individual or institution stuck in linear cause-effect thinking will misdiagnose learning problems. Example: blaming learner motivation (low Engagement) when the problem is actually the system's incentive structure (a systems-level issue). Senge calls this "shifting the burden to the intervenor"—the system designed so that the symptom goes away only when the intervenor supplies remedy, strengthening dependence.

### 2. Personal Mastery
Continually clarifying and deepening personal vision. Connecting daily work to larger purpose. Making explicit the difference between current reality and desired reality (the "creative tension").

**LVA Mapping:** Partial mapping to LVA Engagement L6 (Aligned—purpose integrated with organizational direction) and Capability L6-L7 (Fluent/Generative). But Senge treats personal mastery as an ongoing *discipline*—a practice maintained over time—not a level to achieve and then abandon.

### 3. Mental Models
The deeply ingrained assumptions that constrain thinking. Senge argues these are often invisible until they collide with experience. Organizations can have collective mental models—shared but unexamined assumptions about what works.

**LVA Gap—Critical:** The LVA has no equivalent for mental models. An institution's mental models about learning (e.g., "lecture is the most efficient delivery mode," "grades accurately measure capability," "individual success is unaffected by group dynamics") can systematically prevent it from implementing LVA-recommended interventions, even after diagnosis.

### 4. Shared Vision
When individual purpose aligns with organizational direction. Senge distinguishes this from "imposed vision" (top-down mandate) or "fragmented vision" (multiple competing visions). Shared vision emerges when members genuinely commit to the organization's purpose.

**LVA Mapping:** This is organizational-level Engagement—similar to LVA Engagement L6 (Aligned) but at the collective level. A learning organization needs both individual alignment and collective alignment.

### 5. Team Learning
The discipline of dialogue and collective thinking. Senge emphasizes the difference between *discussion* (breaking ideas apart, advocating for position) and *dialogue* (thinking together, exploring shared meaning). Team learning is the capability to think and learn together, not just individually.

**LVA Gap—Critical:** Team learning describes organizational-level Capability—the group's ability to think, problem-solve, and learn together. This is distinct from individual capability. A team of capable individuals without team learning discipline will not perform as a learning system.

### Organizational Context Layer

Senge's framework makes clear that the LVA maps to only two of the five disciplines: Personal Mastery and partially Shared Vision. The other three—Systems Thinking, Mental Models, and Team Learning—describe the *institutional conditions* that enable or prevent learning. They are not about learning itself but about the environment in which learning occurs.

**Implication:** The LVA needs a companion layer that diagnoses organizational readiness and institutional conditions, not just learner state.

## 3. Argyris & Schön — Double-Loop Learning and Defensive Routines

Chris Argyris and Donald Schön's work on learning theories in action provides a model of *how* organizations learn and where they get stuck.

### Learning Loops

**Single-Loop Learning:** Detect error → adjust action within existing assumptions. Learn how to do things right.

**Mapping:** LVA Capability L3-L4 (Following Rules with accuracy; Adapting within familiar contexts). Single-loop learners improve execution within their current mental model.

**Double-Loop Learning:** Detect error → question and modify underlying assumptions → adjust action. Learn to do the right things—the goals themselves may be wrong.

**Mapping:** LVA Capability L5-L7 (Principled Understanding recognizing when rules don't apply; Adaptive Expertise flexibly applying knowledge; Generativity creating new approaches). Double-loop learners can fundamentally reconceptualize the problem.

### Model I (Defensive) vs. Model II (Productive) Behavior

**Model I Behavior:** Unilateral control (the actor defines the situation), suppress negative feelings, be rational (suppress emotion). Designed to avoid embarrassment or threat.

**Consequence:** Self-fulfilling prophecies, self-sealing processes, error escalation. The very act of suppressing dissent or alternative views makes the actor less likely to detect error.

**Model II Behavior:** Valid information from multiple sources, free and informed choice by affected parties, internal commitment to the choice (not compliance). Designed to enable learning even when it's uncomfortable.

**Consequence:** Enables double-loop learning because error signals aren't suppressed; alternatives are actively solicited; commitment is genuine.

### Defensive Routines

Argyris defines defensive routines as organizational behaviors designed to protect individuals or groups against embarrassment or threat. Examples:
- Undiscussables (topics everyone knows about but won't discuss)
- Circular logic that blocks inquiry
- Rules that protect people from feedback
- Norms against questioning authority

**Critical Gap for LVA:** An institution can diagnose correctly using the LVA (identify that members are at Engagement L2, Capability L3, Portability L1) but be *systematically unable to act* on the diagnosis because of defensive routines. The defensive routine insulates the institution from the discomfort of facing the gap.

**Example:** A university identifies that faculty are at low Capability L3 in active learning pedagogy (following scripted activities without understanding the principles). A defensive routine kicks in: "But our students don't have time for active learning—we have content to cover." This blocks implementation of the recommended intervention (principles-based practice at L5) because examining the underlying assumption (content coverage is more important than learning quality) triggers the threat that the institution's fundamental approach to education might be wrong.

**Implication:** The LVA implementation layer needs to address institutional defenses, not just learner diagnosis. Argyris would argue this is more important than the diagnosis itself.

## 4. Wenger's Communities of Practice

Etienne Wenger's Communities of Practice (CoP) framework (1998, building on Lave & Wenger 1991) argues that learning is fundamentally *situated* in social practice:

### Core Architecture

**Three constitutive elements of a CoP:**
1. **Mutual Engagement:** Members are bound together by doing things together and knowing each other's capabilities and limitations.
2. **Joint Enterprise:** The group is unified by a shared objective and the accountability that comes with it.
3. **Shared Repertoire:** The community accumulates routines, sensibilities, artifacts, vocabulary, and conventions that reflect the community's history and knowledge.

### Legitimate Peripheral Participation

Newcomers learn by gradually moving from peripheral to full participation. Learning is *not* individual-then-social (acquiring knowledge individually, then joining a community). Rather, learning happens *through* participation. The peripheral position is where learning accelerates because the newcomer is exposed to the full practice while having limited responsibility.

### Implications for LVA—Critical Reframing

1. **Unit of Analysis:** Wenger challenges the LVA's individual-learner unit of analysis. Learning is irreducibly social. Capability develops *through* community participation, not prior to it. An individual can have high theoretical knowledge but low community capability.

2. **Embedded Advantage:** A learner embedded in a strong CoP will advance faster through *all three* LVA dimensions:
   - **Engagement:** Participation in the community creates belonging and shared purpose
   - **Capability:** Access to the community's shared repertoire and to legitimate peripheral participation scaffolds skill development
   - **Portability:** The community's vocabulary and conventions become the learner's vocabulary

3. **Isolation Effect:** A learner isolated from community (e.g., self-taught, in a weak department, in a disconnected role) may stall regardless of intervention quality. They lack the mutual engagement and shared repertoire that accelerate learning.

4. **Shared Repertoire as Portability:** The "shared repertoire" is essentially collective Portability. It is knowledge that the community maintains and passes along. This is not individual articulation (LVA Portability) but *collective* externalization and maintenance of knowledge.

5. **Community as Intervention:** Wenger's model suggests that *building community* might be the highest-leverage intervention for LVA development. A strong CoP simultaneously addresses Engagement, Capability, and Portability. This challenges the MECE (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive) intervention structure if the framework is applied at the individual level only.

### Tension: Individual vs. Social Unit

The LVA assumes the individual learner is the unit of diagnosis and intervention. Wenger argues the community is the unit of learning. This is not a technical disagreement—it's a fundamental difference about where learning *lives*. The resolution is likely multi-level: individuals develop within communities, but communities also develop and have properties independent of their members.

## 5. Collective Intelligence Research

Recent empirical research on collective intelligence provides quantitative evidence for emergent group phenomena.

### Woolley et al. (2010)

**Finding:** Woolley, Chabris, Pentland, Hashmi, and Malone discovered a measurable collective intelligence factor ("c-factor")—a team's general ability to perform well across diverse cognitive tasks.

**Key Properties:**
- c-factor is analogous to individual IQ but measured at the team level
- c-factor was *not* predicted by average individual intelligence
- c-factor was *not* predicted by maximum individual intelligence
- c-factor *was* predicted by:
  - Average social perceptiveness (ability to read emotions, intentions)
  - Equality of conversational turn-taking (everyone participates roughly equally)
  - Proportion of women in the group (women on average score higher on social perceptiveness)

**Interpretation:** A team's cognitive capability is determined not by who is in the room but by how the members interact.

### 2024 Brookings Roundtable on Collective Intelligence

Collective intelligence is positioned as a mechanism for addressing complex societal challenges. The value of CI increases as challenges increase in complexity and scale. CI works by:
- Combining diverse perspectives at scale
- Enabling parallel problem-solving
- Creating feedback loops that correct individual errors
- Allowing emergence of novel solutions that no individual could generate

### 2024 PMC Study: AI-Enhanced Collective Intelligence

AI can augment collective intelligence by:
- Supporting group decision-making processes (surfacing relevant evidence)
- Reducing cognitive load (handling information synthesis)
- Improving information flow (making knowledge visible)
- Enhancing perspective diversity (surface relevant alternative framings)

### Shared Mental Models (SMMs)

A key mechanism for team capability is the development of shared mental models—mutually compatible expectations about how the domain works, who does what, and how to coordinate. SMMs enable a team to perform smoothly without constant explicit communication.

**Critical Property:** The team's shared mental model is a form of *collective* capability. The team "knows" things that no individual member knows. This knowledge emerges from the alignment of individual models.

### Implications for LVA

1. **Team Capability as Emergent Construct:** If c-factor is real and replicable, then "team capability" exists as a construct independent of aggregated individual capability. The LVA could develop a team-level Capability diagnostic.

2. **Team Capability Dimensions:** What would team-level Capability look like?
   - **Team L3:** Follows established protocols; communicates decisions
   - **Team L4:** Adapts established protocols to new contexts; coordinates flexibly
   - **Team L5:** Understands principles underlying coordination; diagnoses team dysfunction
   - **Team L6:** Coordinates fluidly across changing contexts; leverages diverse expertise
   - **Team L7:** Generates novel coordination approaches; improves the team's capability itself

3. **Team-Level Engagement:** The Woolley findings suggest team-level Engagement involves equitable participation and mutual perceptiveness, not just aggregated individual motivation. A team where one member dominates (even if all are motivated) will have lower c-factor.

4. **Team Portability:** Can the team articulate its collective capability? Can it make visible to outsiders (or to new members) what the group can do? This requires the team to externalize its shared mental models and shared repertoire.

5. **Network Effects:** Individual LVA trajectories may depend significantly on the network structure of the learning community. A strongly connected community enables faster learning; isolated individuals progress slowly even with good interventions.

## 6. Nonaka's Knowledge Creation Theory and Organizational Portability

Ikujiro Nonaka's SECI model (Socialization, Externalization, Combination, Internalization) provides a framework for understanding how organizations create and manage knowledge, particularly across the tacit-explicit boundary.

### SECI Model

**Socialization (Tacit → Tacit):** Tacit knowledge transferred through shared experience, observation, apprenticeship. The master and apprentice work together; knowledge moves through imitation and practice.

**Externalization (Tacit → Explicit):** Making tacit knowledge explicit through articulation, metaphor, analogy, hypothesis. The craftsperson explaining their technique; the expert writing documentation.

**Combination (Explicit → Explicit):** Integrating explicit knowledge across different formats and contexts. Merging insights from different documents; creating new explicit knowledge by recombining existing pieces.

**Internalization (Explicit → Tacit):** Absorbing explicit knowledge into personal tacit understanding through study and practice. The learner reading a manual and practicing until the knowledge becomes intuitive.

### Knowledge Spiral

The four modes form a spiral that amplifies knowledge across ontological levels: individual → group → organization → inter-organization. Each loop at one level creates potential for the next level.

### The Concept of "Ba"

Nonaka emphasizes that knowledge creation requires *ba*—a shared space (physical, virtual, mental) where people can interact and create meaning together. Ba enables the transitions between tacit and explicit knowledge. Without ba, knowledge creation stalls.

### 2025 Frontiers in Psychology: SECI and Creative Behavior

Recent work shows the SECI model enables creative behavior through absorptive capacity—the ability to recognize new information, assimilate it, and apply it. Organizations with high absorptive capacity are those that practice all four modes and maintain spaces for knowledge creation.

### Implications for LVA—Organizational Portability

1. **Portability as SECI:** LVA Portability at the individual level is essentially the Externalization mode—making one's tacit capability explicit. But Portability at the organizational level requires all four modes:
   - Socialization: Mentoring and communities of practice make capability visible through apprenticeship
   - Externalization: Documentation, articulation, codification make capability explicit
   - Combination: Integration of knowledge across institutional contexts; policies and training programs standardize approaches
   - Internalization: Learners absorb organizational knowledge into their practice

2. **Ba as Portability Infrastructure:** Creating spaces for knowledge creation—communities of practice, cross-functional teams, mentoring relationships, documentation systems—is essential for organizational Portability. Without ba, Portability L4 becomes impossible.

3. **Spiral Amplification:** The LVA individual diagnostic could be integrated into Nonaka's spiral. Individual Portability development feeds into group Portability (shared repertoire), which feeds into organizational Portability (codified knowledge), which enables inter-organizational learning.

4. **Measurement Challenge:** The SECI model is theoretically rich but difficult to measure operationally. Organizations struggle to track whether knowledge is actually moving through all four modes or whether it's stalling in one mode.

## 7. Kotter's 8-Step Change Management Model Applied to LVA Implementation

John Kotter's 8-step model for organizational change provides a roadmap for how institutions might implement LVA-based interventions. While Kotter's model was developed for business change, it has been extensively adapted for higher education.

### The Eight Steps

1. **Create Urgency:** Share LVA diagnostic data showing gaps between current state (individual Engagement L3, Capability L2) and desired state. Make the case that the status quo is unsustainable.

2. **Form Coalition:** Identify and mobilize champions across academic and training leadership. These are respected faculty, administrators, or trainers who can advocate for change across silos.

3. **Create Vision:** Articulate "What would our institution look like at E5-C6-P4?" for students/staff/faculty. Make the vision concrete and desirable.

4. **Communicate:** Make diagnostic findings accessible to stakeholders. Avoid jargon; connect findings to institutional values (e.g., "we want our graduates to be able to *transfer* their knowledge to new contexts—that's Portability L4").

5. **Empower Action:** Remove structural barriers. This might mean curriculum reform, assessment redesign, hiring practices that value mentoring, time allocation for community building.

6. **Quick Wins:** Start with the dimension where change is most visible. If Engagement is strongest, build from there. Create early successes to build momentum.

7. **Build on Change:** Scale small wins across programs/departments. Avoid declaring victory too early (Kotter says this is the most common failure mode).

8. **Anchor in Culture:** Embed LVA into institutional assessment cycles, hiring criteria, promotion processes, and reward systems. Make it "how we work here."

### Challenges in Higher Education

Research on change in higher education (Kezar, Eckel, others) shows Kotter's linear model needs significant adaptation:

- **Political Complexity:** Faculty governance, tenure systems, and disciplinary silos mean change is emergent and contested, not linear.
- **Distributed Authority:** No single person can mandate change; buy-in is required at multiple levels.
- **Resistance to Metrics:** Academics often resist measurement and standardized assessment; "you can't reduce learning to a number."
- **Accreditation Constraints:** Regional and specialized accreditors may not align with LVA categories or interventions.
- **Resource Limitations:** Most institutions lack resources to implement comprehensive change; prioritization is essential.

### Adapted Kotter for Higher Education

The most successful change initiatives in higher ed use a hybrid model:
- Start with early adopters (not institution-wide)
- Build evidence locally before scaling
- Frame change in disciplinary terms ("this is what historians do," "this is how engineers learn")
- Integrate change into existing assessment and accreditation cycles
- Celebrate small wins publicly to build social proof

## 8. Social Movements and Societal-Level Learning

If the LVA extends to organizational level, can it extend further to societal level? Social movements, public health campaigns, and policy diffusion provide models for how learning (or behavior change) spreads at scale.

### Threshold Models (Granovetter, 1978)

Collective behavior emerges when a sufficient number of individuals exceed their personal threshold for action. A society's "Engagement" with a learning challenge may follow threshold dynamics, not a smooth curve.

**Model:** Individual thresholds vary (some people will change with minimal evidence, others need extensive proof). As early adopters change, the social environment shifts for the remaining population, lowering their threshold. Once a critical mass is reached, the behavior cascades.

**Implication:** Societal-level LVA engagement might be characterized by threshold dynamics. Sudden shifts in how a society views learning (e.g., the rise of growth mindset, the recognition of emotional intelligence) don't follow a gradual curve—they follow a cascade.

### Contagion Models

Ideas, behaviors, and motivation spread through social networks. The structure of the network matters:

- **Dense networks** (tight clusters): Reinforce existing beliefs, slower adoption, strong commitment once adopted
- **Bridging ties** (connections across clusters): Accelerate adoption, enable novel combinations, may reduce depth

**Implication:** LVA Engagement at scale may depend on network structure. A society with strong bridging ties (cross-sector dialogue, interdisciplinary collaboration) will spread learning innovations faster than a siloed society.

### Social Movement Theory (Gamson)

For sustained social change, movements need:
1. **Organization:** Institutions and networks that maintain the movement
2. **Cultural framing:** A narrative that makes the movement's goals seem legitimate and achievable
3. **Political opportunity:** Windows where institutional change becomes possible

**Implication:** Societal-level Portability might mean making learning outcomes visible in public discourse and policy. This requires cultural framing (learning is valued) and political opportunity (education funding, policy support).

### Limitations of Societal-Level Extension

The analogy between individual/organizational learning and societal behavior change is suggestive but not conclusive. Key differences:

- **Agent:** Individuals and organizations have intentional goals; societies do not
- **Measurement:** Individual Engagement/Capability/Portability are measurable constructs; societal equivalents are more metaphorical
- **Causation:** At individual level, clear mechanisms (feedback loops, practice); at societal level, causation is diffuse

**Assessment:** Societal-level LVA extension should be viewed as exploratory, not empirically grounded. It provides a vocabulary for thinking about societal learning but not a diagnostic tool.

## 9. Multi-Level LVA Design Options

Given the research above, the LVA could extend to collective levels via three architectural options:

### Option A: 3×4 Matrix — Three Dimensions × Four Levels

| Level | Engagement | Capability | Portability |
|-------|------------|------------|-------------|
| **Individual** | Self-directed motivation; alignment with personal purpose | From rule-following (L3) to generative expertise (L7) | Ability to articulate and transfer learning |
| **Team** | Collective commitment; psychological safety; equitable participation | Shared mental models; collective problem-solving; emergent capability | Team can articulate what it knows collectively |
| **Organizational** | Shared vision; aligned incentives; learning culture | Systems thinking; double-loop learning; adaptive capacity | Knowledge management; codification; shared repertoire |
| **Societal** | Cultural narrative supporting learning; public support for education | Collective capacity to address complex challenges | Learning visible in policy and discourse |

**Pros:**
- Preserves MECE structure at each level
- Allows level-specific diagnostics with concrete interventions
- Honors the non-reducibility of levels (team capability ≠ aggregate individual capability)

**Cons:**
- 12 cells to define and validate empirically
- Risk of over-complication
- Unclear how levels interact (does individual-level intervention affect team-level outcome?)

### Option B: Add a Fourth "Collective" Dimension

Keep E-C-P but add a fourth dimension at individual level focused on social fabric:
- Individual Engagement, Capability, Portability, + Relationality
- Relationality = ability to collaborate, listen, give/receive feedback, navigate group dynamics

**Pros:**
- Simpler architecture than 3×4 matrix
- Captures social fabric as a distinct construct
- Easier to implement incrementally (add Relationality to existing individual diagnostics)

**Cons:**
- Breaks the E-C-P structure
- Relationality could be seen as overlapping with Engagement (belonging) or Capability (team skills)
- Doesn't directly address team/organizational/societal levels

### Option C: Keep Individual Diagnostic, Add Institutional Readiness Overlay

The LVA remains primarily an individual diagnostic (its current strength). Add a companion tool:
- **LVA Individual Diagnostic:** E (engagement), C (capability), P (portability) at individual level
- **Institutional Readiness Overlay:** Assesses organizational conditions that enable/prevent LVA implementation
  - Mental models about learning
  - Systems thinking capacity
  - Shared vision and alignment
  - Defensive routines (blockers)
  - Community of practice infrastructure

**Pros:**
- Doesn't modify the core LVA (preserves its strength and validation)
- Adds organizational context as a modifier/barrier
- Easier to implement incrementally
- Stronger empirical grounding (uses Senge, Argyris, Wenger, Woolley as validation framework)

**Cons:**
- Doesn't directly capture team-level emergent phenomena (e.g., c-factor)
- Requires developing and validating a companion tool
- Institutional readiness assessment is more complex than individual diagnosis

## 10. Recommendation: Hybrid Implementation Path

### Minimum Viable Extension (Immediate): Option C

Implement the institutional readiness overlay now:

1. **Diagnostic Layer:**
   - Keep existing LVA individual diagnostic (E-C-P)
   - Validate against individual learner outcomes

2. **Readiness Layer:**
   - Assess institutional mental models: "What does this organization believe about learning?"
   - Assess systems thinking: "Can this organization diagnose complex learning problems?"
   - Assess shared vision: "Are incentives aligned with learning goals?"
   - Assess defensive routines: "What topics are undiscussable? What barriers to change are invisible?"
   - Assess community infrastructure: "Are there communities of practice? Are they healthy?"

3. **Integration:**
   - Map individual diagnostic onto institutional readiness
   - Identify interventions that work at both levels
   - Prioritize interventions that leverage existing institutional strengths

### Research-Grounded Long-Term Target (2-3 years): Option A

Develop and validate a 3×4 matrix model:

1. **Team-Level Adaptation:**
   - Define and validate team-level E-C-P
   - Test whether team-level diagnostics predict team learning outcomes
   - Study communities of practice as team-level units

2. **Organizational-Level Adaptation:**
   - Map Senge's five disciplines onto org-level E-C-P
   - Develop diagnostic tools for organizational systems thinking, mental models, team learning
   - Test whether org-level interventions affect individual-level outcomes

3. **Research Agenda:**
   - How do levels interact? (Does strong team-level capability accelerate individual-level development?)
   - How do communities of practice function as intermediate units between individual and organizational?
   - What role does network structure play in individual learning trajectories?
   - Can shared mental models be diagnosed and developed systematically?

## 11. Evidence Grading

The following table summarizes the empirical strength of evidence for each research tradition:

| Source | Core Claim | Evidence Grade | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|--------|
| **Wenger (Communities of Practice)** | Learning is situated in social practice; CoP enables/scaffold development | **STRONG** | 25+ years of empirical work; extensive case studies; widely validated |
| **Woolley et al. (Collective Intelligence)** | Teams have measurable general intelligence (c-factor) independent of member IQ | **MODERATE** | Replication in multiple contexts; measurement challenges; social perceptiveness findings robust |
| **Senge (Five Disciplines)** | Organizations that practice systems thinking, shared vision, team learning outperform others | **MODERATE** | Widely influential; theoretical foundation is strong; empirical validation is weaker; self-selection bias in case studies |
| **Argyris (Double-Loop Learning)** | Defensive routines prevent double-loop learning and lock organizations in error patterns | **STRONG** | Extensive case study evidence across organizations; deep qualitative research; practical applicability validated |
| **Fisher (Collective Edge)** | Groups have emergent properties; leadership is condition-setting not commanding | **EMERGING** | Book published 2025; draws on extensive research base; specific "collective edge" framework is new; not yet independently validated |
| **Nonaka (SECI Model)** | Knowledge spirals through tacit→explicit modes and amplifies across levels | **MODERATE** | Influential in KM theory; criticized for cultural specificity (developed in Japan); measurement and operationalization challenging |
| **Kotter (8-Step Model)** | Linear change model with clear steps enables organizational transformation | **MODERATE** | Extensive application; evidence supports effectiveness; linearity criticized; needs adaptation for higher ed and emergent change |
| **Social Movement Theory** | Threshold models, contagion, framing explain societal behavior change | **MODERATE** | Strong theoretical foundation; limited direct application to learning/education; causation difficult to isolate at scale |

**Overall Assessment:**
- **Strong evidence** for CoP, Argyris, and Fisher's interaction-based claims
- **Moderate evidence** for Senge, SECI, Kotter, and social movement theory
- **Emerging evidence** for collective intelligence and its mechanisms
- **Weak evidence** for societal-level application of learning frameworks

## 12. Key Research Gaps and New Frontiers

The analysis above identifies five major research areas that should inform LVA extension:

### 1. Team-Level LVA Diagnostic
**Question:** Can the three dimensions meaningfully apply to teams?

**Specific investigations needed:**
- What does "team engagement" look like empirically? Is it measurable? Does it predict team learning outcomes?
- Can "team capability" be distinguished from aggregated individual capability? (Woolley suggests yes; we need classroom/workplace validation)
- What would "team portability" mean? Can teams articulate their collective knowledge? Can they transmit it to new members or other teams?

### 2. Defensive Routines as Barriers to Implementation
**Question:** How to diagnose and overcome institutional defensive routines?

**Specific investigations needed:**
- What defensive routines most commonly block learning interventions?
- How can institutions identify their own defensive routines (by definition, they are invisible to the organization)?
- What interventions shift from Model I (defensive) to Model II (productive) behavior?

### 3. Community as Intervention
**Question:** Is building a strong CoP more effective than individual-level interventions?

**Specific investigations needed:**
- Woolley's research suggests social structure matters more than individual capabilities. Does this hold in learning contexts?
- Can communities of practice be deliberately cultivated? What conditions enable their emergence?
- How do CoPs relate to LVA interventions? (Do they address all three dimensions simultaneously, making them higher-leverage?)

### 4. Network Effects on Individual Learning
**Question:** How does the social network structure of a learning community affect individual LVA trajectories?

**Specific investigations needed:**
- Do learners in dense networks progress differently than those with bridging ties?
- How does network position predict learning outcomes?
- Can network structure be modified to accelerate learning?

### 5. Shared Mental Models as Team Capability
**Question:** How do shared mental models relate to team-level capability?

**Specific investigations needed:**
- Can SMMs be diagnosed reliably? (Current methods are costly and time-intensive)
- Can SMMs be developed systematically? (Or do they emerge only through extended collaboration?)
- How do SMMs relate to team Portability? (Can teams externalize their shared mental models?)

**Key Findings Summary:**

1. **The LVA's Strength is also its Limitation:** Individual-level diagnostic precision comes at the cost of ignoring emergent collective phenomena. A team of individually capable members can fail due to coordination failures invisible to the individual diagnostic.

2. **Multiple Research Traditions Point to Collective Capability:** Fisher (organizational behavior), Woolley (collective intelligence), Wenger (CoP), Argyris (defensive routines), Senge (five disciplines) all independently conclude that organizational capability is not reducible to individual capability.

3. **Institutional Readiness is Critical:** The gap between LVA diagnosis and successful implementation is often institutional defensive routines and misaligned mental models, not learner capability.

4. **Minimum Viable Extension:** An institutional readiness overlay (Option C) can be implemented immediately using existing research. This preserves the LVA's core strength while adding organizational context.

5. **Long-Term Target:** A 3×4 matrix model (Option A) is the research-grounded direction, requiring 2-3 years of empirical validation.

6. **Strongest Evidence for Extension:** Communities of practice and collective intelligence research provide the strongest empirical grounding for multi-level extension.

7. **Weakest Evidence:** Societal-level application remains largely analogical; it provides vocabulary but not diagnostic capability.

## Appendix: Definitions for Extension Framework

**Team-Level Engagement:** Collective commitment to shared purpose, psychological safety, equitable voice, mutual respect, and group energy directed toward learning objectives.

**Team-Level Capability:** Shared mental models enabling coordination; collective problem-solving; ability to leverage diverse expertise; c-factor—team's general ability to perform well across diverse cognitive tasks.

**Team-Level Portability:** Team's ability to articulate what the group knows collectively; ability to transmit collective knowledge to new members; explicit shared repertoire maintained across time and membership changes.

**Organizational-Level Engagement:** Shared vision aligned with learning goals; incentive structures supporting learning; culture of continuous improvement; psychological safety at institutional level.

**Organizational-Level Capability:** Systems thinking; capacity for double-loop learning; ability to diagnose complex learning problems; adaptive capacity in response to change; institutional mental models aligned with learning science.

**Organizational-Level Portability:** Knowledge management systems; codified practices and shared repertoire; accessible documentation; mechanisms for cross-functional learning; integration of learning into institutional routines.

**Institutional Readiness:** Capacity of organization to diagnose learning problems (systems thinking), openness to examining underlying assumptions (mental models), alignment of incentives with learning goals (shared vision), capacity to overcome defensive routines (productive dialogue), and infrastructure for team learning (communities of practice, shared spaces).
