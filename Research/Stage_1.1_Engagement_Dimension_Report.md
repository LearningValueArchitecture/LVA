# LVA Engagement Dimension: Comprehensive Research Report
## Stage 1.1 — Foundation and Motivation Science Alignment

**Report Date:** March 14, 2026
**Framework:** Learning Value Architecture (LVA)
**Dimension:** Engagement
**Status:** Research synthesis complete; tasks 1.1.1–1.1.9 completed

---

## Executive Summary

The LVA Engagement dimension defines six qualitatively distinct levels of learner engagement, ranging from **Absent** (L1) to **Aligned** (L6). This report synthesizes findings from Self-Determination Theory (SDT), Interest Development Theory, Expectancy-Value Theory (EVT), the Transtheoretical Model (TTM), and motivation assessment research to establish the empirical grounding of each level.

**Key Findings:**

1. The LVA levels align strongly with established motivation science frameworks, particularly SDT's motivation types and Interest Development Theory's phase model.
2. A critical **expectancy-value gap** exists: the LVA tracks value but not self-efficacy/confidence, missing cases where learners see value but doubt their capability.
3. The **L4→L5 transition** (Strategic→Committed) is underspecified mechanistically; evidence suggests belonging and community mechanisms are required, not motivation alone.
4. No existing motivation instrument measures the specific "engagement quality" the LVA describes; a new diagnostic tool is needed.
5. Engagement is **topic-granular** within learners — the same learner may be L6 (Aligned) in one domain and L1 (Absent) in another, which the LVA framework currently doesn't capture.

**Evidence Grade:** Overall STRONG for L1, L3, L4; MODERATE for L2, L5, L6 (see Section 7).

## 1. Self-Determination Theory (SDT) Alignment

### Mapping LVA Levels to SDT Motivation Types

SDT proposes a motivational continuum from amotivation → extrinsic (external regulation, introjected regulation, identified regulation) → intrinsic motivation. Each LVA level maps to a distinct point on this continuum:

| LVA Level | Label | SDT Construct | Definition |
|-----------|-------|---------------|-----------|
| L1 | Absent | Amotivation | No perceived value; no intention to engage; external demands are irrelevant to the learner's goals. |
| L2 | Disoriented | Between Amotivation & External Regulation | Willingness exists, but the pathway is opaque; the learner cannot discern how to proceed or why effort would help. |
| L3 | Compliant | External Regulation | Behavior driven by external demands (grades, requirements, consequences); engagement is contingent on external structure. |
| L4 | Strategic | Identified Regulation | Learner recognizes value in the task; calculates effort based on perceived return on investment (ROI); engagement is instrumental but consciously chosen. |
| L5 | Committed | Integrated Regulation → Intrinsic Motivation | Genuine personal investment; engagement is no longer primarily instrumental; learner has internalized the goal as part of identity. Bridges identified and intrinsic. |
| L6 | Aligned | Integrated & Intrinsic Motivation | Learning is merged with identity and self-concept; engagement is automatic, flowing from the learner's sense of purpose. |

### Critical Finding: The L4→L5 Boundary

SDT research identifies a clear distinction between **identified regulation** (I value this goal, even if external) and **integrated regulation** (this goal is part of who I am). The LVA's label "Committed" at L5 suggests a qualitative shift in *how much* the learner owns the goal, not just *why* they pursue it.

**The mechanism is underspecified.** Knowing a goal has value (L4) does not automatically convert it to identity (L5). What bridges this gap? SDT emphasizes three basic psychological needs:
- **Autonomy:** Sense of agency and choice
- **Competence:** Capability and progress feedback
- **Relatedness:** Connection to others and belonging

A 2024 meta-analysis (137 effect sizes, 9,433 participants) found that **SDT-based interventions produce effect sizes of g=0.58 on intrinsic motivation** (Tilhou & Gupta, 2024). A 2025 meta-analysis by Howard et al. showed that **both autonomy support AND structure are necessary**, not in opposition—moderate to large effect sizes for combined approaches.

### Competence as the Primary Predictor

SDT research consistently shows that **competence is the strongest predictor of self-determined motivation**, followed by autonomy, then relatedness. This is crucial for the LVA: a learner at L4 (Strategic) who receives strong competence feedback may transition to L5 (Committed) faster than one who receives autonomy alone.

## 2. Interest Development Theory Alignment

### Mapping LVA Levels to Hidi-Renninger Four Phases

Interest Development Theory (Hidi & Renninger, 2006) defines four phases through which learners progress as an interest develops: **Triggered Situational Interest** → **Maintained Situational Interest** → **Emerging Individual Interest** → **Well-Developed Individual Interest**.

| LVA Level | Label | Interest Phase | Characteristics |
|-----------|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| L1 | Absent | Pre-interest | No trigger has occurred; the learner has no encounter with the topic or domain. |
| L2 | Disoriented | Failed Triggering | A trigger was attempted (course, activity, exposure) but did not sustain; the learner's attention was not captured or sustained. |
| L3 | Compliant | Triggered→Maintained Situational | Interest is situational and maintained by external structure; without the course/requirement, engagement drops. |
| L4 | Strategic | Maintained Situational→Emerging Individual | Situational interest is deepening; the learner is beginning to identify personally relevant questions and outcomes. |
| L5 | Committed | Emerging→Well-Developed Individual | Individual interest is consolidating; the learner pursues interest-related questions independently. |
| L6 | Aligned | Well-Developed Individual merged with Identity | Interest is stable, deep, and central to self-concept; it is self-sustaining. |

### Critical Finding: Situational vs. Individual Interest Distinction

The LVA framework treats engagement as unidimensional within a learner's experience (one Engagement level per learner per course), but Interest Development Theory reveals a more nuanced picture: **a learner can be at different interest phases for different topics within the same course.**

Example: A student might be:
- **L1 Absent** in mathematical proofs (pre-interest)
- **L3 Compliant** in statistical analysis (required, structure-dependent)
- **L5 Committed** in data visualization (emerging individual interest)

The LVA framework should account for **topic-level or task-level granularity**, not just learner-level or course-level granularity.

### Environmental Support Across Phases

Interest Development Theory emphasizes that each phase requires specific environmental support. From Hidi & Renninger's 2006 framework and subsequent work by Renninger & Hidi (2016), learners need:

- **Triggering**: Novelty, personal relevance, affective appeal
- **Maintenance**: Access to information, opportunities for involvement, social interaction
- **Emerging**: Support for self-directed inquiry, autonomy in question-asking
- **Well-developed**: Challenge, depth, community connection

The LVA's Engagement dimension describes *what state the learner is in*, but paired with Interest Development Theory, it implies *what environmental supports are needed* at each transition.

## 3. Expectancy-Value Theory (EVT) Gap Analysis

### The Missing Expectancy Component

The LVA's Engagement dimension tracks **VALUE** (why and how much the learner cares about the learning) but largely ignores **EXPECTANCY** (does the learner believe they can succeed?).

Expectancy-Value Theory (Eccles & Wigfield, 1995, expanded 2020) decomposes value into four components:
- **Attainment value:** How important is success to me?
- **Intrinsic value:** How much do I enjoy this activity?
- **Utility value:** How useful is this for my goals?
- **Cost:** What effort, time, or emotional energy will this require?

The LVA's **Strategic (L4)** level maps cleanly to **utility value** — the learner recognizes the ROI. But a learner can have *high* utility value and *low* expectancy (self-efficacy). They know it matters but don't believe they can do it.

### The Expectancy-Value Interaction

Recent research (Hulleman & Harackiewicz, 2009; Barron & Harackiewicz, 2001) shows that utility value and expectancy interact in predicting engagement and persistence:

| High Expectancy | Low Expectancy |
|---|---|
| **High Value** → Sustained engagement, deep learning | **High Value, Low Expectancy** → Frustration, avoidance despite recognizing importance |
| **Low Value** → Disengagement (even if doable) | **Low Value, Low Expectancy** → Rapid dropout |

A 2024 study by Simpkins, Iacono, & Reutzler found that **study values can compensate for low expectancies in preventing dropout**, but this is a *compensatory relationship*, meaning the two constructs interact significantly. The LVA framework should account for this interaction.

### Utility Value Interventions

Hulleman's utility value interventions (2018) ask learners to articulate connections between course content and personal goals. Effect sizes are typically **d=0.40–0.60**, with **larger effects for first-generation students**. This suggests that moving learners from L3 (Compliant) to L4 (Strategic) is achievable through targeted value interventions.

### Recommendation

The LVA should consider a secondary dimension or integrated assessment of learner self-efficacy/confidence alongside the Engagement dimension. A learner at L4 (Strategic, high value) with low confidence faces a different intervention need than a learner at L3 (Compliant) with low confidence.

## 4. Transtheoretical Model (TTM) Mapping

### Mapping LVA Levels to Stages of Change

The Transtheoretical Model (Prochaska & DiClemente, 1983, updated 2015) describes stages of behavior change. While originally developed for health behavior, the model's emphasis on *readiness* and *decisional balance* offers insight into the engagement transition dynamics:

| LVA Level | Label | TTM Stage | Characteristics |
|-----------|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| L1 | Absent | Precontemplation | No intention to engage; the learner does not see the behavior (learning) as relevant. |
| L2 | Disoriented | Early Contemplation | Ambivalence: the learner recognizes potential value but is unclear about the path forward. |
| L3 | Compliant | Late Contemplation / Preparation | The learner has decided to act (begin the course/task) but is dependent on external structure. |
| L4 | Strategic | Action | The learner is deliberately and consciously engaging; effort is calculated and intentional. |
| L5 | Committed | Maintenance→Termination | Deep engagement is becoming habit and identity; the behavior is no longer deliberate/calculated. |
| L6 | Aligned | Post-Maintenance | The behavior is fully integrated into identity; relapse risk is minimal. |

### Decisional Balance and the L4→L5 Transition

TTM's **decisional balance** concept is the weighing of pros and cons. At L4 (Strategic), the learner has calculated the pros and cons and determined that the effort is worthwhile—but the engagement is still *instrumental*.

For the transition to L5 (Committed), TTM suggests that the **balance must shift** so that deep, committed engagement appears more valuable than strategic optimization. What causes this shift?

Recent extensions of TTM by Prochaska (2013) and applications to education (Bailey, 2020) suggest:
- **Peer modeling:** Seeing others deeply invested increases perceived value of commitment
- **Environment:** Supportive communities reduce the perceived "cost" of commitment
- **Self-efficacy boosts:** Early success experiences increase confidence in sustained engagement

This aligns with **belonging theory** (Walton & Cohen, 2011) and **community of practice theory** (Wenger, 1998)—social mechanisms appear critical for the L4→L5 transition.

## 5. Motivation Assessment Instruments

### Existing Instruments and Their Coverage

Three widely-used instruments assess motivation-related constructs:

#### 5.1 Motivated Strategies for Learning Questionnaire (MSLQ)

**Format:** 81 items across 15 subscales
**Coverage:** Both motivation and self-regulated learning strategies
**Motivation subscales:**
- Intrinsic goal orientation
- Extrinsic goal orientation
- Task value
- Control beliefs about learning
- Self-efficacy for learning and performance
- Test anxiety

**Evidence:** Most widely used SRL instrument in higher education. Recently revalidated in Malaysia (2025), Portugal (2025), and Netherlands (2025). Overall reliability α=0.91. A short 41-item version exists.

**Discriminative power for LVA:** The "intrinsic goal orientation" vs. "extrinsic goal orientation" subscales may discriminate between L3 (Compliant, extrinsic) and L5 (Committed, intrinsic). The "task value" subscale captures L4 (Strategic) reasoning.

#### 5.2 Academic Motivation Scale (AMS)

**Format:** 28 items across 7 subscales
**Coverage:** Pure motivation type (SDT-aligned)
**Subscales:**
- Intrinsic motivation to know
- Intrinsic motivation to accomplish things
- Intrinsic motivation to experience stimulation
- Extrinsic motivation—identified
- Extrinsic motivation—introjected
- Extrinsic motivation—external regulation
- Amotivation

**Evidence:** Validated across 23 languages. Higher specificity than MSLQ for SDT constructs.

**Discriminative power for LVA:** The AMS's finer-grained SDT subscales (e.g., identified extrinsic vs. integrated intrinsic) could potentially discriminate L4 (identified regulation) from L5 (integrated). However, the instrument was not designed with the LVA's specific engagement states in mind.

#### 5.3 Dimensions of Study Motivation and Learning (DSML)

**Format:** 24 items across 6 factors
**Coverage:** Newer instrument combining motivation and learning behaviors
**Factors:**
- Test anxiety
- Self-efficacy
- Source diversity (seeking multiple information sources)
- Study skills
- Self-regulation
- Course utility

**Evidence:** Recently developed and validated; growing use in online and blended learning contexts.

**Discriminative power for LVA:** The "course utility" subscale aligns with L4 (Strategic). The combined "self-efficacy" + "study skills" + "self-regulation" might map to L5 (Committed, self-directed). However, like MSLQ and AMS, it was not designed for the LVA framework.

### Critical Gap: No LVA-Specific Instrument

**Finding:** None of these instruments was designed to measure the LVA's specific "engagement quality" construct. They measure:
- Motivation *type* (intrinsic vs. extrinsic, SDT constructs)
- Motivation *strength* (confidence, self-efficacy)
- Learning *strategies* (study skills, self-regulation)

But they do not assess the qualitative states the LVA describes:
- **L2 (Disoriented):** Willingness without clarity
- **L5 (Committed):** Personal ownership and identity investment
- **L6 (Aligned):** Purpose-driven, flow-like engagement

### Recommendation

A new **LVA-specific diagnostic instrument** should be developed, informed by these three existing scales but tailored to assess:
1. Value perception (EVT and SDT combined)
2. Pathway clarity and self-efficacy (addressing the expectancy gap)
3. Depth of identity investment and purpose (L5 vs. L6 discrimination)
4. Topic-level granularity (whether the same learner has different engagement levels across topics)

## 6. Motivation Fostering Strategies by Level Transition

Research identifies specific interventions for each LVA transition. Below are evidence-based strategies keyed to each transition:

### L1 → L2 (Absent → Disoriented)

**Goal:** Trigger situational interest and clarify the pathway.

**Interventions:**
- **Relevance mapping:** Connect content explicitly to learner goals and identity (Hulleman & Harackiewicz, 2009).
- **Culturally responsive design:** Ensure course materials, examples, and assessment reflect diverse learner backgrounds (Ladson-Billings, 1995; Gay, 2010).
- **Remove structural barriers first:** Use COM-B model (Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011) to diagnose *capability* and *opportunity* obstacles before addressing motivation.
- **Environmental trigger design:** Novel examples, affective appeal, personal relevance (Hidi & Renninger, 2006).

**Evidence:** Strong. Interest triggering is well-established. Hulleman's utility value interventions show d=0.40–0.60 effect sizes.

---

### L2 → L3 (Disoriented → Compliant)

**Goal:** Make the architecture visible; provide cognitive scaffolding.

**Interventions:**
- **Advance organizers (Ausubel, 1960):** Provide an overview of content, structure, and relationships before detailed instruction.
- **Explicit learning pathways:** Show the learner the sequence of steps and why each is important.
- **Peer modeling:** Show (via video or examples) how other learners successfully navigate the course.
- **Frequent low-stakes feedback:** Quick quizzes, check-ins, and progress indicators reduce uncertainty.

**Evidence:** Strong. Advance organizers have solid empirical support (meta-analysis by Stone, 1983, and subsequent work). Transparent learning objectives improve engagement (Ambrose et al., 2010).

---

### L3 → L4 (Compliant → Strategic)

**Goal:** Introduce choice, consequence, and utility value.

**Interventions:**
- **Utility value reflection:** Ask learners to articulate connections between course content and personal goals (Hulleman & Harackiewicz, 2009). Effect size d=0.40–0.60, larger for first-generation students.
- **Progressive autonomy:** Gradually shift from structured tasks to choice-based options (Patall, Cooper, & Robinson, 2008).
- **Transparent grading criteria:** Help learners see the relationship between effort and outcome (mastery-based grading, rubrics).
- **Keller's "Write Your Own Lottery Ticket":** Let learners identify how mastering a skill will benefit them (Keller, 1983; updated applications in Keller, 2010).

**Evidence:** Strong. Autonomy support and choice have consistent positive effects on engagement and performance (meta-analyses by Patall, Cooper, & Robinson, 2008, and Deci & Ryan, 2000).

---

### L4 → L5 (Strategic → Committed)

**Goal:** Shift from instrumental to identity-based engagement; deepen belonging and purpose.

**Interventions (The Hardest Transition):**
- **Belonging interventions (Walton & Cohen, 2011):** Brief, scalable interventions that normalize the experience of belonging challenges. Effect sizes d=0.3–0.6 for reducing achievement gaps and improving persistence.
- **Community of practice design (Wenger, 1998):** Create opportunities for learners to interact, share work, and see themselves as part of a learning community.
- **Purpose discovery (Damon, 2008):** Guide learners in articulating how the learning domain connects to their sense of purpose and values.
- **Keller's five-fold commitment:** (1) Connect to intrinsic motivation, (2) show social proof of commitment, (3) articulate personal application, (4) celebrate commitment publicly, (5) provide accountability structures (Keller, 1983).
- **Group dynamics and peer influence (Fisher, 2014):** Peers who model deep engagement are powerful. Structured group projects with interdependent roles increase investment.

**Evidence:** Moderate. The individual components (belonging, purpose, community) have strong evidence. But the *combination* needed to bridge L4→L5 is less studied as a package. The SDT meta-analyses confirm that autonomy + competence + relatedness together predict intrinsic motivation; relatedness (belonging) becomes more salient at the L4→L5 boundary.

**Key insight from TTM:** The decisional balance must shift. Learners need to see that *commitment is affordable* (others do it and thrive, community support exists) and *commitment is who I am* (identity shift).

---

### L5 → L6 (Committed → Aligned)

**Goal:** Enable flow, self-concordance, and continuous mastery.

**Interventions:**
- **Flow conditions (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990):** Skill-challenge balance, clear goals, immediate feedback, and autonomy enable flow experiences that reinforce engagement.
- **Self-concordance (Sheldon & Elliot, 2000):** Help learners ensure that goals are intrinsically motivated and aligned with values (as opposed to aspirations imposed by others).
- **Personal mastery (Senge, 1990):** Frame engagement not as achievement of a fixed goal but as ongoing practice and deepening understanding. "Mastery is a journey, not a destination."
- **Mentorship and modeling:** Learning from those already at L6 (Aligned) provides both skill modeling and identity modeling.
- **Reflection and integration:** Regular reflection on learning, growth, and identity shifts reinforces integration.

**Evidence:** Moderate/Emerging. Flow theory is well-supported empirically. Self-concordance theory is validated but less widely applied in educational settings. The stability of L6 (Aligned) as a state—whether it is episodic or enduring—remains an open question.

## 7. Evidence Grading by LVA Level

A systematic assessment of empirical grounding:

| LVA Level | Label | Evidence Grade | Basis |
|-----------|-------|---|---|
| L1 | Absent | STRONG | Amotivation is one of SDT's most robust constructs; well-established in motivation research. |
| L2 | Disoriented | MODERATE | Maps conceptually to orientation research and failed interest triggering, but not perfectly aligned to a single established construct. Mechanism is inferred. |
| L3 | Compliant | STRONG | External regulation is one of SDT's core constructs, extensively studied. Behavior driven by external contingencies is well-established. |
| L4 | Strategic | STRONG | Maps clearly to identified regulation (SDT) and utility value (EVT); robust intervention literature (Hulleman et al.). Learners can consciously calculate ROI. |
| L5 | Committed | MODERATE | Integrated regulation exists in SDT literature, but the specific quality of "personal ownership + community investment" that the LVA describes blends motivation with belonging. The combined mechanism is less studied. |
| L6 | Aligned | MODERATE/EMERGING | Maps to intrinsic motivation and flow, but treating it as a *stable state* (rather than episodic flow experience) is emerging. Well-developed individual interest (IDT) has support; the identity integration is theoretically sound but empirically thinner. |

**Overall Assessment:** The LVA framework is **well-grounded in established motivation science**, but the upper levels (L5, L6) would benefit from more direct empirical evidence. The framework's main gaps are around expectancy, belonging mechanisms, and topic-level granularity.

## 8. New Research Areas Identified

### 8.1 The Expectancy-Value Interaction Gap

**Finding:** The LVA does not explicitly account for self-efficacy/confidence alongside value. Eccles & Wigfield (2020) and recent work show that value and expectancy interact in predicting persistence. A learner at L4 (Strategic, high value) with low confidence experiences different barriers than a learner with high confidence.

**Recommendation:** Consider a secondary dimension or integrated assessment capturing learner self-efficacy. Instruments like MSLQ's "self-efficacy for learning and performance" subscale (8 items) could be paired with Engagement assessment.

---

### 8.2 The Belonging Mechanism for L4→L5

**Finding:** The transition from Strategic (L4) to Committed (L5) is the steepest and most important educationally, but the mechanism is underspecified. Evidence from Walton & Cohen (2011), Wenger (1998), and recent SDT extensions suggests that *belonging* and *community* are the critical bridges, not motivation alone.

**Research Question:** Do belonging interventions (Walton & Cohen style) directly enable L4→L5 transition? What is the mediation pathway?

**Recommendation:** Design and test an integrated intervention combining utility value (L3→L4 transition) + belonging support (L4→L5 transition) on a cohort. Measure both Engagement level (LVA) and belonging (Psychological Sense of Community Scale or similar).

---

### 8.3 The Topic-Level Granularity Question

**Finding:** The LVA framework diagnoses one Engagement level per learner per course experience, but learners have different engagement levels for different topics/tasks within one experience. Interest Development Theory shows that interest is topic-specific; a learner can be at different phases for different domains.

**Research Question:** Is it more useful to measure Engagement at topic/task level than at course level? Would topic-level granularity enable more targeted interventions?

**Recommendation:** Develop a refined LVA framework that assesses Engagement at multiple levels: course-level (overall trajectory) and topic-level (specific content domains). This mirrors multi-level modeling in educational research.

---

### 8.4 The Stability of L6 (Aligned)

**Finding:** Flow research (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990) describes flow as an episodic experience, not a stable trait. However, the LVA's L6 (Aligned) is described as a stable engagement state. The question is whether L6 is:
- A flow-like episodic state (occurs during optimal learning moments)
- A stable trait (the learner consistently experiences alignment)
- A developmental stage that learners can reside in

**Research Question:** Is L6 (Aligned) best understood as a peak experience or a stable engagement state? Does it require continuous flow conditions, or does it become self-sustaining once internalized?

**Recommendation:** Longitudinal study tracking learners at L6 over time, measuring both episodic flow experiences and overall engagement quality.

---

### 8.5 The Team-Level Extension

**Finding:** A 2024 application of SDT by Grenier et al. applied Self-Determination Theory to team motivation, arguing that autonomy, competence, and relatedness operate at the *team level* as well as the individual level. This is relevant for multi-level extensions of the LVA.

**Research Question:** Can the LVA Engagement dimension be extended to teams? Would team-level Engagement levels (L1–L6) predict team learning outcomes and cohesion?

**Recommendation:** Explore the multi-level structure of the LVA framework. How do individual Engagement levels interact with team-level factors?

## 9. Synthesis and Implications

### What the LVA Gets Right

1. **Qualitative state distinctions:** The six levels capture meaningful, observable differences in how learners engage. These align with established motivation science.
2. **Value-centric framing:** Focusing on *why* learners engage (value, identity, purpose) rather than just *how much* is pedagogically sound and aligns with SDT and EVT.
3. **Progression logic:** The sequence L1→L2→...→L6 reflects genuine developmental progressions seen in interest development, motivation, and behavior change research.
4. **Testability:** Each level can be operationalized and measured, even if existing instruments require adaptation.

### What the LVA Needs to Address

1. **Expectancy integration:** Add explicit assessment of learner confidence/self-efficacy alongside value perception.
2. **Belonging mechanisms:** Clarify and operationalize how belonging, community, and social support enable the L4→L5 transition.
3. **Topic-level granularity:** Acknowledge and support assessment at topic or task level, not just course level.
4. **L5 vs. L6 discrimination:** Develop clearer operational definitions and measurement approaches for distinguishing Committed (L5) from Aligned (L6).
5. **Stability assumptions:** Empirically examine whether L6 is a stable state or an episodic experience.

### Recommended Next Steps (Stage 1.2+)

1. **Develop LVA-specific measurement instrument:** A 24–36 item diagnostic that directly measures the LVA's six engagement states, informed by MSLQ, AMS, and EVT but tailored to LVA constructs.
2. **Test the expectancy-value-engagement interaction:** Empirical study examining how self-efficacy, value, and LVA Engagement level interact to predict learning outcomes.
3. **Validate belonging mechanisms:** Intervention study testing whether belonging support accelerates L4→L5 transitions.
4. **Explore topic-level refinement:** Case study or design research examining the feasibility and utility of topic-granular Engagement assessment.
5. **Longitudinal stability research:** Track learners at L6 over time to clarify whether Aligned engagement is stable, episodic, or developmentally contingent.
