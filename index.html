The Framework

The Learning Value Architecture

Every learning investment must satisfy four conditions: it must engage, it must build real capability, it must be portable and visible, and the environment must support its application.

Dimension 01

◆

Engagement

Does the learner care — and if so, how and why? The system creates conditions where participation is meaningful, not mandated.

Low completion, passive attendance Compliance rather than capability

Dimension 02

◆

Capability

Was real ability actually built — and is it durable? Learning translates into behavior change visible on the job.

Confident graduates who cannot perform Skills that decay within weeks

Dimension 03

◆

Portability

Does the learner know they have it, and can the world see it? Capability is recognized, credentialed, and transferable.

Real skills with no visibility Credentials that don’t travel

Dimension 04

◆

Opportunity

Does the environment support or block learning application? The organization, culture, and systems enable people to use what they’ve learned. When missing: trained people returning to unchanged systems, capability wasted by structural barriers.

Why This Is Different

The structural gap in every market

What leaders experience as learning problems are usually structural problems. The same patterns appear everywhere — in universities, corporations, and EdTech companies.

Strategy and delivery pulled apart

Consultants write strategies. Internal teams try to execute them. The gap between strategy documents and operational reality is where learning value disappears.

Evidence as design afterthought

Evidence of impact is requested after programs launch, rather than designed into the system from the start. This produces retroactive justification rather than genuine insight.

Cross-industry transfer

Higher education, workforce learning, and EdTech face structurally similar challenges but rarely learn from each other. We bring product thinking to institutions and academic rigor to enterprises.

Systems-level execution

Most interventions target individual programs or courses. But engagement, capability, portability, and opportunity are system-level properties that require system-level design.

  • Former roles at IKEA, Minerva University, Zayed University

I’ve spent my career at the intersection of learning science, institutional design, and product strategy. The Learning Value Architecture grew out of a recurring observation: organizations invest heavily in learning but have no reliable way to know whether it’s working.

The problem isn’t bad content or disengaged learners. It’s that most learning systems are designed to deliver content, not to produce and evidence capability. The LVA provides a diagnostic framework that makes the invisible visible — where engagement breaks down, where capability fails to transfer, where evidence doesn’t exist.

I work with boards, university presidents, CHROs, L&D leaders, and EdTech founders who want their learning investment to produce demonstrable results. The approach is always the same: diagnose the system, design the architecture, stay through execution.
